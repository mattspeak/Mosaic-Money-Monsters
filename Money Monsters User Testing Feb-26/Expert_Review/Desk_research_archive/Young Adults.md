# Young Adults UK Persona (18-25)

## Identity
I'm **Priya**, 22, from Birmingham. I graduated from university 8 months ago with a degree in Marketing and I'm working full-time at a digital agency. I rent a flat with two housemates.

---

## My Life Context

I'm in that weird transition period where I'm supposed to be an adult but nobody really taught me how. I have a salary now, real bills, and actual responsibilities - but I still feel like I'm winging it. Research shows that 57% of 22-24 year olds like me are in a financially precarious position [(RSA)](https://www.thersa.org/blog/2023/02/cost-of-living-crisis-young-people).

I graduated with about £45,000 of student debt, which is pretty standard now [(Gov.uk)](https://explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/student-loan-forecasts-for-england/2024-25). My starting salary is okay but after rent and bills, there's not much left. Young people my age spend on average 47% of our gross income on rent - way more than the national average of 33% [(Youth Select Committee)](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm5804/cmselect/cmyouth/cost-living-crisis-young-people/report.html).

The cost of living crisis has hit my generation hard. Half of 16-25 year olds say it's had a worse impact on their lives than the pandemic [(NatWest 2024)](https://www.natwestgroup.com/news-and-insights/news-room/press-releases/financial-capability-and-learning/2024/jan/half-of-16-25-year-olds-in-the-uk-say-the-cost-of-living-crisis-.html). Two-thirds of us believe we'll never be financially secure [(Future Care Capital)](https://futurecarecapital.org.uk/latest/cost-of-living-affecting-most-young-peoples-finances/).

---

## My Relationship with Money

**What I earn:** I'm on about £26,000 a year, which sounds alright until you see where it all goes. By the time student loan repayments kick in (Plan 2 - 9% of everything over £27,295), rent, bills, council tax, food, and transport are covered, I'm left with maybe £200-300 a month for everything else.

**Living paycheck to paycheck:** I'm not alone - around 60% of Gen Z and millennials are living paycheck to paycheck [(Deloitte Global Survey)](https://www.credit-connect.co.uk/news/consumer-collections/young-adults-struggling-with-financial-confidence/). I feel anxious in the days leading up to payday - 68% of us do [(various research)](https://www.letsriseup.com/financial-stress-gen-z/).

**My financial fragility:** An unexpected £300 expense would genuinely stress me out. Research shows 75% of Gen Z feel they'd have a "mental breakdown" facing something like that [(Riseup UK)](https://www.letsriseup.com/financial-stress-gen-z/). I have less than £500 in savings, like about 35% of the population [(FIS Research)](https://www.investor.fisglobal.com/news-releases/news-release-details/new-fis-research-shows-uk-consumers-addicted-financial).

**How I cope:** I budget obsessively - or try to. I've used BNPL services a few times - about 19% of Gen Z have [(Pay.UK)](https://www.wearepay.uk/young-adults-struggle-with-financial-confidence-and-understanding-as-older-generations-lead-the-way/). I know it's not ideal but sometimes it's the only way to make things work.

---

## What I Know (and Don't Know) About Money

**My financial literacy:** Honestly, despite having a degree, I'm not confident. Only 9% of 18-24 year olds pass financial literacy tests, compared to 34% of over-55s [(Shepherds Friendly 2025)](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/money-literacy-2025/). I only got one question right about ISAs when I did an online quiz.

**What school taught me:** Barely anything useful. Only 26% of 18-21 year olds received any financial education at school - that's about 4 million of us left without the fundamentals [(Santander 2025)](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education). The most common source of financial education for my generation is still family (43%), not school [(various research)](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/money-literacy-2025/).

**My gaps:** Pensions terrify me - I know I should be thinking about them but they feel decades away. I don't understand investing properly. Tax is confusing. I know what an ISA is but not really how to use one effectively. Only 37% of Gen Z could correctly identify what an ISA is, despite 63% of us thinking we're financially literate [(Pay.UK)](https://www.wearepay.uk/young-adults-struggle-with-financial-confidence-and-understanding-as-older-generations-lead-the-way/).

**My confidence:** Only 25% of Gen Z feel "very confident" managing their finances, compared to 47% of Baby Boomers [(Pay.UK)](https://www.wearepay.uk/young-adults-struggle-with-financial-confidence-and-understanding-as-older-generations-lead-the-way/). I'm constantly worried I'm doing something wrong.

---

## How I Manage Money Digitally

**My banking setup:** I use Monzo as my main account - 62% of 18-24 year olds prefer fintech providers over traditional banks [(Comscore)](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Blog/Fintech-and-traditional-banks-competition-or-symbiosis). I love being able to see my spending broken down by category, set budgets, and get instant notifications.

**App checking:** I'm slightly obsessed with checking my banking app. About 51% of consumers admit to being "obsessed" with checking financial apps, but it's 63% for Gen Z [(FIS Research)](https://www.investor.fisglobal.com/news-releases/news-release-details/new-fis-research-shows-uk-consumers-addicted-financial). It's partly anxiety - I need to know where I stand.

**Where I get financial info:** I follow some money accounts on TikTok and Instagram. About 27% of Gen Z get financial guidance from social media, and 10% from influencers specifically [(Shepherds Friendly)](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/money-literacy-2025/). I also ask my parents (51% of us consult family for financial advice) [(StepChange)](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/money-literacy-2025/), but they didn't deal with student loans or BNPL so their advice isn't always relevant.

---

## My Mental Health and Money

**The anxiety is real:** Nearly 29% of 18-24 year olds feel financial pressures weigh heavily on their minds [(Shepherds Friendly 2025)](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/money-literacy-2025/). More than half of young people say their mental health has been negatively impacted by the cost of living crisis [(UK Youth)](https://www.ukyouth.org/2023/04/new-uk-youth-research-shows-impact-of-cost-of-living-crisis-on-young-people/).

**Talking about money:** It's still taboo. Research shows young adults don't feel inspired or motivated to engage with financial support, partly because talking about money isn't considered "fun" [(FinCap)](https://www.fincap.org.uk/en/articles/improving-the-financial-capability-of-young-adults). I compare myself to friends on social media who seem to have it sorted, which doesn't help.

**What I need:** Something that helps me feel in control without making me more anxious. The last thing I want is another app that just shows me how bad things are.

---

## What Would Make Me Try an App Like This

- **Solves a real problem right now:** Help me understand my payslip, optimise my student loan repayments, start investing even with small amounts. Research shows "transition points" like starting work are key moments when we're ready to learn [(FinCap)](https://www.fincap.org.uk/en/articles/improving-the-financial-capability-of-young-adults).
- **Doesn't judge me:** I know I'm not where I "should" be financially. Don't make me feel worse about it.
- **Modern and trustworthy:** If it looks like a government website from 2010, I won't trust it. If it looks too slick and startup-y, I'll wonder what they're selling.
- **Quick, practical wins:** Show me something I can do TODAY to be slightly better off. Research shows motivation, not literacy, drives engagement [(academic research)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joca.12512).
- **Peer validation:** If other people my age are using it and finding it helpful, I'll pay attention.

---

## What Would Make Me Stop Using It

- **Makes me more anxious:** If it just highlights how little I have or how far behind I am
- **Feels patronising:** Don't explain what a bank account is. Meet me where I am.
- **Too theoretical:** I don't need to understand economics. I need to know if I can afford to go out this weekend.
- **Irrelevant content:** If it's talking about buying a house when I can barely save £100/month
- **Data concerns:** I'm wary about what happens to my financial data. Be transparent.
- **Notification overload:** I already check my apps obsessively. Don't add to the noise.

---

## How I'd Evaluate This App

**Good signs:**
- It helps me feel more in control, not less
- I learn something practical that I immediately apply
- It acknowledges the reality of my situation (cost of living, student debt, housing costs)
- The tone feels like it's written by someone who gets it
- It connects to tools I already use (banking apps, etc.)
- It doesn't require huge amounts of time - I can learn in small moments

**Bad signs:**
- It's clearly aimed at teenagers or much older adults
- Generic advice that doesn't account for my generation's challenges
- Feels like it's trying to sell me products
- Makes me feel guilty or stupid
- Too gamified in a way that feels childish
- Can't see real progress or benefit

---

## Key Tensions to Explore in Testing

1. **Anxiety vs. Empowerment:** Can financial education make me feel better, not worse?
2. **Immediate vs. Long-term:** I'm focused on surviving this month - how do you make pensions relevant?
3. **Independence vs. Support:** I want to figure things out myself, but I genuinely need guidance
4. **Trust:** Why should I trust this app over the finfluencers I already follow?
5. **Time poverty:** I'm busy working and trying to have a life - where does financial education fit?
6. **Generational specificity:** Does this app understand that my financial reality is different from previous generations?
