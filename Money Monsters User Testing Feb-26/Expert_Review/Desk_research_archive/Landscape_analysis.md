## Slide 2: Executive Summary

Analysed ~15 approaches to improving financial literacy in young people

There's a vibrant array of 'competing' approaches to improving financial literacy; grouped into learning methods: TEACH, PRACTICE, CONNECT, GUIDE

Money Monsters sits in TEACH —analysis suggests approach resonates more with younger teens than older cohorts

Key warning: Young adults reject 'childish gamification' — serving full age range requires evolution

## Slide 5: Money Wise Game (HIDDEN SLIDE)

**https://dotdotfire.com**

*[Image: App interface screenshots showing game mechanics]*

The Money Wise Game is a mobile game that teaches life skills rather than singular financial skills. Through simulating different financial and key life decisions, players learn to analyse the consequences of their choices, problem-solve, and find their own strategies and paths towards success. Just like real life.

These financial skills players learn in-game become the foundations to establish good money habits in real life. The Money Wise Game has inspired players across the world to open a bank account, start saving for university, actually read the fine print, and more, changing lives by taking small steps to build life-long habits. As more and more players join the Money Wise Game journey, they are coming together, empowered, to break the cycle of generational poverty.

## Slide 7: Money Ready - We make money make sense (HIDDEN SLIDE)

**https://moneyready.org**

*[Image: Money Ready branding and interface]*

Money Ready is a UK-based financial education charity focused on enhancing financial literacy among young people.

Working with 50,000 people across the UK every year, they aim to empower individuals with the skills necessary to manage their finances effectively, addressing the critical need for financial education as a fundamental right.

Expert-led financial education workshops linked to PSHE, Maths, Citizenship and Business curriculums, designed to build practical money skills. Topics include budgeting, banking, borrowing, saving, student finance, tax and pensions.

**By Age Group**
- Primary (4–11 yrs): Fun workshops introducing basic money habits and concepts.
- Secondary (11–16 yrs): Interactive sessions to deepen financial understanding and confidence.
- College / Uni Ready (16–19 yrs): Focus on money management, student finance and independent living skills. Includes the Uni Ready programme (formerly Uni Dosh).

**Primary Format – Change the Game**
- A whole-school, progressive programme for ages 4–11, partnered with volunteers and schools.
- Stages include: Money Minis®, Money Risers® and Money Counts® covering earning, saving, budgeting and financial decision-making.
- Includes teacher resources, classroom activities, games and take-home family challenges.

**Secondary and College Workshops (examples of topics)**
- Money basics and consumer awareness
- Budgeting, bank accounts and interest
- Tax, wages, digital finance
- Savings strategies, borrowing, credit and fraud
- Student finance, household costs and financial goals

**Delivery Options**
- In-person or virtual sessions; tailored formats for different groups available.

## Slide 8: Young Enterprise (HIDDEN SLIDE)

**https://www.young-enterprise.org.uk/what-we-do**

*[Image: Young Enterprise logo and programme materials]*

Young Enterprise offers a range of flexible programs that can last from a single day to an entire school year. These programs are tailored to fit educational curricula and the specific needs of learners, making them accessible to various age groups (up to 18 years).

**Money Heroes**
- What it is: Free primary financial education programme for ages 3 to 11, supported by HSBC UK.
- How it works: Provides teachers and parents with ready-to-use activities, storybooks/games, and free CPD to build children's money skills.

**My Money Week**
- What it is: Annual UK activity week for ages 4 to 18 focused on money skills (the page lists 15 to 21 June 2026).
- How it works: Schools use free themed resources from the online hub to run lessons and whole-school activities during the week.

**Centres of Excellence**
- What it is: UK accreditation award for schools and providers delivering high-quality financial education, includes a £1,500 bursary.
- How it works: Uses an improvement framework plus tailored expert support, training, and tools to embed financial education across the setting.

**My Money Matters**
- What it is: Online course for ages 14 to 19 exploring attitudes to money and practical skills, typically £5 per student licence.
- How it works: 6 self-paced units (about 1.5 to 2 hours total), assignable in any order via the YE Learning Zone, covering topics like saving, borrowing, debt, scams, and post-school choices.

**10X**
- What it is: Free enterprise challenge for ages 11 to 19 that links enterprise, money, and employability.
- How it works: 4-week build-and-run-a-business challenge starting from a £10 pledge, supported by teacher guide, workbook, and intro deck, with optional competitions and showcase.

## Slide 10: Money Time - financial literacy course for kids (HIDDEN SLIDE)

**https://www.moneytimekids.com**

*[Image: Money Time logo and platform interface]*

MoneyTime is an online financial literacy program designed for children aged 10 to 14, aimed at teaching them essential money management skills and smart financial decision-making. The program has gained significant traction, being utilised by over 130,000 students across 1,500 schools and endorsed by 4,000 teachers and 46,000 parents.

**Core Curriculum**
- 30+ interactive lessons teaching practical money skills like earning, budgeting, saving, borrowing, investing, property, business basics and more.
- Lessons include automatic quizzes and progress tracking.
- Learning is paired with a fun money management game where children earn virtual money and make decisions about spending, saving, investing and donating.
- Modules typically take about 30 minutes each.

**Delivery Formats**
- For Schools: Self-taught, web-based programme that fits PSHE, maths and enterprise topics; easy setup and no teacher training required.
- For Homeschool: Self-study curriculum with optional parent guides and modules for joint activities.
- For Parents/Home Use: Kids can complete the programme independently at home.

**Additional Features**
- Optional parent-child modules to bring learning into real life.
- Versions customised for multiple countries (UK, USA, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa).
- Some school access may be free or low-cost depending on region.

## Slide 24: Four Engagement Levers

### 1. PROPOSITION: "What's in it for me?"

Clear, tangible and resonating outcome the learner will gain.

**Examples:**
- Money Wise Game – "Life skills, not just financial skills" — positions learning as holistic empowerment
- Blackbullion – Student money focus: e.g. access to bursaries, partner rewards
- Gimi – Bank account framed as learning tool — every transaction becomes a teaching moment

### 2. UTILITY: "Does this have a use?"

Every interactivity drives toward a functional outcome

**Examples:**
- Money Wise Game — Virtual decisions → visible consequences — choices in-game translate to real-world habits
- Bloom "Build wealth early" — safest way for teens to start investing with real money
- KidVestors – "Earn while you learn" — get cash and stock rewards for completing lessons

### 3. RELEVANCE: "Connect it to my world"

Relevant concepts, framed in engaging (real or playful) formats pitched at the right level

**Examples:**
- Gimi – "Financial Superskills", bank account positioned as superpower, not boring adult thing
- Money Ready – £5 investment challenge for 8–12-year-olds where they invest real £5
- Money Wise – Scam simulation whilst buying trainers
- Blackbullion — Student focussed

### 4. CAPABILITY "Can I understand this?"

Don't over OR underestimate; concepts must be made relational

**Examples:**
- KidVestors – Reframes key concepts e.g. "Diversification" → "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"
- Bloom – 300+ bite-sized lessons + real investing from age 13 — treats teens as capable investors
- Blackbullion – Student money focus: e.g. access to bursaries, partner rewards

## Slide 28: Action 1: Lead with Outcomes

**Based on: PROPOSITION — "What's in it for me?"**

App/lesson/level should open with a clear, tangible benefit

Not just "Learn about X" — but "After this, you'll be able to Y"

### E.g. Lesson Opener

**❌ Before**
- "In this lesson, you'll learn about interest"
- "This level covers budgeting basics"

**✅ After**
- "After this, you'll know how to make your savings grow faster"
- "You'll be able to plan exactly where your money goes"

---

## Slide 29: Action 2: Reframe to Their World

**Based on: CONTEXTUAL FRAMING — "Does this connect to my world?"**

```
OUTCOME
    ↓
FRAMING
    ↓
Action / Knowledge
    ↓
'Adult' world / Target user's world
```

|  | PRESCRIPTIVE | RELATABLE |
|---|---|---|
| Knowledge | "Interest is the cost of borrowing money" | "When your £10 becomes £11, that's interest working" |
| Action | "Save 20% of your income" | "Next time you get birthday money, try putting half in savings" |

**Move from:**
- TEXTBOOK → EMPOWERING
- 'Adult' world → Target user's world

---

## Slide 30: Action 3: Elevate, Don't Simplify

**Based on: CAPABILITY "Can I understand this?"**

Don't remove complexity — translate it. Keep the sophistication, change the language.

### For example…

| Financial Jargon | Elevated Translation |
|---|---|
| Interest | "How your money grows while you sleep" |
| Compound interest | "Growth on top of growth" |
| Diversification | "Don't bet everything on one thing" |
| Credit score | "Your money reputation" |
| Budget | "Your money plan" |
| Liability | "Something that costs you money" |
| Asset | "Something that makes you money" |
