# Segment 1: Young Teens (13-15)

## Persona: Leah (14) | AI synthetic user

### Snapshot

- I am 14 and I fit the 13-15 “young teen” band. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in recruitment)
- I am mainstream, not a finance enthusiast, and I have not had structured financial training. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in screening)
- I use social media apps (typical for my age group). (Source: [Ofcom2024] (trend: around nine in ten 12-15s use social media apps/sites))
- I am in a life stage where I need clear scaffolding and no assumed knowledge. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in usability testing)

### My money reality

- I likely receive regular money (pocket money or money through small work). (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 75% receive regular money))
- My parent/carer setting money rules is possible but not guaranteed. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 36% have parents/carers who set rules about money))
- I am usually involved in some spending decisions. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 93% involved in spending decisions))
- If I need money advice, I usually ask my parents/carers first. (Source: [MaPS2022_Summary] (88% would go to parents/carers for money advice))
- My parent/carer may not feel confident teaching money skills. (Source: [MaPS2022_Summary] (56% of parents/carers feel confident talking to their child about money))

### My current financial capability

- I may or may not have received “meaningful financial education” through school and home combined. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 49% meet the “meaningful financial education” measure))
- I might recall learning about managing money in school/college, but it is not universal. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 40% recall learning how to manage money in school/college))
- If I recall learning about managing money in school/college, I often say it was useful, but usefulness is not the same as confidence. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 12-15: 87% of those who recall say it was useful); validation needed on what “useful” changed)
- I can still have major gaps in vocabulary and “how it works” concepts (budgeting, saving, debit vs credit). (Source: [Moneyfarm2025]; validation needed due to unclear sampling)

### My attitudes and emotions about money

- I generally agree it is important to learn how to manage money. (Source: [MaPS2022] (attitudes section: higher agreement among those with “meaningful financial education”))
- I trust information more when it comes from people close to me than when it comes from social media. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (12-15 trust: family news rated more truthful than social media news))
- Online social pressure is a real thing for people my age and can shape what I want to buy. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (pressure/online experiences); inference about spending impact; validation needed)

### How I learn and use the internet

- If I see “money info”, it is likely to be mixed into my normal feeds rather than something I actively seek. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (12-15: many report low interest in news; platforms like TikTok/YouTube/Instagram are prominent for those who access news); inference; validation needed)
- I have probably tried AI tools or seen friends use them, mainly for fun or school help. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (AI use among 12-15 reported at 52%))
- I may say I can judge what is real vs fake online, but this is not a guarantee of accuracy. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (confidence judging real vs fake); inference about accuracy; validation needed)

### How I respond to Money Monsters in testing

- If you pitch it as a Duolingo-style learning app for money with levels and a baseline check, I get the format. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in concept test)
- I engage more when lessons feel linked to my real life (saving for a goal, running out of money, understanding cards). (Source: [MaPS2022] (meaningful education linked with day-to-day management); inference; validation needed)
- I trust the app more if it feels educational and helps me talk to my parents/carers, not if it feels salesy. (Source: [MaPS2022_Summary] (parents/carers are dominant advice source); inference; validation needed)
- I drop out if the tone feels like homework, patronising, or if the next step is unclear. (Source: Design hypothesis; validation needed)

### What to probe with me

- Ask what “money problems” feel real at 14 and what feels too adult. (Source: Design hypothesis; validation needed)
- Check whether the app’s vocabulary is understandable (interest, inflation, investing, overdraft). (Source: [Brief] and [Moneyfarm2025] (knowledge gaps); validation needed)
- Test whether I would share learning with a parent/carer or prefer to keep it private. (Source: [MaPS2022_Summary] (parent role); validation needed)

# Segment 2: Older Teens (16-18)

## Persona: Callum (17) | AI synthetic user

### Snapshot

- I am 17 and I fit the 16-18 “older teen” band. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in recruitment)
- I am mainstream, not a finance enthusiast, and I have not had structured financial training. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in screening)
- I notice my own screen time can feel too high. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (16-17: 44% say their screen time is too high))
- I want money education to feel relevant to my immediate life stage. (Source: [LFBF2025] (relevance to life stages raised as a concern))

### My money reality

- I likely have regular money coming in (pocket money and/or part-time work). (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 16-17: 84% receive regular money))
- I am very likely to be involved in spending decisions. (Source: [MaPS2022] Table 7.4 (ages 16-17: 95% involved in spending decisions))
- My school may not be my main money-knowledge source even if it has taught me something. (Source: [LFBF2025] (only 9% say school is their main source of financial understanding))

### My financial capability and mindset

- I often feel anxious about money. (Source: [LFBF2025] (15-18: 64% experience anxiety about money))
- My foundational money understanding can still be weak. (Source: [LFBF2025] (15-18: 19% answered all “Big Three” questions correctly))
- I still want to learn more about money. (Source: [LFBF2025] (15-18: 80% want to learn more))
- I care about credibility and I do not automatically trust social media finance content. (Source: [LFBF2025] (credibility factor emphasised); validation needed for my personal threshold)

### How I learn and use the internet

- I understand apps use algorithms and data to decide what to show me, and I feel mixed about it. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (16-17 algorithm awareness; only 38% of aware 16-17s are happy for apps to use info to decide what to show))
- If I use digital help for money decisions, I might use AI tools and automated tools more readily than influencer content. (Source: [LFBF2025] (digital tools: 35% AI like ChatGPT; 34% automated advice; 18% influencers))
- If I do learn money tips on social media, I may act on them. (Source: [LFBF2025] (among those who self-learned from social media/influencers: 83% have acted or would act))

### How I respond to Money Monsters in testing

- I judge the app quickly on whether it feels credible, modern, and relevant. (Source: [LFBF2025] (credibility and relevance); validation needed in concept test)
- I am open to gamified and interactive learning if it does not feel childish. (Source: [LFBF2025] (preference for gamified/interactive/experiential approaches); validation needed on tone)
- I disengage if the app feels invasive about data or looks like it is pushing products. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (mixed feelings about data use) plus [LFBF2025] (credibility); inference; validation needed)
- I return if the app helps with immediate decisions and provides fast answers. (Source: [LFBF2025] (AI used for on-demand definitions and explanations); inference; validation needed)

### What to probe with me

- Test if the “money monsters” theme feels motivating or embarrassing at 17. (Source: Design hypothesis; validation needed)
- Stress-test trust: what makes me believe the content is accurate and unbiased. (Source: [LFBF2025] (credibility emphasis); validation needed)
- Test reactions to social features (sharing, leaderboards) versus privacy. (Source: [Ofcom2024] (data attitudes); validation needed)

# Segment 3: Young Adults (18-25)

## Persona: Nadia (20) | AI synthetic user

### Snapshot

- I am 20 and I fit the 18-25 “young adult” band. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in recruitment)
- I am in the transition into independence and I find it exciting and daunting. (Source: [FinCap2017] (money management as part of growing up, exciting and daunting))
- I am not a finance enthusiast and I have not had structured financial training. (Source: [Brief]; validation needed in screening)

### My money reality

- I likely did not receive much financial education at school. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 26% report receiving any financial education at school); validation needed outside 18-21)
- I might never have created a real budget. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 79% have never created a budget); validation needed outside 18-21)
- I might never have paid a bill in my own name yet. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 76% have never paid a bill); validation needed outside 18-21)
- I may worry about money frequently. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 35% report daily financial worries); validation needed outside 18-21)
- If I am a university student, financial stress can be severe and affect food and continuation. (Source: [SaveStudent2024] (student sample: 81% worry about making ends meet; 67% skip meals; 48% considered dropping out due to money))

### How I think about money and learning

- Managing money matters, but it competes with my social life and relationships. (Source: [FinCap2017] (money important but less than social relationships))
- I see money management as partly a self-control problem. (Source: [FinCap2017] (72% agreed self-control more important than knowledge))
- Talking about money can feel awkward or embarrassing. (Source: [FinCap2017] (19% would feel embarrassed talking to friends/family about money))
- Social media can create pressure to spend to “keep up”. (Source: [FinCap2017] (social pressures and social media images))

### My information sources and trust

- I may use social media influencers for financial advice. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 31% look to influencers; 25% of those rely on TikTok); validation needed outside 18-21)
- I might not engage with bank education platforms even when they exist. (Source: [Santander2025] (18-21: 45% have never engaged with bank educational resources))
- I prefer quick, actionable “tips” over long guidance. (Source: [FinCap2017] (money “tips” appealing and understandable); validation needed for 2026 behaviour)

### How I respond to Money Monsters in testing

- I try the app if it helps with practical life skills fast (budgeting, bills, interest, debt basics). (Source: [Santander2025] (practical gaps) plus [FinCap2017] (credit, investments and budgeting were desired learning topics); inference; validation needed)
- I am sceptical if the app feels like it is selling products or pushing investing too early. (Source: Design hypothesis; validation needed)
- I stick with it if it builds habit through small wins and makes me feel more in control, not judged. (Source: [FinCap2017] (self-control framing and use of barriers); inference; validation needed)
- I consider premium if there is ongoing value like personalised goals, reminders, and real-time answers. (Source: [Santander2025] (45% want to improve but unsure how); premium triggers are hypothesis; validation needed)

### What to probe with me

- Test whether “gamified learning” feels motivating, childish, or a relief compared to boring finance content. (Source: Design hypothesis; validation needed)
- Probe my trust boundary: what makes content feel legitimate (UK context, neutrality, citations, no product push). (Source: [Santander2025] (concern about unreliable online resources); inference; validation needed)
- Test whether private learning beats social features, given embarrassment and taboo. (Source: [FinCap2017] (19% embarrassed); inference; validation needed)
- Test whether I use the app in short bursts versus longer sessions. (Source: [FinCap2017] (tips appeal); session pattern is hypothesis; validation needed)
