# Comprehensive Report on UK Youth Segments for Money Monsters Financial Literacy App

## Young Teens (13-15) Segment

### Internet Usage and Digital Behavior

Young teens aged 13-15 in the UK spend approximately 3 to 4 hours online daily, primarily on smartphones and gaming consoles. Their key online activities include social media engagement, video streaming, gaming, and schoolwork. Popular social media platforms are YouTube (96% usage), Snapchat (around 71%), Instagram (69%), TikTok (58%), and WhatsApp (63%). Facebook usage has declined in this age group. They use a diverse range of apps, averaging 41 apps monthly, discovering new content mainly through social media feeds, peer recommendations, influencer content, and targeted ads. Despite high digital engagement, they are aware of negative effects like excessive screen time and impulsive spending, especially in games ([Ofcom 2025](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/online-habits/from-apps-to-ai-search-how-the-uk-goes-online-in-2025), [Ipsos MORI 2025](https://www.warc.com/content/article/warc-curated-datapoints/uk-teens-prefer-snapchat-and-instagram-facebook-slips-to-third/en-gb/129410)).

### Financial Literacy and Education

Financial literacy among 13-15 year olds remains low despite curriculum inclusion. Only about 9% cite school as their primary financial education source. Parents are the main informal educators but often lack comprehensive knowledge. Many teens have limited practical experience with budgeting, saving, or understanding interest. They express a strong desire for relevant, engaging financial education that is easy to understand and connected to real-life money management. Social media and digital tools are emerging as alternative information sources, though engagement with formal financial education platforms is low ([LFBF YPMI 2025/26](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/LFBF-YPMI-Report-2025-26.pdf), [Santander UK 2025](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education)).

### Attitudes Towards Learning and Gamification

Young teens are motivated by gamified, interactive learning environments that feel like play rather than work. They prefer bite-sized, visually stimulating content with challenges, rewards, and immediate feedback. Personalized learning that adapts to their pace and style enhances engagement. Social aspects such as collaboration and competition also increase motivation. However, socio-economic disparities affect access and digital literacy, influencing learning outcomes. Parents support digital learning but have concerns about screen time and content exposure ([PLOS One 2024](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294350), [Ofcom 2025](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/research-and-data/media-literacy-research/children), [Ygam 2025](https://ygam.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Children-and-Gaming-An-Analysis-of-Parental-Attitudes_Ygam-Mumsnet-2025-2.pdf)).

### Financial Behaviors and Experiences

Most 13-15 year olds receive regular pocket money, often weekly or monthly, which they manage with varying parental oversight. Spending focuses on snacks, clothing, phone credit, and social activities. Digital tools like prepaid debit cards help monitor spending. About a third earn money through part-time jobs or chores, fostering financial responsibility. Saving is encouraged with short- and long-term goals, often supported by parents. Financial anxieties include peer pressure, limited funds, and family financial stress. Teens want to learn how to budget, save, and avoid impulsive spending ([GoHenry UK](https://www.gohenry.com/uk/blog/financial-education/teaching-teens-about-money-management), [Cashfloat](https://www.cashfloat.co.uk/blog/money-saving/budgeting-for-teens)).

### Persona Summary (First-Person Perspective)

"I'm 14 and online most days, mainly gaming and using social media. Money feels confusing and stressful; I sometimes regret spending on in-game items. School doesn't teach much about money, and my parents try to help but don't always know. I want to learn about money in a fun way, with games and rewards, so I can be confident and avoid mistakes. An app that helps me save, budget, and control spending while making it fun would be great."

## Older Teens (16-18) Segment

### Internet Usage and Digital Behavior

UK teens aged 16-18 spend around 5 to 6 hours daily online, mostly via smartphones. Their activities include social media, video streaming, messaging, gaming, and educational content. Preferred platforms remain Snapchat (71%), Instagram (69%), TikTok (61%), YouTube, and WhatsApp. They use an average of 41 apps monthly. Content and app discovery is driven by social media feeds, peer recommendations, and AI-driven suggestions. Awareness of content algorithms is higher among older teens and those from more affluent backgrounds. Parental concerns about misinformation and inappropriate content persist ([Ofcom 2025](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/siteassets/resources/documents/research-and-data/online-research/online-nation/2025/online-nations-report-2025.pdf?v=409837), [WARC 2025](https://www.warc.com/content/article/warc-curated-datapoints/uk-teens-prefer-snapchat-and-instagram-facebook-slips-to-third/en-gb/129410)).

### Financial Literacy and Education

Financial literacy remains low; only 19% correctly answer key questions on interest, inflation, and risk, with just 3% answering all financial capability questions correctly. Financial anxiety affects 64%, especially females and disadvantaged groups. Parents are the main information source (61%), with schools and banks playing minor roles. Despite low capability, 80% want to learn more about money. The UK government plans curriculum reforms to make financial literacy compulsory from primary school. Industry initiatives have reached millions but funding and curriculum consistency remain challenges ([LFBF YPMI 2025/26](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/financial-capability-lfbf-calls-for-a-bold-shift-to-grasp-a-once-in-a-generation-opportunity), [UK Gov 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/new-curriculum-to-give-young-people-the-skills-for-life-and-work), [UK Finance 2024](https://www.ukfinance.org.uk/system/files/2024-09/Financial%20Education%20Report%202024.pdf)).

### Attitudes Towards Learning and Gamification

Older teens are motivated by education that connects clearly to career goals and real-world outcomes. They have short attention spans and prefer fast-paced, technology-rich environments. Gamification elements such as challenges, competition, rewards, creativity, and ownership strongly engage them. They prefer credible, transparent content and are skeptical of monetization tactics that feel exploitative. Interactive, meaningful learning balanced with fun is preferred. They use the internet extensively for homework and career research but have moderate skepticism about online content reliability ([Education Oxford](https://www.education.ox.ac.uk/project/career-intentions-in-16-18-year-olds), [Reflect Digital](https://www.reflectdigital.co.uk/blog/why-gamification-is-the-perfect-match-for-16-24-year-olds), [University of York](https://www.york.ac.uk/res/e-society/projects/1/UKCGOsurveyreport.pdf)).

### Financial Behaviors and Experiences

About 34% have part-time jobs, earning money and learning financial responsibility. Pocket money and earnings from chores remain common. Teens learn to read payslips and use prepaid debit cards with parental oversight. They are encouraged to budget, distinguish needs from wants, and save for goals like higher education, driving lessons, and emergencies. Despite confidence in knowledge, many lack practical experience: 79% have never budgeted, 76% never paid a bill, and 77% have not set aside emergency funds. Financial anxieties are common, and many seek advice from social media influencers due to gaps in formal education. Awareness of credit and debt is low, with limited understanding of BNPL and borrowing risks ([GoHenry UK](https://www.gohenry.com/uk/blog/financial-education/teaching-teens-about-money-management), [Santander UK 2025](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education)).
