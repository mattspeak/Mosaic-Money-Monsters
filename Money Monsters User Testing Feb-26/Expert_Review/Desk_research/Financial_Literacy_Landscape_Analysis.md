# Financial Literacy Landscape Analysis for Young People

**Prepared for:** Money Monsters
**Date:** February 2026
**Purpose:** Strategic analysis of approaches to youth financial literacy to inform product direction

---

## Executive Summary

This report analyses diverse approaches to youth financial literacy (ages 13-25) and consolidates them into **four strategic directions** for Money Monsters to consider:

| Direction | Core Premise | Best Audience Fit |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **A. Entertainment First** | Make it feel like play, not learning | Young Teens (13-15) |
| **B. AI Personal Coach** | Judgment-free guide that adapts to you | All ages, esp. 16+ |
| **C. Peer Community** | Learn with and from people like you | Young & Older Teens |
| **D. Real-World Integration** | Connect learning to actual money behaviour | Young Adults (18-25) |

**Key insight:** Money Monsters currently sits in Direction A (Entertainment First). This is strong for young teen acquisition but insufficient alone for older users or behaviour change. The most viable evolution path is to add **Direction B (AI Coach)** as a unifying layer across ages, then consider **Direction C (Peer)** or **Direction D (Real-World)** based on strategic priorities.

**Audience fit warning:** Young Adults (both graduate and non-graduate) explicitly reject "childish gamification" — the Entertainment First approach that works for 13-15s becomes a turn-off for 18-25s. Serving the full age range requires either product evolution or deliberate segmentation.

## Part 2: Gap Analysis — Alternative Approaches

The following approaches represent **genuinely different modalities** not adequately covered in the existing deck:

### 1. Content Creator / Finfluencer-Led Education

**What it is:** Financial education delivered through social media creators on TikTok, YouTube, and Instagram who have built trust with young audiences.

**Why it matters:**
- 65% of TikTok users report feeling more financially secure since using the platform for money advice
- 84% of Gen Z rely on parents/family for financial information—finfluencers fill the gap for those without financial role models
- Short-form content (30-60 seconds) suits attention spans and mobile consumption

**Key Players:**
- **Vivian Tu** ("Your Rich BFF") — 8M+ TikTok followers, bridges Millennial/Gen Z audiences
- **Taylor Price** — Gen Z creator focusing on stock investing and side hustles
- **Kia Commodore** — UK-based advocate covering budgeting, investing, money-making apps

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could partner with creators for authentic promotion
- Could incorporate social video content within the app
- Risk: Finfluencer advice is unregulated and varies wildly in quality

---

### 2. AI-Powered Personalised Coaching

**What it is:** Chatbots and AI assistants that provide on-demand, personalised financial guidance adapted to individual knowledge levels and circumstances.

**Why it matters:**
- Can scale one-to-one coaching affordably
- Adapts to user's knowledge level in real-time
- Available 24/7 for "just-in-time" learning moments
- Removes embarrassment of asking "basic" questions

**Key Players:**
- **Tendi** — AI financial advisor designed for beginners of any age
- **Cleo** — Light-hearted AI that chats about spending habits (popular with young adults)
- **Coach mAIa** — Affordable AI coaching with personalised budgeting plans
- **Kiro** — Gamified coaching with "Money Starter Packs" and badge rewards

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could integrate AI coaching for personalised learning paths
- AI could adapt difficulty and topics to individual monster-taming progress
- Could provide judgment-free support between lessons

---

### 3. Peer-to-Peer & Community Learning

**What it is:** Learning structures where young people teach each other, often with near-peer mentoring (older teens teaching younger teens).

**Why it matters:**
- Peer learning is more relatable than adult instruction
- Builds social accountability and shared motivation
- Teaching reinforces learning for the peer-educators
- Creates sustainable, scalable models

**Key Examples:**
- **Futures Financials Inc.** — Student-run 501(c)(3) where high schoolers teach financial literacy to 250+ peers
- **Teen Teach-In model** — Peer-to-peer educational events coordinated by Jump$tart Coalition
- **Lending Circles** — Groups of 6-12 people learning money management together through collective lending
- **Financial literacy social media groups** — Peer accountability and motivation

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could add multiplayer or social features (challenge friends, share progress)
- Could enable user-generated content or tips
- Could create community forums or Discord-style spaces

---

### 4. VR/Immersive Financial Simulations

**What it is:** Virtual reality experiences that simulate financial environments like trading floors, entrepreneurship scenarios, or life decisions.

**Why it matters:**
- Emotional engagement through immersion
- Safe space to make mistakes without real consequences
- Can simulate years of financial life in hours
- Appeals to gaming-oriented youth

**Key Examples:**
- **Citi's "The Finance Lab"** — VR trading with virtual counterparties
- **Goldman Sachs VR trading challenge** — 92% increase in participant proficiency
- **StockVR / Wall Street Survivor VR** — Real-time market data integration

**Industry Data:**
- VR reduces training costs by 40% vs in-person
- Hardware accessibility improving (Meta Quest 3, Apple Vision Pro)

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Long-term opportunity as VR becomes mainstream
- Current app could incorporate AR elements
- Monster battles could become more immersive

### 5. Board Games & Tabletop Experiences

**What it is:** Physical games that teach financial concepts through play, often in family or classroom settings.

**Why it matters:**
- Face-to-face social interaction
- Multi-generational engagement (family play)
- No screen time concerns for parents
- Tactile, memorable learning

**Key Examples:**
- **Monopoly** — Classic, teaches property, cash management, negotiation
- **BeFree** — Modern board game on earning, cash flow, investing
- **Bank Account Game** — Teaches checking accounts and reconciliation
- **Bakhita Business** — Entrepreneurship through young tailor's journey

**Research Finding:** Board games show short-term improvements in financial literacy, but gains diminish without ongoing reinforcement—suggesting games work best as part of broader programs.

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could develop companion physical game/merchandise
- Could host in-person events or workshops
- Reinforces digital learning through different medium

### 6. Podcast & Audio Learning

**What it is:** Audio content specifically designed for young listeners covering personal finance topics.

**Why it matters:**
- Consumed during commutes, exercise, or downtime
- Long-form allows deeper exploration of topics
- Personal, intimate format builds trust
- Low production barrier enables diverse voices

**Key Podcasts for Youth:**
- **Money Moves Podcast** — Monthly, by Gen-Z for Financial Literacy founders

- Audio could complement app learning
- Could partner with existing youth-focused podcasts

### 7. Behavioural Economics & Nudging

**What it is:** Designing systems that guide (nudge) users toward better financial behaviours, rather than just teaching concepts.

**Why it matters:**
- Financial literacy alone doesn't change behaviour
- Even pension experts need nudges to save adequately
- Small design choices have outsized impact on outcomes

**Effective Nudges for Youth:**
- **Opt-out mechanisms** — Auto-enrol in savings, require action to withdraw
- **Reminders** — Bring saving to attention, counter "availability bias"
- **Peer pressure** — Social visibility of saving progress
- **Default controls** — Pre-set saving amounts
- **Micro-investing** — Round-ups and small automatic transfers

**Research Finding:** "Financial literacy and awareness of the importance of saving are not enough for individuals to act—the combination of rational instruments like financial literacy AND nudging is necessary to move individuals to action."

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could integrate reminder systems
- Could add "automatic save" features tied to lessons
- Could make progress social/visible
- Could partner with banking apps for real-world nudges

**Why it matters:**

**Key Examples:**

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could sponsor or host financial literacy hackathons
- Could create in-app challenges with real prizes
- Could partner with existing competition organisers

### 9. Youth-Led Peer Teaching Initiatives

**What it is:** Programs where young people are trained to deliver financial education to their peers.

**Why it matters:**
- Empowers youth as educators, not just recipients
- More relatable delivery than adult-led programs
- Creates multiplier effect
- Builds leadership alongside financial skills

**Key Examples:**
- **Futures Financials Inc.** — High school club became 501(c)(3) teaching 1,000+ students
- **Cardinal Credit Union student branches** — Teens run actual bank branches serving peers
- **SFEPD peer-to-peer programs** — College students teaching financial literacy

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could create "Money Monsters Ambassador" program
- Could enable in-app peer mentoring
- Could provide resources for youth to teach others

### 10. Interactive Narrative / Choose Your Own Adventure

**What it is:** Story-based learning where learners make financial decisions that affect narrative outcomes.

**Why it matters:**
- Engages emotional investment through story
- Shows consequences of decisions over time
- Makes abstract concepts concrete through character identification
- More memorable than abstract lessons

**Key Examples:**
- **Ten Dollar Adventure** — Interactive children's book with 10+ storylines about mini-businesses
- **Fidelity's Choose Your Own Adventure curriculum** — High school modules with interactive checkpoints
- **Banzai** — 32 life-based scenarios in choose-your-own-adventure format

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could deepen narrative elements around monsters

- Could create "life simulation" elements showing long-term consequences

### 11. Employer/Workplace Youth Programs

**What it is:** Financial education delivered through employers to apprentices, interns, and young workers.

**Why it matters:**
- Reaches youth at moment of first earnings
- Can tie to real paycheck and benefits decisions
- Employer incentive to have financially stable workforce
- Can include matching savings programs

**Key Examples:**
- **FLEC Resource Guide** — Federal guide for youth employment + financial literacy partnerships
- **JFF National Apprentice Fund** — $2M supporting apprentice completion through financial support

- Could create B2B offering for employers
- Could partner with apprenticeship programs
- Could develop workplace-specific modules

### 12. Real-Money Practice with Gamification

**What it is:** Combining actual money management (real debit cards, real savings) with educational gamification.

**Why it matters:**
- Real stakes create real learning
- Bridges gap between theory and practice
- Parents involved through oversight features
- Builds actual financial track record

**Key Examples:**
- **Greenlight** — 6M+ users, debit card + savings (1-5% interest) + gamified learning
- **GoHenry/Acorns Early** — "Money Missions" with videos, quizzes, points, badges for real money use
- **Gimi** (in deck) — Banking as learning tool

**Implications for Money Monsters:**
- Could partner with kids' banking providers
- Could add features that connect to real money progress
- Could offer rewards redeemable for real value

## Part 3: Strategic Framework — Four Clear Directions

Rather than 17 overlapping modalities, financial literacy approaches can be distilled into **four distinct strategic directions**. Each represents a fundamentally different theory of change.

---

### Direction A: ENTERTAINMENT FIRST
**"Make it feel like play, not learning"**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Core premise** | Young people will engage with financial content if it doesn't feel like education |
| **Key mechanics** | Gamification, streaks, points, badges, leaderboards, short-form video, social sharing |
| **Content style** | Finfluencer-led, TikTok-native, meme-friendly, character-driven |
| **Learning model** | Incidental learning through entertainment; knowledge absorbed through repetition and fun |
| **Real-world connection** | Minimal — focuses on knowledge acquisition, not behaviour |

**What this looks like:**
- TikTok-style content within app
- Creator partnerships for authentic promotion
- Monster battles and collecting mechanics
- Shareable achievements and viral moments
- Duolingo-style progression systems

**Examples:** Duolingo, TikTok finfluencers, YouTube edutainment

---

### Direction B: AI PERSONAL COACH
**"A judgment-free guide that adapts to you"**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Core premise** | People need personalised, on-demand support — not one-size-fits-all curricula |
| **Key mechanics** | Conversational AI, adaptive difficulty, personalised recommendations, 24/7 availability |
| **Content style** | Responsive, contextual, shame-free, meets you where you are |
| **Learning model** | Just-in-time learning; answers when you need them, adapts to your situation |
| **Real-world connection** | Medium — can contextualise to user's actual financial situation |

**What this looks like:**
- AI "monster guide" that knows your progress
- Ask any question without embarrassment
- Personalised learning paths based on knowledge gaps
- Proactive nudges based on behaviour patterns
- Contextual explanations tied to your financial situation

**Examples:** Cleo, ChatGPT for finance, Coach mAIa, Kiro

---

### Direction C: PEER COMMUNITY
**"Learn with and from people like you"**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Core premise** | Financial learning is social; peers are more relatable than experts or institutions |
| **Key mechanics** | Social features, challenges with friends, community forums, peer mentoring, ambassadors |
| **Content style** | User-generated, authentic voices, shared struggles and wins |
| **Learning model** | Social learning theory; motivation through accountability and shared experience |
| **Real-world connection** | Medium — accountability creates behaviour change, but still primarily knowledge-focused |

**What this looks like:**
- Challenge friends to money quizzes
- Community discussion spaces
- Share progress and achievements
- "Money Monsters Ambassador" program
- Near-peer mentoring (older teens guiding younger)
- User tips and success stories

**Examples:** Discord communities, Reddit r/personalfinance, Lending Circles, peer teaching programs

---

### Direction D: REAL-WORLD INTEGRATION
**"Connect learning to actual money behaviour"**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Core premise** | Knowledge doesn't change behaviour — real stakes and smart defaults do |
| **Key mechanics** | Banking integration, behavioural nudges, real money practice, automatic saving |
| **Content style** | Action-oriented, linked to transactions, immediate feedback loops |
| **Learning model** | Behavioural economics; learn by doing with real consequences |
| **Real-world connection** | High — directly tied to actual financial behaviour |

**What this looks like:**
- Partnership with kids' banking apps (Greenlight, GoHenry)
- Lessons unlock when you complete real saving goals
- Round-up investing tied to app progress
- Push notifications at spending moments
- Track real financial progress alongside learning progress

**Examples:** Greenlight, GoHenry Money Missions, Acorns, auto-enrolment pensions

### Strategic Questions for Money Monsters

**1. Which direction is Money Monsters today?**
Currently sits primarily in **Direction A (Entertainment First)** with strong gamification and monster-taming metaphor. Some elements of interactive narrative.

**2. Where should it evolve?**
The key strategic choice: double down on one direction, or build bridges to others?

**3. What would it take to add each direction?**

| Direction | Integration Effort | Partnership Required? |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| A. Entertainment First | Already core | Finfluencer deals optional |
| B. AI Coach | Medium (product build) | No |
| C. Peer Community | Medium (product build) | No |
| D. Real-World Integration | High (partnership-dependent) | Yes (banking apps) |

**4. Are directions complementary or exclusive?**
- A + B work well together (fun base + personalised depth)
- A + C work well together (fun base + social motivation)
- D requires external partnerships regardless of other choices
- B + C have some tension (private vs social learning)

| **D. Real-World Integration** | ⬤◯◯ Weak | ⬤⬤◯ Medium | ⬤⬤⬤ Strong | ⬤⬤⬤ Strong |

### Rationale by Segment

#### Young Teens (13-15) — "Lily"

**Primary fit: A (Entertainment) + C (Peer)**

| What they need | Why |
|----------------|-----|
| Game-first experience | "If it feels like homework, I'll zone out" — needs to compete with Roblox and TikTok |
| Social proof | "If my friends are using it" — peer validation critical for adoption |
| Quick wins | Low tolerance for delayed gratification; need immediate sense of progress |
| Not childish | Want to feel grown-up; design must thread the needle between fun and mature |

**Why B (AI Coach) is medium fit:**
- Could work for shame-free questions
- But may feel impersonal; this age wants social connection
- Risk of feeling like a chatbot homework helper

**Why D (Real-World) is weak fit:**
- Limited real money to manage (£5-6/week pocket money)
- Abstract connection to financial consequences
- Parents still gatekeep banking

---

#### Older Teens (16-18) — "Jake"

**Primary fit: B (AI Coach) + C (Peer)**

| What they need | Why |
|----------------|-----|
| Credibility and trust | "I'm skeptical" — wary of manipulation, aware of scams |
| Real decision support | Facing university, student loans, first jobs — need practical answers |
| Peer relatability | Want to learn from/with people in same situation |
| Not patronising | "I'm basically an adult" — don't talk down to them |

**Why A (Entertainment) is medium fit:**
- Still responds to gamification, but notices when it's manipulative
- "Forced gamification" is a stated turn-off
- More interested in substance than fun

**Why D (Real-World) is medium fit:**
- Has part-time job income (£300-400/month)
- Starting to make real financial decisions
- But banking integration feels "for kids" (Greenlight/GoHenry positioning)

---

#### Young Adults — Graduate (18-25) — "Priya"

**Primary fit: B (AI Coach) + D (Real-World)**

| What they need | Why |
|----------------|-----|
| Anxiety reduction | "I feel like I'm winging it" — wants to feel in control, not worse |
| Immediate practical help | Living paycheck to paycheck; needs to solve this month, not learn theory |
| Judgment-free support | Worried about appearing behind; needs safe space to ask basic questions |
| Integration with existing tools | Already using Monzo; wants financial education connected to real money |

**Why A (Entertainment) is weak fit:**
- "Too gamified feels childish" — explicit concern
- Wants practical value, not points and badges
- Time-poor; won't engage with anything that feels frivolous

**Why C (Peer) is medium fit:**
- Some interest in peer validation ("if other people my age use it")
- But also comparing themselves negatively to peers on social media
- Anxiety about money means less appetite for social exposure

---

#### Young Adults — Non-Graduate (18-25) — "Ryan"

**Primary fit: B (AI Coach) + D (Real-World)**

| What they need | Why |
|----------------|-----|
| Practical, not aspirational | "I just want to get through the month" — survival mode |
| Acknowledgment of different reality | No student debt but no graduate salary; content often assumes wrong things |
| Quick actionable wins | "Show me what to DO" — not reading articles or learning theory |
| Non-judgmental | "I know I should save. I can't afford to." — don't lecture |

**Why A (Entertainment) is weak fit:**
- Practical mindset; "live for today" but not in a playful way
- Gamification feels irrelevant to real struggles
- "Gamification that feels childish or manipulative" — explicit concern

**Why C (Peer) is medium fit:**
- Could value peer support from others in similar situation
- But distrust of institutions extends to organised communities
- Values authenticity over organised programs

---

### Strategic Implications

#### If Money Monsters stays focused on Young Teens:
- Double down on **Direction A (Entertainment)** + add **Direction C (Peer)**
- Gamification, social features, shareable content, friend challenges
- Accept that older segments will need a different product or evolution path

#### If Money Monsters wants to span all ages:
- **Direction B (AI Coach)** is the unifying element — works across all segments
- Execution must adapt: playful for young teens, practical for young adults
- Add **Direction D (Real-World)** via partnerships to capture young adult value

#### If Money Monsters prioritises behaviour change over engagement:
- **Direction D (Real-World)** is essential but partnership-dependent
- **Direction B (AI Coach)** can bridge knowledge to action through personalisation
- **Direction A (Entertainment)** becomes a hook, not the core

#### Hybrid approaches to test:

| Combination | Best for | Trade-off |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| A + B | All ages | Entertainment hooks, AI provides depth; but may feel unfocused |
| A + C | Young Teens, Older Teens | Social + fun; but limited behaviour change |
| B + D | Young Adults | Practical + integrated; but may lose younger users |
| A + B + C | Young Teens through Older Teens | Maximum engagement; but complex to execute |
