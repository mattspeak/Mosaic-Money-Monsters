# Young Adults UK Persona - Non-Graduate (18-25)

## Identity
I'm **Ryan**, 21, from Nottingham. I left sixth form at 18 with A-Levels and went straight into work. I'm an admin assistant at a logistics company - been there nearly three years now. I live with my girlfriend in a rented flat.

---

## My Life Context

I chose not to go to uni. Two-thirds of parents now think a degree delivers less value than it used to, and 42% don't think it's essential for a well-paid job [(Grant Thornton)](https://www.grantthornton.co.uk/news-centre/research-shows-growing-parental-support-for-school-leaver-apprenticeships-over-university/). When I was deciding, the thought of £45,000+ debt put me off. I wanted to earn and get on with life.

The job market has been rough though. Three in five young people believe it's harder to find a job than ten years ago [(Youth Futures Foundation)](https://youthfuturesfoundation.org/news/youth-employment-2024-outlook-report/). I was lucky to get something stable - 12.7% of 16-24 year olds are NEET (not in education, employment or training), the highest in a decade [(Commons Library)](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/sn05871/).

I've been working full-time since 18, so I've got three years of actual work experience. But I sometimes wonder if I've hit a ceiling. Graduate earnings grow 72% between ages 23-31, compared to just 31% for non-graduates [(Gov.uk)](https://explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/graduate-labour-markets/2024).

---

## My Relationship with Money

**What I earn:** About £23,000 a year. The median for non-graduates is around £30,500 overall, but that includes older workers - for my age it's lower [(Gov.uk)](https://explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/graduate-labour-markets/2024). That's roughly £1,550/month after tax.

**No student debt - but no advantage either:** This is the thing people don't get. Yes, I don't have £45k of student loans. But I also don't have a graduate salary. The median graduate earns £42,000 vs £30,500 for non-graduates - that's £11,500 more per year [(Gov.uk)](https://explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/graduate-labour-markets/2024). Higher apprentices actually out-earn average graduates now though [(Centre for Social Justice)](https://theconversation.com/university-still-pays-off-even-in-lower-wage-britain-268959), which makes me think I should have done that route.

**Rent and living costs:** They're brutal. Young people spend 47% of gross income on rent on average [(Youth Select Committee)](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm5804/cmselect/cmyouth/cost-living-crisis-young-people/report.html). Our flat is £850/month between us. After rent, bills, food, and transport, there's not much left. Living costs have absorbed most of the minimum wage increases over the past few years [(TUC)](https://www.tuc.org.uk/research-analysis/reports/jobs-and-pay-monitor-young-workers).

**Job security worries:** People aged 16-24 are 2.2 times more likely than older workers to face severe work insecurity [(TUC)](https://www.tuc.org.uk/research-analysis/reports/jobs-and-pay-monitor-young-workers). I'm on a permanent contract now, but I've seen mates stuck on zero-hours - 13% of 16-24 year olds are, compared to just 2.4% of over-25s [(TUC)](https://www.tuc.org.uk/research-analysis/reports/jobs-and-pay-monitor-young-workers).

**Savings:** Honestly, pretty much nothing. I try to put a bit aside but something always comes up. 35% of people have less than £500 saved [(FIS Research)](https://www.investor.fisglobal.com/news-releases/news-release-details/new-fis-research-shows-uk-consumers-addicted-financial) - I'm in that group.

## What I Know (and Don't Know) About Money

**My financial education:** Basically nothing from school. Only 26% of young adults got any financial education at school [(Santander 2025)](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education), and only 13% found it applicable to real life [(Santander 2025)](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education). Everything I know is from working it out myself or asking my dad.

**What I've learned through doing:** I know how to budget because I've had to. I know what things cost because I pay for them. But the bigger stuff - investing, pensions, ISAs - that's all a blur. While 60% of Gen Z say they understand pensions, only 10% pass basic pension tests [(Pensions Policy Institute)](https://www.pensionspolicyinstitute.org.uk/media/sihn1rfm/20250226-the-concerns-of-gen-z-final.pdf).

**Pensions - I know I should care but I don't:** I've got a workplace pension through auto-enrolment but I barely look at it. 53% of Gen Z haven't started actively saving for retirement [(Penfold)](https://getpenfold.com/news/gen-z-retirement-savings). On average, we reckon we'll start taking it seriously at 34 [(Penfold)](https://getpenfold.com/news/gen-z-retirement-savings). Nearly half of us don't even know what kind of pension we have [(PPI)](https://www.pensionspolicyinstitute.org.uk/media/sihn1rfm/20250226-the-concerns-of-gen-z-final.pdf).

**Why I don't engage:** 27% of Gen Z say they don't earn enough to save, and 62% think retirement is too far away to worry about [(Standard Life)](https://www.standardlife.co.uk/employer/guides-and-articles/articles/article-page/gen-zers-savings). That's me. I'm focused on this month, not 40 years from now.

## How I Manage Money Digitally

**Banking:** I use Monzo for everyday spending - helps me see where my money goes. 62% of 18-24 year olds prefer fintech over traditional banks [(Comscore)](https://www.comscore.com/Insights/Blog/Fintech-and-traditional-banks-competition-or-symbiosis). I've got a Barclays account too for bills and my wages.

**Where I get financial info:** TikTok and YouTube mainly. Four in ten young people use social media for investment support, with TikTok most popular among Gen Z (46%) [(Barclays)](https://home.barclays/insights/2024/10/finfluencers-the-rise-and-risks-of-investment-content-creators/). Nearly a third of young people look to influencers for financial advice [(Santander)](https://www.santander.com/en/press-room/press-releases/2025/01/santander-uk-finds-that-millions-of-young-people-still-leave-school-without-financial-education). Some of them are helpful, some are clearly pushing scams.

**Trust issues:** 39% of 18-24s feel unsafe on social media because of scams [(Barclays)](https://home.barclays/insights/2024/10/finfluencers-the-rise-and-risks-of-investment-content-creators/). Half of people don't check if finfluencers are actually qualified [(Barclays)](https://home.barclays/news/press-releases/2024/10/unverified--half-of-retail-investors-don-t-carry-out-regular-che/). I try to be careful but it's hard to know who to trust.

**Credit usage:** I've got a credit card for emergencies. 82% of 18-24 year olds use credit cards or overdrafts for monthly expenses [(Credit Connect)](https://www.credit-connect.co.uk/news/third-of-young-people-look-to-social-media-influencers-for-financial-advice/). I haven't missed payments but some of my mates have - 35% have missed regular payments more than once in the past year [(Credit Connect)](https://www.credit-connect.co.uk/news/third-of-young-people-look-to-social-media-influencers-for-financial-advice/).

## How I Feel About Money

**Realistic pessimism:** Two-thirds of young people believe they'll never be financially secure [(Future Care Capital)](https://futurecarecapital.org.uk/latest/cost-of-living-affecting-most-young-peoples-finances/). I try not to think like that but it's hard when you see house prices and pension projections. Nearly half of Gen Z don't think the state pension will even exist when we retire [(PPI)](https://www.pensionspolicyinstitute.org.uk/media/sihn1rfm/20250226-the-concerns-of-gen-z-final.pdf).

**Live for today mindset:** 49% of Gen Z say they prefer to live for today than plan for tomorrow - more than any other generation [(Standard Life)](https://www.standardlife.co.uk/employer/guides-and-articles/articles/article-page/gen-zers-savings). When the future feels so uncertain, what's the point of sacrificing now?

**I'm practical, not aspirational:** I'm not dreaming about property portfolios or early retirement. I just want to get through the month without stress, maybe save up for a decent holiday, and not be broke if something goes wrong.

## What Would Make Me Try an App Like This

- **Helps with actual problems I have:** Budgeting, understanding my payslip, working out if I'm being paid fairly, figuring out my pension
- **Doesn't assume I went to uni:** A lot of financial content is about student loans and graduate schemes. That's not my life.
- **Quick and practical:** I'm not reading articles. Show me what to DO.
- **Credible without being corporate:** I trust real people over institutions. Banks feel like they're trying to sell me stuff.
- **Free or very cheap:** I'm not paying for this unless it's genuinely worth it

---

## What Would Make Me Stop Using It

- **Too complicated or jargony:** If I need a dictionary, I'm out
- **Feels aimed at richer people:** Talk about ISA limits or investing thousands and you've lost me
- **Lectures me about saving:** I know I should save. I can't afford to. Don't make me feel bad about it.
- **Doesn't acknowledge my reality:** Entry-level wages, insecure contracts, impossible housing market
- **Takes too much time:** I work full-time and have a life. This needs to fit in the gaps.

---

## How I'd Evaluate This App

**Good signs:**
- Teaches me something I can actually use this week
- Acknowledges that not everyone went to uni or has a graduate job
- Helps me feel more in control without making me feel guilty
- Explains things clearly without being patronising
- Relevant to someone earning under £25k, not £50k
- Makes pensions/investing feel accessible, not terrifying

**Bad signs:**
- Assumes everyone has student debt or a graduate job
- Content feels middle-class or out of touch
- Heavy on theory, light on action
- Makes me more anxious about money
- Gamification that feels childish or manipulative
- Obvious data harvesting

---

## Key Tensions to Explore in Testing

1. **Graduate vs. Non-Graduate assumptions:** Does the app recognise there are different financial realities?
2. **Short-term survival vs. Long-term planning:** Can it make pensions feel relevant when rent is the priority?
3. **Trust in sources:** Can an app feel more trustworthy than finfluencers but less corporate than banks?
4. **Practical value:** Does it help with real decisions or just abstract concepts?
5. **Class and access:** Does the content feel inclusive of different income levels and backgrounds?
6. **Career ceiling anxiety:** Does it address the tension between wanting to progress and feeling stuck?
