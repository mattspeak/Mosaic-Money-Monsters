# Money Monsters
## UX & Proposition Review
## Pre-Launch Assessment for User Testing

# 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## Overall Verdict

Money Monsters has the right ambition and a sound educational foundation. The progressive curriculum, character-driven scenarios, and monster collection mechanic are genuine assets — they create a framework capable of delivering engaging financial learning that competitors cannot easily replicate.

The current execution has headroom to do justice to that structure. The app asks users to commit (email, name, DOB, parental consent, 15-question quiz) before showing any value. The learning phase is passive: read text, tap 'Next', answer quiz. Age differentiation is absent — a 12-year-old and a 24-year-old see identical onboarding, tone, and visual language. Some content moralises rather than teaches. Visual assets remain incomplete.

The good news: the core architecture is right, and many high-impact fixes are small. This is a **'Fix & Go'** situation — adapting current UX without major redesign is achievable before user testing. The research that follows should focus not on whether users like what exists, but on **what would make them choose this over alternatives**.

|--------|-----------|-------------------------------------------|
| Learning Structure | 7 | Well-sequenced across 8 levels — maintain as a core strength |

| Proposition Clarity | 3 | Add value proposition screen before sign-up |

| Age Differentiation | 2 | Add age-adapted onboarding copy; scale vocabulary by level |
| Content Quality | 5 | Reframe from academic definitions to practical user goals |
| Visual Completeness | 3 | Complete all placeholders; fix spelling errors (7× 'Languge') |

## CORE STRENGTHS — WHAT THE APP GETS RIGHT
- **Progressive curriculum** delivering in a familiar Duolingo-style format — this is the competitive moat
- **Character-driven scenarios** tied to real life stages (Naya 12, Tom 15, Ranveer 17, Sarah 24) create immediate relevance
- **Monster collection** as reward and progress metaphor — genuine emotional hook for 12-15s
- **Habit-building mechanics:** streak + daily CTA provide proven retention loop

## KEY CONCERNS — WHAT NEEDS TO CHANGE
- **No value proposition before commitment** — users give email, name, DOB before seeing what the app does
- **Passive learning model** — read-tap-quiz cannot compete with TikTok's 15-second engagement or AI's instant answers
- **Some questions moralise rather than teach** — "Should you spend all your money?" tests values, not knowledge
- **Age differentiation is absent** — identical experience from age 12 to 25 will alienate older segments

## Part 2: Engagement Levers

**Learnings from competitors on how to drive engagement**

Four Engagement Levers

### 1. PROPOSITION: "What's in it for me?"

Clear, tangible and resonating outcome the learner will gain.

**Examples:**
- Money Wise Game – "Life skills, not just financial skills" — positions learning as holistic empowerment
- Blackbullion – Student money focus: e.g. access to bursaries, partner rewards
- Gimi – Bank account framed as learning tool — every transaction becomes a teaching moment

### 2. UTILITY: "Does this have a use?"

Every interactivity drives toward a functional outcome

**Examples:**
- Money Wise Game — Virtual decisions → visible consequences — choices in-game translate to real-world habits
- Bloom "Build wealth early" — safest way for teens to start investing with real money
- KidVestors – "Earn while you learn" — get cash and stock rewards for completing lessons

### 3. RELEVANCE: "Connect it to my world"

Relevant concepts, framed in engaging (real or playful) formats pitched at the right level

**Examples:**
- Gimi – "Financial Superskills", bank account positioned as superpower, not boring adult thing
- Money Ready – £5 investment challenge for 8–12-year-olds where they invest real £5
- Money Wise – Scam simulation whilst buying trainers
- Blackbullion — Student focussed

### 4. CAPABILITY "Can I understand this?"

Don't over OR underestimate; concepts must be made relational

**Examples:**
- KidVestors – Reframes key concepts e.g. "Diversification" → "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"
- Bloom – 300+ bite-sized lessons + real investing from age 13 — treats teens as capable investors
- Blackbullion – Student money focus: e.g. access to bursaries, partner rewards

## Appling engagement principles to Money Monsters

### Action 1: Lead with Outcomes

**Based on: PROPOSITION — "What's in it for me?"**

App/lesson/level should open with a clear, tangible benefit

Not just "Learn about X" — but "After this, you'll be able to Y"

** E.g. Lesson Opener

**❌ Before**
- "In this lesson, you'll learn about interest"
- "This level covers budgeting basics"

**✅ After**
- "After this, you'll know how to make your savings grow faster"
- "You'll be able to plan exactly where your money goes"

---

### Action 2: Reframe to Their World

**Based on: CONTEXTUAL FRAMING — "Does this connect to my world?"**

```
OUTCOME
    ↓
FRAMING
    ↓
Action / Knowledge
    ↓
'Adult' world / Target user's world
```

|  | PRESCRIPTIVE | RELATABLE |
|---|---|---|
| Knowledge | "Interest is the cost of borrowing money" | "When your £10 becomes £11, that's interest working" |
| Action | "Save 20% of your income" | "Next time you get birthday money, try putting half in savings" |

**Move from:**
- TEXTBOOK → EMPOWERING
- 'Adult' world → Target user's world

---

### Action 3: Elevate, Don't Simplify

**Based on: CAPABILITY "Can I understand this?"**

Don't remove complexity — translate it. Keep the sophistication, change the language.

**For example…**

| Financial Jargon | Elevated Translation |
|---|---|
| Interest | "How your money grows while you sleep" |
| Compound interest | "Growth on top of growth" |
| Diversification | "Don't bet everything on one thing" |
| Credit score | "Your money reputation" |
| Budget | "Your money plan" |
| Liability | "Something that costs you money" |
| Asset | "Something that makes you money" |

# 3. MONEY MONSTERS UX EXPERT REVIEW

## 3.1 PROPOSITION & DESIRABILITY

### What works
- The "adventure" framing is likely to peak interest to learn about money for 12-15s 
- Character-driven scenarios (Naya, Tom etc.) tied to recognisable life stages creates  relevance
### Improvement areas
- User-framed benefits aren't clear above the fold on the landing page; "Financial skills" is a means not an end.
- The app's "reasons to believe" (Certified...) before establishing value
- "Story-driven lessons" are promised but not evident in actual modules
- Could use visual explanation alongside text — show, don't just tell
### Competitive Reality Check: Time to First Value

| Alternative        | Time to value | Personalisation                | Effort to start                  |
| ------------------ | ------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------- |
| ChatGPT / AI       | 10 seconds    | Fully personalised to question | Type a question                  |
| TikTok / YouTube   | 15-60 seconds | Algorithm-curated              | Scroll and watch                 |
| Duolingo [1]       | 60 seconds    | Choose subject, set goal       | Tap to begin                     |
| **Money Monsters** | 60+ seconds   | None until quiz completion     | Email, name, DOB, consent, 15 Qs |
|                    |               |                                |                                  |
[1] https://goodux.appcues.com/blog/duolingo-user-onboarding 
### Recommendations (Quick Wins)
- Add a single-screen value proposition before sign-up: e.g. "Get better with money in 5 minutes a day" with 3 benefit bullets. e.g. "Get your kid to better manage their pocket money" / "Ensure your child is thinking about the future"
- Ask age first, then adapt the proposition language to segment (e.g., "adventure" for 12-15s, "money skills" for 15+)

---

## 3.2 ENGAGEMENT & MOTIVATION

### What works
- Strong habit spine: 'Your Turn' daily prompt + streak mechanic provide a proven retention loop
- Monster transformation (angry → friendly) creates a satisfying progress metaphor
- Collectable monster library gives users a tangible artefact of achievement
- Question format variety (multiple choice, true/false, match-pairs, multi-select) prevents autopilot answering

### What needs to change
- Onboarding 'hook' is weak: Test questions start with no personalisation 'tell me your age', "Professor Money" signals complex/abstract topic upfront 
- Learning topics associated as different monsters is not clear (see related point in 3.3 re labelling of topics)
- Learning phase is passive in places — read text, tap 'Next', repeat with no interactivity until quiz
- No intro to learner objective "0 monsters" is meaningless
- Progress metrics ('2 Monsters 12') are unclear — users need to understand the reward loop upfront
- 'Leave Anyway — all your progress will disappear!' uses fear rather than positive motivation

### Recommendations (Quick Wins)
- Revise onboarding to start with age selection, then present age-appropriate quiz
- Following onboarding present the Monsters/topics the learner will learn (see long term recommendations); add single word description below monster name (e.g. 'SAMMY' 'SAVING')
- Add explanation of the reward loop early: "Complete the level to unlock new monsters"
### Recommendations (Longer Term)
- Let users choose what topics learn 
- Instead of reading about budgeting → users drag income/expenses into categories
- Instead of 'True/False: save money' → users decide how to split £50 and see compound effects
---

## 3.3 LEARNING EFFECTIVENESS & CONTENT QUALITY

### Where content is stronger
- Higher levels (5-8) have genuinely useful, actionable content: pay-yourself-first, compound interest, credit scores
- Character scenarios like Sarah's Australia savings use specific, practical calculations
- Calculation-based questions test actual skill rather than opinion
- Level structure is well-paced, building complexity progressively

### Where content needs work
- Moralising disguised as learning: "Should you spend all your money?" tests values, not knowledge
- Trivially obvious questions with joke distractors insult even a 12-year-old's intelligence
- Questions focus on money in the abstract rather than the user's relationship with money
- Definitions framed in textbook style ("What is money?") rather than practical application

%% 1. EARNING – Understanding where money comes from
1. SPENDING – Making smart purchase decisions
2. SAVING – Building up a reserve for the future
3. BORROWING – Understanding debt and credit
4. PROTECTING – Avoiding scams, managing risk
5. GROWING – Introduction to investing concepts  %%

### Intro quiz: right idea, needs calibration
- 15 questions is too long before users have committed
- A 12-year-old encountering mortgage questions may conclude "this isn't for me"
- Should start with age-appropriate items, ramp quickly, and shift to confidence-building if struggling

### Recommendations (Quick Wins)
- Shorten intro quiz to 8-10 questions; remove mortgage/retirement Qs for younger users
- Replace moralising questions with genuine comprehension tests
- Reframe questions to be relatable: "You've earned pocket money. What do you do next?" not "Once you earn money, you should spend it immediately — True/False?"
- Start with familiar examples (local currency) before building to unfamiliar

---

## 3.4 JOBS-TO-BE-DONE & USABILITY

### The content solves the right problems — but frames them academically
- Domain coverage is genuinely broad and well-chosen: saving, credit, currency, budgeting, investment
- Teens don't wake up wanting to "learn financial literacy" — they want to save for something, understand a payslip, or avoid being ripped off
- The "what" is right; the "why should I care right now?" is missing
- Every lesson needs a clearer answer to "When will I actually use this?"

### Usability: Functional Layout, but Friction-Heavy Flow
- Basic screen layouts are clean; simple CTAs reduce decision fatigue
- Magic-link email forces context switch (leave app → email → click → return) adding drop-off points
- Date format isn't market-adaptive (UK vs Czech)
- 'Languge' misspelled 7 times; "Name can't be changed once saved" feels rigid

### Top 5 Likely Abandonment Moments
1. Splash/sign-up loop without value promise
2. 'Check your inbox' (context switch, delay)
3. 'Invalid date format' on DOB entry
4. Parental approval wait (user has no control)
5. Question 10+ of intro quiz (still no content seen)

### Quick Wins
- Fix 'Languge' × 7 and all copy errors
- Add locale-adaptive DOB picker with format hint
- Allow nickname changes
- Replace 'progress disappears' with positive retention message
- Let users learn while email verifies (guest mode)

---

## 3.5 VISUAL IDENTITY & TONE | AGE DIFFERENTIATION

### Visual Identity: Incomplete and Skewing Young
- 28 'Lorem Ipsum' placeholders; intro animation "waiting for animation from Bo"
- Currently text-heavy with sparse use of icons and imagery
- "Save for a New Gaming Console" has no picture of a gaming console
- Primary-school aesthetic (Budgie the Budget Monster, "Managing money is your first adventure!")

### Age Differentiation: The Biggest Gap
- A 12-year-old sees identical onboarding, tone, visual language as a 24-year-old
- "Welcome to your financial adventure!" may charm a 12-year-old but will alienate a 17-year-old
- Vocabulary definitions use identical simplified language across all 8 levels
- This is the single design decision most likely to cause rejection across multiple segments

### Recommendations (Quick Wins)
- Complete all monster profiles, animations, visual assets before user testing
- Run 'trust pass' QA: fix every spelling error, placeholder, incomplete sentence
- Scale vocabulary definitions by level — Level 1 gets simplified language, Level 6-8 gets adult terminology
- Add age-adapted onboarding copy: 12-15 "Start your money adventure" / 15-18 "Get smart about your money" / 18-25 "The financial skills you never learned"

## SEGMENT 1: 12-15 YEAR OLDS

### Likely hooks
- **Monster collection and gamification** — this group responds to points, badges, and streaks; 80% of Duolingo users cite gamification as key to enjoyment
- **"Adventure" framing reduces money anxiety** — learning positioned as fun rather than serious adult stuff lowers the barrier to entry
- **Visual, interactive format** — 4+ hours daily on phones (Ofcom 2024); they expect colour, movement, and doing over reading
- **Short, snappy sessions** — fits between other activities; respects "5-minute attention" reality

### Likely blockers
- **Text-heavy = homework** — if it looks like school, they'll zone out; research shows passive learning doesn't work for this group
- **Patronising or childish tone** — they're 14, not 8; "this feels like it's for little kids" is a death sentence
- **Abstract money concepts** — interest rates and mortgages feel irrelevant; they need pocket money scenarios, not salary planning
- **Slow onboarding** — 15-question quiz before any value? They'll bounce to TikTok inside 90 seconds

### Top 3 Recommendations for This Segment
1. **Lead with the game, not the learning** — position as "money game" not "financial education"; let them discover it teaches something
2. **Connect scenarios to pocket money, birthday cash, and saving for games** — make examples concrete and immediate
3. **Keep text under 3 sentences per screen** — add visuals, animations, interactions wherever possible

---

## SEGMENT 2: 15-18 YEAR OLDS

### Likely hooks
- **Student loan / uni debt relevance** — 17-year-olds facing £45k debt want to understand what they're signing up for
- **Real money earned** — 66% have part-time jobs; budgeting their own wages is immediately relevant
- **Credibility signals** — they're skeptical of finfluencers (39% feel unsafe from scams); a certified, trustworthy source has appeal
- **Quick wins they can use now** — "understand your payslip", "know if you're being ripped off" beats abstract theory

### Likely blockers
- **Cartoon aesthetic = instant cringe** — monsters and "adventure" language will trigger rejection before they reach content
- **Feels like it's for younger kids** — "my parents would love this" is a warning sign, not a compliment
- **Too much theory, not enough practical** — they want to know what to DO, not just definitions
- **Forced gamification** — points and streaks are fine, but if it feels manipulative they'll notice and resent it

### Top 3 Recommendations for This Segment
1. **Create a 'Money Skills' mode** — subdued aesthetic, neutral language, same curriculum but without the cartoon wrapper
2. **Lead with real-world scenarios** — student loans, part-time job budgeting, first credit card — topics in their near future
3. **Respect their intelligence** — explain things clearly without dumbing down; they can handle complexity in accessible language

---

## SEGMENT 3: 18-25 YEAR OLDS (NON-GRADUATE)

### Likely hooks
- **Pensions and ISAs made accessible** — 60% claim to understand pensions but only 10% pass basic tests; they know they need help but don't know where to start
- **Practical budgeting for real bills** — rent takes 47% of income; they're managing money already and want to do it better
- **No student debt content** — most financial literacy assumes the graduate path; content that acknowledges non-graduates is rare and valuable
- **Mobile-first fintech positioning** — 62% prefer fintech to traditional banks; an app that "gets" digital money feels credible

### Likely blockers
- **Cartoon monsters are embarrassing** — they're adults with rent to pay; anything childish undermines trust
- **Content for richer people** — talk about investing thousands or ISA limits and you've lost someone on £23k
- **Lecturing about saving** — they know they should save; they can't afford to; don't make them feel guilty
- **Assumes everyone went to uni** — references to student loans, graduate schemes, or "your first job after uni" feel exclusionary

### Top 3 Recommendations for This Segment
1. **Strip back aesthetic completely** — clean, fintech-style interface; let the content earn respect without visual barriers
2. **Acknowledge economic reality** — content should speak to "managing on £25k" not aspirational wealth-building
3. **Make pensions/investing feel achievable** — "even £20/month" framing; remove the sense that this is only for people with money to spare

# 5. CROSS-SEGMENT SYNTHESIS

## Which segment has the strongest product-market fit?
- **12-15 year olds (Strongest fit):** Monster mechanic, character scenarios, and Level 1-2 content topics align well with this group's life stage and interests
- **18-25s (Best content, mismatched packaging):** Levels 5-8 cover genuinely useful topics but the cartoon aesthetic undermines credibility for this age group
- **15-18s (Highest risk of rejection):** Without meaningfully different tone and visual treatment, this segment will likely dismiss the app before reaching content
- If a single launch design is necessary, recommend optimising for 12-15s (where fit is closest) and building outward

## 'Dirty Average' Warning for User Testing
- The biggest analytical risk is averaging reactions across segments
- "60% liked the monsters" hides that 90% of 12-year-olds loved them and 80% of 17-year-olds found them embarrassing
- Every insight must be tagged by segment — what works for one group may actively repel another
- This segment-specific insight is the most valuable thing this research can deliver

## The Strategic Question This Review Raises
- Should Money Monsters serve all three segments with one experience, or differentiate the wrapper?
- The curriculum can span all ages — it's well-designed for that
- The experience around it (tone, visuals, onboarding) probably needs variation
- Two tone modes ('Adventure' vs 'Money Skills'), age-adapted copy, and scaled vocabulary would address most of the gap without requiring separate apps

# 8. FINAL ASSESSMENT

## Who will this app most likely win?
**12-15 year olds** — especially through school or parent-directed adoption. The monster mechanic, character scenarios, and early-level content align best with this age group.

## Who will it most likely lose without changes?
**15-18 year olds.** The tone and aesthetic will trigger 'cringe' rejection before they reach any content. A 'Money Skills' tone mode that backgrounds the monster aesthetic could unlock this segment.

## What single change would most improve success?
**Add a clear value proposition before sign-up.** Users currently commit email, name, DOB, and parental consent before seeing any value. One screen explaining "what this is and why it matters" would significantly reduce early abandonment.

## Where does it outperform alternatives?
**Structured, progressive curriculum.** This is genuinely hard to replicate on TikTok or with AI. But this advantage is invisible until well into the experience — it needs to lead the proposition, not be discovered after 10 minutes.

## Is this launchable?
**Yes — as a 'Fix & Go'.** The core architecture (curriculum, quiz placement, streak, monsters) is sound. Onboarding, proposition clarity, QA polish, and age-tone calibration need treatment as launch-critical. Without those fixes, onboarding drop-off is a real risk.

## What should user testing focus on?
Not "Do users like what we have?" but **"What would make users choose this?"**
- Test the value proposition
- Test age-differentiated experiences (full vs stripped-back aesthetic)
- Test competitive comparison directly: "Why would you use this vs asking ChatGPT?"
- Contrast reveals truth — show alternatives
