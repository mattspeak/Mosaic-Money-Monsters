# Money Monsters App Review - Organised Analysis

*Based on Matt's voice note analysis of the Figma board*

---

## 1. Proposition & Landing Page

**What users first encounter - does it land?**

- Benefits not clear above the fold - what does the user *get* from this?
- Jumps too quickly into "reasons to believe" (e.g., Certified...) before establishing value
- "Story-driven lessons" promised but not evident in the actual modules
- Could be explained visually as well as with words

**Example benefit framing:**
- "Get your kid to better manage their pocket money"
- "Ensure your child is thinking about the future as well as today"

---

## 2. Onboarding & User-Centricity

**The app doesn't start with the user**

- Opens with "Don't worry if these seem challenging" - could frame more positively
- Should start simple: "How old are you?" then tailor questions to that age to avoid older children feeling patronised
- Questions focus on *money in abstract* rather than *the user's relationship with money* - should be more relatable
- "This is only important for adults and full-time jobs" framing feels patronising

**Hypothetical vs. Relatable framing:**
| Current (Hypothetical) | Better (User-Centred) |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| "Once you earn money, you should spend it all immediately. True or false?" | "You've earned some pocket money. What do you do next?" |
| Match Japanese Yen to Japan | Start with familiar currencies, build to unfamiliar |

---

## 3. Information Architecture & Learning Structure

**Need overarching organisation before diving into detail**

- Universal themes that make sense: **Earning, Spending, Saving, Borrowing, Protecting, Growing**
- Current monster themes less intuitive: Foresight, Credit Tracking, Currency
- Single monsters cover too many concepts (e.g., Budgie has spending, saving, AND earning)
- Sub-concepts needed under each big topic

**Monster connection unclear:**
- Only understood later that monsters connect to themes
- This mapping should be immediately obvious

---

## 4. Gamification Clarity

**What's the point of playing?**

- No explanation of what coins earn you
- No explanation of what levelling up achieves
- Users need to understand the reward loop upfront

---

## 5. Visual Design & Age-Appropriateness

**Target audience lives in a visual, technicolour digital world**

- Currently very text-heavy
- Sparse use of icons and imagery
- Example: "Save for a New Gaming Console" has no picture of a gaming console
- Visuals should change in relation to speech bubbles during lessons

**What's working:**
- Lesson intro screens with large image + short speech bubbles
- Progressive reveal via "Next" button breaks down topics well

---

## 6. Language & Reading Level

**Check age-appropriateness of vocabulary**

- "Trailblazer" - potentially difficult for 12-year-olds
- "Time horizon" - complex terminology
- Definitions could be framed in context of the young learner

---

## 7. Feedback & Encouragement

**Wrong answer handling needs positive framing**

| Current | More Encouraging |
|---------|------------------|
| "The answer is not complete" | "Nearly there!" |
| "Not this time" | "Good try - have another go" |

---

## Summary: Key Hypotheses for User Testing

### To Validate:
1. **Proposition clarity** - Do users understand what they get before signing up?
2. **User-centricity** - Do questions/scenarios feel relevant to *their* life?
3. **Information architecture** - Can users navigate and understand the monster/topic structure?
4. **Visual engagement** - Is text-heaviness a barrier for this audience?
5. **Gamification motivation** - Do users understand and care about coins/levels?
6. **Language accessibility** - Are terms age-appropriate across all bands?
7. **Emotional tone** - Does feedback feel encouraging or deflating?

### Areas of Potential Strength:
- Lesson intro screen format (image + speech bubbles + progressive reveal)
- Core concept of "taming money monsters" (needs clearer execution)
