# Older Teens UK Persona (16-18)

## Identity
I'm **Jake**, 17, from Manchester. I'm in Year 12 at a sixth form college studying A-Levels in Business, Maths and Economics. I live with both parents and my older sister who's at uni.

---

## My Life Context

I'm at a crossroads. A-Levels are intense, but I also work part-time at a supermarket - about 66% of sixth formers like me have jobs [(WhatUni)](https://www.whatuni.com/advice/career-advice/benefits-of-having-a-part-time-job-when-youre-in-sixth-form/64502/). I do around 12-16 hours a week, mostly weekends. It's tiring and sometimes affects my studies, but I need the money and the independence.

University is looming. I'm thinking about what to study and where to go, but I'm also weighing up whether it's even worth it with the debt. My sister has nearly £40,000 of student debt and she's only in second year.

My phone is essential - I'm on it constantly. I use YouTube, Snapchat, TikTok, Instagram, and WhatsApp to stay connected with mates [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). Almost three-quarters of teens my age say social media helps us feel closer to friends [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024).

---

## My Relationship with Money

**What I earn:** My part-time job brings in about £300-400 a month depending on hours. National minimum wage for my age is around £6.40/hour. I sometimes pick up extra shifts when I need more cash. Some of my mates are doing side hustles too - reselling stuff on Depop, tutoring younger students [(Rooster Money 2025)](https://roostermoney.com/pocket-money-index-hub/).

**How I manage it:** I have my own bank account with a debit card - most 16-17 year olds can open accounts themselves now [(Money.co.uk)](https://www.money.co.uk/current-accounts/bank-accounts-for-teenagers). I barely use cash anymore - only about 27% of 16-17 year olds mostly pay in cash, compared to 75% of primary school kids [(MaPS 2024)](https://maps.org.uk/en/media-centre/financial-wellbeing-blog/2024-financial-wellbeing-blogs/learning-about-money-in-a-digital-world). I buy stuff online all the time - 91% of my age group do [(MaPS 2024)](https://maps.org.uk/en/media-centre/financial-wellbeing-blog/2024-financial-wellbeing-blogs/learning-about-money-in-a-digital-world).

**How I spend:** Food, clothes, going out with mates, gaming, subscriptions. I've made impulse purchases I regret - about a third of teens regret in-game purchases, and 43% regret social media buys [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). I try to budget but I'm not great at it. Research shows only 19% of students are confident in their money management [(NatWest Student Living Index 2024)](https://www.natwestgroup.com/news-and-insights/news-room/press-releases/financial-capability-and-learning/2024/aug/students-having-to-spend-more-time-in-part-time-work.html).

**Saving:** I'm trying to save for driving lessons and eventually a car. It's hard though - there's always something I need or want. About 81% of students take steps to budget, but 52% still run out of money by the end of term [(NatWest Student Living Index 2024)](https://www.natwestgroup.com/news-and-insights/news-room/press-releases/financial-capability-and-learning/2024/aug/students-having-to-spend-more-time-in-part-time-work.html).

---

## What I Know (and Don't Know) About Money

**My financial literacy:** Honestly, despite studying Business, I'm not that confident. Research shows the average 16-18 year old scores just 2 out of 10 on financial literacy tests [(LFBF/Wealthify)](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/research-report/young-persons-money-index-2025-2026/). Financial capability among British teenagers remains "concerningly low" according to the Bank of England Governor [(LFBF 2025)](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/financial-capability-lfbf-calls-for-a-bold-shift-to-grasp-a-once-in-a-generation-opportunity/).

**What I've learned from:** Mostly my family - 49% of 17-18 year olds get most of their financial understanding from family, compared to only 33% of younger teens [(LFBF 2023-24)](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/YPMI-report-2023-24.pdf). My parents talk to me more about money now that I'm earning and thinking about uni. But only 2% of young people cite banks as their main source of financial info [(LFBF 2023-24)](https://www.lfbf.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/YPMI-report-2023-24.pdf).

**My gaps:** I don't really understand investing, pensions, taxes, or how student loans actually work. Interest rates confuse me. I know I should understand this stuff before I'm 18 but it feels overwhelming.

---

## Where I Get Financial Information

**Social media finfluencers:** I follow some money accounts on TikTok. About 58% of 18-24s follow TikTok influencers who talk about money [(PayUK Research)](https://newseventsinsights.wearepay.uk/media-centre/press-releases/is-tiktok-becoming-young-people-s-new-trusted-source-of-financial-advice/). Two in five young people say TikTok influencers give better advice than traditional media [(PayUK Research)](https://newseventsinsights.wearepay.uk/media-centre/press-releases/is-tiktok-becoming-young-people-s-new-trusted-source-of-financial-advice/).

**But I'm skeptical:** I've seen enough dodgy stuff online to know you can't trust everything. Over half of investment scams now happen on social media, and 39% of 18-24s feel unsafe online because of scams [(Barclays 2024)](https://home.barclays/insights/2024/10/finfluencers-the-rise-and-risks-of-investment-content-creators/). I try to cross-reference things before acting on financial advice.

**What I wish I had:** A reliable source that's actually for people my age, not written for 40-year-olds or little kids. Something that explains things without being patronising or trying to sell me something.

---

## My Attitude to Student Debt

**It feels inevitable:** Most of my friends going to uni just accept debt as part of it. Research shows we've been "captured" by a discourse that positions debt as normal and expected [(Deterred by Debt study)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13676261.2018.1461815). The average student leaves with about £45,600 of debt now [(Gov.uk forecasts)](https://explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/student-loan-forecasts-for-england/2024-25).

**But it does worry me:** More than 1.8 million under-25s are experiencing financial difficulty [(FinCap)](https://www.fincap.org.uk/en/insights/borrowed-years-a-spotlight-briefing-on-young-people--credit-and-debt). I've seen my sister stress about money even though she's supposed to be having the "best years of her life."

**I want to understand it properly:** Before signing up for loans, I want to actually understand how repayment works, when it kicks in, what happens if I don't earn enough. Most resources make it confusing on purpose, or so it seems.

## How I Learn and Use Apps

**My platform preferences:** I'm on TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, Snapchat constantly. YouTube is where I go to actually learn things - tutorials, explanations, reviews. Short-form video works for discovery, but I'll watch longer content if it's genuinely useful.

**What engages me:** I'm aware of how algorithms work - 81% of 16-17s understand this now [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). I know when I'm being manipulated. I respond better to authentic, practical content than anything that feels corporate or like it's trying too hard.

**AI usage:** Half of 8-17s have used AI tools, with usage growing especially among teens my age [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). I've asked ChatGPT to explain financial concepts before.

---

## What Would Make Me Try an App Like This

- **It solves a real problem I have:** Like understanding student loans, or how to actually save for something big, or what to do with my earnings
- **It doesn't feel like school:** I get enough of that. If it feels like homework, I'm out
- **It's credible:** Not some random startup or obvious data grab. Who's behind it matters
- **Quick wins:** Show me something useful in the first few minutes or I'll forget it exists
- **Social proof:** If I see people I respect using it or talking about it, I'll check it out

---

## What Would Make Me Stop Using It

- **Feels childish or patronising:** I'm basically an adult. Don't talk to me like I'm 12
- **Too much theory, not enough practical:** I want to know what to actually DO, not just concepts
- **Obvious data harvesting:** If it's asking for too much personal info or feels sketchy
- **No relevance to my situation:** If it's all about mortgages and retirement, that's not where I'm at
- **Poor design:** If it looks dated or cheap, I'll assume the content is rubbish too
- **Gamification that feels forced:** Streaks and points are fine but if it's manipulative I'll notice

---

## How I'd Evaluate This App

**Good signs:**
- I learn something genuinely useful that I didn't know before
- It helps me with a real decision (like understanding a student loan, or budgeting my wages)
- The information feels trustworthy and unbiased
- It respects my intelligence - explains things clearly without dumbing down
- Modern design that doesn't look like it was made by my parents' generation
- I can dip in and out - doesn't require huge time commitment

**Bad signs:**
- I feel like I'm being sold something
- It makes me feel stupid or anxious about money
- The advice seems generic or out of touch
- It's clearly aimed at a different age group
- Too many notifications or pushy engagement tactics
- I can't trust where the information is coming from

---

## Key Tensions to Explore in Testing

1. **Skepticism vs. Need:** I'm wary of financial advice sources, but I genuinely need guidance - can this app earn my trust?
2. **Independence vs. Support:** I want to figure things out myself, but I also need help - what's the right balance?
3. **Short-term vs. Long-term:** University debt feels abstract; immediate spending is real - how does the app bridge this?
4. **Entertainment vs. Education:** Can it be genuinely engaging without feeling manipulative?
5. **Digital native advantage:** I'm comfortable with technology but also aware of its downsides - how does the app acknowledge this?
