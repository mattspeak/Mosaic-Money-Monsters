# UK Persona Research Plan

## Objective
Create three evidence-based UK personas for Money Monsters user testing, representing hypothetical users who will evaluate the financial literacy app prototype.

## Segments

| Segment | Age | Key Characteristics (Hypothesis) |
|---------|-----|----------------------------------|
| Young Teens | 13-15 | Forming habits, need clarity/scaffolding, limited real money experience |
| Older Teens | 16-18 | Real-money relevance emerging, higher scepticism, approaching independence |
| Young Adults | 18-25 | Varying independence levels, increasing responsibility, some financial experience |

## Research Log

### Session 1: Young Teens (13-15)
*Completed: 4 Feb 2026*

**Key findings:**
- Only 33% of children recall learning about money in school and finding it useful (MaPS 2022)
- 47% have received meaningful financial education - unchanged since 2019 (MaPS)
- 66% of kids worry about money (GoHenry)
- Average weekly pocket money £3.85, rising with age to £8.31 at 17 (Rooster Money 2025)
- 13-14 year olds spend ~4 hours online daily (Ofcom 2024)
- Only 10% of 6-18s spending in cash - digital-first generation (GoHenry)
- Gamification shown to improve engagement - 80% of Duolingo users cite it as reason for enjoyment
- Main barrier: lack of resources that engage learners; worksheets don't work (Just Finance Foundation)

---

### Session 2: Older Teens (16-18)
*Completed: 4 Feb 2026*

**Key findings:**
- Financial capability among 15-18s remains "concerningly low" (LFBF 2025)
- Average 16-18 year old scores just 2/10 on financial literacy tests (Wealthify/LFBF)
- 66% of sixth formers have part-time jobs (various)
- Only 27% of 16-17s mostly pay in cash; 91% have bought online (MaPS)
- 58% of 18-24s follow TikTok finfluencers; 46% say they influenced financial decisions (PayUK)
- 52% of investment scams now occur on social media (Barclays)
- Student debt normalised - average £45,600 on graduation (Gov.uk)
- More than 1.8 million under-25s experiencing financial difficulty (FinCap)
- 81% of older students take steps to budget; 52% run out of money by end of term (NatWest)
- Family main source of financial info for 17-18s (49% vs 33% for younger) (LFBF)

---

### Session 3: Young Adults - Graduate (18-25)
*Completed: 4 Feb 2026*

**Key findings:**
- Only 9% of 18-24s pass financial literacy quiz vs 34% of 55+ (Shepherds Friendly 2025)
- Only 26% of 18-21s received any financial education at school (Santander 2025)
- 75% of Gen Z would have "mental breakdown" over unexpected £300 expense (Riseup UK)
- 60% living paycheck to paycheck (Deloitte Global Survey)
- Only 25% of Gen Z feel "very confident" managing finances vs 47% of Baby Boomers (Pay.UK)
- 68% feel anxiety in days leading up to payday (various)
- 57% of 22-24 year olds financially precarious (RSA)
- 16-24s spend 47% of gross income on rent vs 33% national average (Youth Select Committee)
- 62% of 18-24s prefer fintech providers over traditional banks (Comscore)
- 51% "obsessed" with checking financial apps; 63% of Gen Z (FIS Research)
- 19% of Gen Z using Buy Now Pay Later (Pay.UK)
- Motivation rather than literacy is primary driver of financial engagement (academic research)
- Transition points are key "teachable moments" for intervention (FinCap)

---

### Session 4: Young Adults - Non-Graduate (18-25)
*Completed: 4 Feb 2026*

**Key findings:**
- Non-graduate median salary £30,500 vs £42,000 for graduates (Gov.uk 2024)
- Non-graduate employment rate 68% vs 87.6% for graduates (Gov.uk 2024)
- No student debt, but no graduate salary premium either
- 16-24s 2.2x more likely to face severe work insecurity than older workers (TUC)
- 13% of 16-24s on zero-hours contracts vs 2.4% of over-25s (TUC)
- Two-thirds of parents now believe degree delivers less value than it used to (Grant Thornton)
- Higher apprentices now out-earn average graduates (Centre for Social Justice)
- 53% of Gen Z haven't started pension saving; average expects to start at 34 (Penfold)
- 60% of Gen Z say they understand pensions, but only 10% pass basic tests (PPI)
- 49% of Gen Z prefer to "live for today than plan for tomorrow" (Standard Life)
- 82% of 18-24s use credit cards/overdrafts for monthly expenses (Credit Connect)
- Nearly half of Gen Z don't believe state pension will exist when they retire (PPI)

## Cross-Cutting Insights for Money Monsters Testing

### Universal Findings Across All Segments
1. **Financial education in schools has failed** - Only 26-33% report useful money learning at school
2. **Family is the primary source** - Parents/family remain the main financial educators
3. **Digital-first money behaviours** - Cash usage declining rapidly, especially with age
4. **Worry about money is widespread** - 66% of kids worry; rises to anxiety disorder levels in young adults
5. **Gamification works** - When done well, significantly improves engagement and retention

### Key Differences by Segment

| Factor | Young Teens (13-15) | Older Teens (16-18) | Young Adults (18-25) |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Money source | Pocket money (~£4-6/wk) | Part-time jobs (66% have them) | Salary, living paycheck to paycheck |
| Key concern | Abstract/doesn't feel relevant | Student debt looming | Surviving cost of living |
| Primary need | Foundational concepts, habit formation | Understanding debt, budgeting earned income | Practical money management, reducing anxiety |
| Barrier to engagement | Feels like school homework | Skepticism of sources | No appetite to engage, talking about money "isn't fun" |
| What works | Gamification, quick wins, visual/interactive | Credible, practical, acknowledges skepticism | Solves immediate problems, doesn't increase anxiety |

### Graduate vs Non-Graduate Young Adults

| Factor | Graduate | Non-Graduate |
|--------|----------|--------------|
| Median salary | £42,000 | £30,500 |
| Employment rate | 87.6% | 68% |
| Student debt | ~£45,000 | None |
| Disposable income | Lower due to loan repayments | Higher despite lower gross |
| Job security | More stable | 2.2x more likely to be insecure |
| Key financial concern | Managing debt + living costs | Lower income ceiling, insecurity |
| Content needed | Student loan optimisation, investing | Budgeting on lower income, career progression |

### Implications for Money Monsters

1. **Differentiate by age** - Content and tone must shift significantly across segments
2. **Avoid school-like experience** - Worksheets, long text, passive learning will cause dropout
3. **Quick value demonstration** - Show usefulness in first few minutes
4. **Emotional safety** - Especially for young adults, avoid increasing financial anxiety
5. **Credibility matters** - Older teens and young adults are skeptical; who's behind it?
6. **Mobile-first, short-form** - Match existing digital behaviours
7. **Connect to real situations** - Abstract concepts need grounding in lived experience
