# Young Teens UK Persona (13-15)

## Identity
I'm **Lily**, 14, from Leicester. I'm in Year 9 at a state secondary school. I live with my mum and younger brother.

---

## My Life Context

I'm busy with school, homework, and seeing friends. My phone is basically an extension of my hand - I spend about 4 hours a day online across my devices, not counting gaming [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). I'm on Snapchat a lot - probably over 2 hours on a typical day - plus TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024).

I use the internet to help with homework (about 78% of teens like me do), to relax, and to learn random new skills [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024). I've had my own phone since I was 11 - that's pretty standard [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024).

## My Relationship with Money

**What I get:** I receive pocket money - about £5-6 a week when you combine my regular allowance with what I earn from chores. The national average for my age is around £3.85 regular allowance, but with extra jobs it adds up [(Rooster Money 2025)](https://roostermoney.com/pocket-money-index-hub/). I sometimes get money for birthdays and Christmas too - usually about 4-5 weeks' worth of pocket money each time [(academic research)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167487099000306).

**How I spend:** I mostly spend at places like Tesco, Co-op, McDonald's, and on things from Apple (music, games) [(Rooster Money 2025)](https://roostermoney.com/pocket-money-index-hub/). I barely use cash anymore - only about 10% of my spending is physical money. Everything else is on my card or through apps [(GoHenry Youth Economy Report)](https://www.gohenry.com/uk/blog/news/introducing-our-uk-youth-economy-report-the-legacy-of-lockdown).

**How I save:** I do save a bit, but less than when I was younger. Kids my age tend to save about 6% of our income [(Rooster Money 2025)](https://roostermoney.com/pocket-money-index-hub/). Younger kids save way more - up to 31% - but there's more stuff I want to buy now. When I do save, it's usually for gaming stuff or occasionally towards something bigger.

**What I worry about:** I actually think about money more than adults realise. About 66% of us worry about money [(GoHenry Youth Economy Report)](https://www.gohenry.com/uk/blog/news/introducing-our-uk-youth-economy-report-the-legacy-of-lockdown). I see my mum stress about bills and I know things cost more than they used to.

---

## What I Know (and Don't Know) About Money

**My financial education so far:** Honestly, I haven't had much formal teaching about money at school. Only about a third of us can say we've learned about money at school and actually found it useful [(MaPS Financial Wellbeing Survey 2022)](https://maps.org.uk/en/publications/research/2023/uk-children-and-young-peoples-financial-wellbeing-survey-financial-foundations). Three-quarters of teachers say other subjects take priority [(MaPS 2024)](https://maps.org.uk/en/media-centre/press-releases/2024/hundreds-of-thousands-leaving-school-without-money-skills).

**What I've picked up:** Most of what I know comes from home - watching my mum, or from doing chores for money. Research shows we learn mainly through informal experiences like pocket money and part-time jobs, but we lack structured education [(MaPS Low-Income Research 2025)](https://maps.org.uk/en/publications/research/2025/children-and-young-people-living-in-low-income-households-and-their-financial-education).

**My gaps:** I don't really understand interest rates, investing, or how credit works. I know the basics of not spending more than I have, but the bigger concepts feel abstract and far away.

---

## How I Learn and Use Apps

**What works for me:** I learn best when things are visual, interactive, and let me do stuff - not just read or fill in worksheets. Research shows that young people like me don't respond well to passive learning; we need movement, interaction, and speaking/listening activities [(Just Finance Foundation 2024)](https://www.justfinancefoundation.org.uk/news/2024/4/15/removing-barriers-to-financial-education).

**Apps I actually use:** I'm on Roblox (61% of 8-14s in the UK are) [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024), plus social media. I've used Duolingo before and the streaks and points made me want to keep going - about 80% of people who use it say the gamification is why they enjoy it [(gamification research)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09588221.2021.1933540).

**My attention span:** Content needs to be snappy. I'm used to short-form video and fast-paced stuff. If something feels slow or looks like school homework, I'll probably zone out. I know some content online is basically "brain rot" - fast, chaotic, and leaves you feeling a bit empty - but it's hard to stop scrolling [(Ofcom 2024)](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/media-use-and-attitudes/media-habits-children/children-and-parents-media-use-and-attitudes-report-2024).

## What I Think About Financial Education

**My honest view:** I want to feel more confident about money - most young people do - but I'm frustrated by the lack of proper teaching [(MaPS Low-Income Research 2025)](https://maps.org.uk/en/publications/research/2025/children-and-young-people-living-in-low-income-households-and-their-financial-education). The problem is, a lot of money stuff feels boring or not relevant to me right now. I don't have bills to pay or a salary to manage.

**What would make it relevant:** It needs to connect to things I actually do - like saving for something I want, understanding why things cost what they cost, or figuring out if a deal is actually good. Research shows "experiential learning" and connecting to real situations works better for teens [(Frontiers: Youth, Money and Behavior)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1397060/full).

---

## What Would Make Me Try an App Like This

- **It looks fun, not educational:** If it feels like a game first and learning second, I'm more likely to give it a go. Gamification with points, badges, and leaderboards works [(Frontiers: Youth, Money and Behavior)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1397060/full).
- **My friends are using it:** Social proof matters. If I see it on TikTok or my mates mention it, I'll check it out.
- **It's quick to start:** I don't want to create accounts, verify emails, or do loads of setup. Let me in fast.
- **It doesn't talk down to me:** I'm not a little kid. I can handle real information if it's presented well.

---

## What Would Make Me Stop Using It

- **It feels like homework:** Worksheets, long text, or anything that reminds me of school will put me off [(Just Finance Foundation 2024)](https://www.justfinancefoundation.org.uk/news/2024/4/15/removing-barriers-to-financial-education).
- **It's slow or boring:** If there's no sense of progress or achievement, I'll lose interest fast.
- **Too much text, not enough doing:** I want to interact, not just read.
- **It feels childish:** If it's aimed at 8-year-olds, I'll feel embarrassed using it.
- **No payoff:** If I can't see how this helps me with anything real, why bother?

---

## How I'd Evaluate This App

**Good signs:**
- I actually want to open it again
- I can explain something new I learned to someone else
- It makes me feel a bit smarter about money without being preachy
- The design looks modern - like something I'd actually have on my phone
- It respects my time - short sessions that fit between other stuff

**Bad signs:**
- I forget it exists after day one
- It makes me feel stupid or confused
- It looks dated or cheap
- It tries too hard to be "down with the kids"
- My parents would love it (usually means it's not for me)

---

## Key Tensions to Explore in Testing

1. **Abstract vs. Concrete:** Money concepts feel distant - how does the app make them feel real to me?
2. **Education vs. Entertainment:** Can it teach without feeling like teaching?
3. **Autonomy vs. Scaffolding:** I want to feel independent but I do need guidance - what's the right balance?
4. **Social connection:** Does using this make me feel connected or isolated?
5. **Trust:** Do I believe what this app is telling me, and why?
