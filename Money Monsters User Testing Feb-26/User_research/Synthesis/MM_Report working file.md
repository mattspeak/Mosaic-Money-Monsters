# 2. Who We Spoke To

## 2.1 Participant Overview

We spoke to 16 young people across the UK, recruited across three age-defined segments. All fieldwork was conducted virtually between 20–27 February 2026, using 60-minute screen-share and think-aloud sessions. Participants were recruited to be mainstream — not finance enthusiasts — with a deliberate skew toward lower financial literacy and lower household income. Finance-industry households and those with any formal financial training were excluded.

| Segment | n | Age range | Basis for inclusion |
|---------|---|-----------|----------------------|
| Young Teens (YT) | 7 | 12–14 | School-age; income from pocket money and gifts; no financial independence |
| Older Teens (OT) | 3 | 15–17 | Approaching or beginning first earnings; facing near-term transition decisions |
| Young Adults (YA) | 6 | 18–25 | In early employment or higher education; varying degrees of financial independence |

Participants spanned UK. None had completed structured financial education beyond standard school curricula, and most reported that school financial content was minimal or unhelpful. The sample reflects young people who are making real money decisions without adequate support — the target audience Money Monsters is designed to serve.

## 2.2 Participant Context and Money Habits

### 2.2.1 Money in their life — income, spending, saving

The financial lives of our three segments differ substantially in scale, complexity, and the nature of the decisions they face. Understanding this context is essential for evaluating where the app needs to meet each group.

**Young Teens** operate in a financially small but emotionally significant world. Income is irregular and gift-dependent: pocket money from parents (typically £5–15 per week or fortnight), cash from grandparents at birthdays and Christmas, and occasional earnings from household chores. Lottie (13) receives £10 every two weeks from her grandmother. Isaac (12) relies almost entirely on birthday and Christmas money. Sam (14) gets £10 monthly from parents plus what he earns from chores. The financial challenge at this life stage is not budgeting in any formal sense — it is impulse control. Nearly every Young Teen described spending money quickly after receiving it: Lottie describes herself as "very impulse driven" and part of a "family of spenders"; Zain (13) spent £11 at a shop "without thinking" while out with friends; Sam's money "only lasts very briefly after receiving it." Yet several had succeeded in saving toward concrete goals — Sam had saved for a PC over two years; Isaac had saved £100 for football boots — demonstrating that motivation-based saving is within reach, even if hard to sustain.

**Older Teens** are beginning to encounter real money. Levi (17) earns £12/hour on a zero-hours events contract, working 10–20 hours per month. Ovie (15) generates income selling vintage clothes on Vinted. Aiden (15) earns pocket money plus birthday and Christmas cash, and is actively looking for part-time work. At this stage, money is no longer purely about daily spending — Aiden successfully saved for both a PC and DJ decks, demonstrating genuine goal-directed discipline. But impulse tension remains. Aiden articulates it directly: *"I see money and I want to spend it."* This segment is also starting to think beyond the immediate: Levi is weighing gap year plans; Aiden is considering apprenticeships over university to avoid student debt. Financial decisions are beginning to connect to life trajectory.

**Young Adults** are navigating real financial complexity with varied degrees of support. Grace (19) is a second-year student managing a student loan (paid three times a year), part-time bar work, and monthly parental support. Mohammad (23) lives independently on close-to-minimum-wage earnings after a computer science degree, managing rent as his primary financial decision. Zain (20) lives at home while studying, has accumulated savings over three years of part-time work, and bought a car outright rather than financing it. Enoch (18) is a full-time college student working weekends, living "paycheck to paycheck." Mia (25) owns a home with her partner, has a mortgage, and is saving £3,000 for a yoga certification. Althea (19) is an international student in London, managing daily expenses from tutoring income while parents cover tuition and rent. For this segment, the financial stakes are real and consequential — not theoretical.

### 2.2.2 How they manage their money

**Young Teens** primarily use basic digital tools where they have access. Lottie and Sam both use Monzo, Norah uses a NatWest Rooster account, Hugo uses Royal Bank of Scotland, and Olivia has a single standard bank account. Isaac is the exception: he keeps physical cash in a bedroom "money pot" and has a bank account controlled by his parents. "Managing money" for most Young Teens means monitoring a balance and self-regulating when it gets low — not budgeting in any structured way. Pot features (available in Monzo and Revolut) are known but used inconsistently.

**Older Teens** have moved to adult current accounts. Levi uses Nationwide, Aiden uses NatWest Adapt, Ovie uses Revolut. All three check their balances regularly but maintain no formal budgets. Ovie uses Revolut's automatic savings sweep, which removes a small amount from her main pot weekly — the closest any Older Teen participant comes to structured saving behaviour.

**Young Adults** show the widest range of approaches, and almost universally report that existing systems are insufficient. Mohammad manages finances "in his head," with rough weekly mental calculations and bank statement checks 1–2 times a week — having abandoned formal budgeting apps as "tedious." Grace monitors her banking app daily and uses a separate savings account for top-up saving. Zain (20) uses spreadsheets for major financial decisions (he built one when researching and buying his car). Althea writes down spending informally but acknowledges she doesn't follow through. Enoch splits money across three accounts (Monzo current, Monzo business, Revolut) to enforce separation and reduce overspending. Mia uses the Plum app for automated saving but finds it doesn't provide enough investment guidance or effective friction against impulsive convenience spending. The consistent thread: current tools are either too passive, too manual, or too easy to override.

## 2.3 What They Know About Money

### 2.3.1 Confidence and knowledge self-rating

Confidence was gathered through a relative self-rating (compared to peers), not an objective test. The numbers tell a partial story — the reasoning is more revealing.

**Young Teens** generally rate themselves in the 5–7 range, with one outlier: Hugo (13) rates himself 9/10, noting that his friends "know nothing." Olivia (14) rates herself 7/10, attributing peers' higher financial literacy to greater parental involvement in household financial decisions. Norah (13) rates herself just 4/10 — she knows basics but struggles with deeper questions. The reality across the segment is broadly consistent: Young Teens understand that saving is good, know the mechanics of spending, and have a general sense of income — but have no working knowledge of financial products, concepts like interest, or what financial life looks like when they're older.

**Older Teens** self-rate slightly higher (6–7), with meaningful nuance. Aiden (15) rates himself 7/10, drawing on school life skills lessons and family messaging — but explicitly acknowledges saving execution as his weak point despite understanding the principles. Ovie (15) rates herself 6–7, noting most peers are at a similar level because money is barely taught. Levi (17) represents his financial confidence with a grimacing emoji: not about capability with basics, but specifically about feeling "on edge" in conversations with financially knowledgeable people.

**Young Adults** split into two distinct groups. At one end, Mohammad (23) rates himself 8/10, attributing it to weeks of self-directed research — yet his credit card minimum-payment mistake demonstrates that confidence can outrun actual comprehension. Zain (20) rates himself 6/10, describing "a little bit above average" based on three years of working and saving, with clear acknowledged gaps in investment, ISAs, and making savings work harder. Grace (19) rates herself 4/10; Mia (25) rates herself 5/10. Althea (19) describes herself simply as having a "helpless" relationship with money — caught in a cycle of avoidance and end-of-month panic.

### 2.3.2 Where they learned about money

The dominant pattern across all segments is informal, reactive, and family-led. Formal financial education plays a peripheral role.

**Parents and family** are the primary channel for almost every participant. Sam learned about emergency saving from parental conversations. Hugo's father discusses investing. Norah primarily learns from her mother about saving and taxes. Aiden receives consistent saving messages from family. Ovie's parents invest in stocks and have shaped her understanding of what's possible. For Young Adults, family remains important even as other channels diversify: Mia's aunties share financial knowledge actively; Althea's mother is her primary financial role model, with her sister as a practical day-to-day resource; Grace relies exclusively on friends and family for financial guidance.

**School is consistently described as inadequate.** Across all three segments, participants describe school financial content as either absent, too academic, or basic enough to be redundant with what parents already taught. Sam's Year 8 lesson on bank accounts simply repeated parental teaching. Althea describes school coverage as "more like economics rather than actual finance." Aiden received life skills content but characterizes it as covering money's importance in general terms without practical depth. Several participants report not that school content was forgotten, but that it was never meaningfully provided.

**Self-directed digital learning** becomes increasingly important for Older Teens and Young Adults. Enoch (18) learns primarily from TikTok, YouTube, and AI. Mohammad (23) describes an intensive "push" approach — researching deeply when specific situations demand it, via YouTube, Google, and ChatGPT. Zain (20, YA) passively absorbs content from Martin Lewis, Money Saving Expert, and social media feeds. Hugo (13) already watches TikTok content about making money as a kid. The quality and relevance varies widely — Mohammad notes that YouTube financial content often assumes income levels higher than most young people have, making it practically useless for their actual situation.

### 2.3.3 What they wish they understood better

Knowledge gaps follow life stage with notable precision.

**Young Teens** want to understand the adult money concepts that surface in their immediate environment — not because they need them urgently, but because not understanding feels excluding. Isaac (12) specifically mentions mortgages, prompted by his parents' recent house move. Norah (13) overhears adult financial conversations and wants to feel less lost. Lottie (13) identified currency exchange, prompted by the prospect of foreign travel. These aren't ambitious knowledge goals — they're about belonging in conversations, not feeling left behind.

**Older Teens** want practical knowledge for decisions approaching them within 1–3 years. Aiden (15) wants mortgage and housing finance detail. Ovie (15) wants to understand whether she should be investing at her age, and how cashback products work — features she sees on Revolut but doesn't understand. Levi (17) identifies taxes as his most significant knowledge gap. All three are aware of impending transitions and want to feel prepared.

**Young Adults** want the mechanics behind specific products they are already encountering. The credit card minimum-payment default caught Mohammad (23) off guard — a feature of a product he actively holds. Grace (19) wants to understand ISAs, compound interest, and different savings account types. Zain (20) is actively researching LISAs and ISAs but finds existing content too generic or too shallow. Althea (19) wants to understand how savings accounts actually generate interest, beyond the knowledge that they exist. The distinguishing feature for this group: gaps are not abstract or aspirational — they are live and consequential.

## 2.4 Learning Styles of Young People

### 2.4.1 Introduction to learning styles

Financial education doesn't land in a vacuum — it lands in the context of how a young person already processes, seeks, and retains information. Understanding learning styles here is not about categorizing participants into fixed types; it's about identifying what conditions make learning more likely to happen and stick. Three dimensions are particularly relevant for Money Monsters: **how participants prefer to encounter new information**, **how much cognitive friction they'll tolerate before disengaging**, and **what motivates sustained return**.

### 2.4.2 Learning styles mapped by our three segments

**Young Teens — learn through play, social comparison, and doing**

Young Teens are not passive learners — they are actively curious, but that curiosity needs activating and sustaining. The medium matters enormously. Content that looks like school, reads like a textbook, or requires extended concentration without reward will not hold this group. What works is short, visual, interactive, and social. Lottie's observation that "people are on TikTok and Instagram that much, it's hard to keep people's attention span" represents a broadly shared reality. Sam actively seeks game-like formats — previous finance apps were abandoned because they were "way too hard" or "really boring." Hugo's strongest learning moments came from curiosity-driven discovery (researching price inflation, watching TikTok about making money as a kid) rather than instruction.

Social comparison is a meaningful learning driver for this group. Hugo rates his money knowledge against peers constantly. Sam aspires to the saving habits of friends who have "piles and piles of money." Zain (13) wants to get "a head start." This suggests that social and competitive features — visible streaks, peer progress comparison — are not peripheral gamification extras but central to how this group will engage with financial learning.

**Older Teens — learn through relevance and growing financial identity**

Older Teens are at a developmental inflection point. They are beginning to form a financial self-concept — views about who they are with money, what their values and priorities are — and they don't respond as readily to peer-comparison motivation as Young Teens do. What lands for this group is content that connects directly to situations they're already in or can clearly anticipate: earning real income, approaching independence, making first decisions about credit or further education.

Levi (17) responded strongly to the 50/30/20 rule because he could apply it immediately to his own earnings structure. Aiden (15) wants role-play scenarios and practical application — learning by doing — rather than calculation exercises or abstract lessons. Both Aiden and Ovie immediately flagged the app's visual design as "primary school"-level, suggesting a higher aesthetic threshold than Young Teens. This segment has outgrown some of the gamification that motivates younger teens, but hasn't arrived at the fully self-directed research habits of Young Adults. Content that positions them as emerging adults making real decisions — rather than young people receiving financial instruction — will engage better.

**Young Adults — learn through immediate need and situation-specific depth**

Young Adults learn most effectively when content maps directly onto something they are facing or have recently experienced. Mohammad's credit card mistake illustrates the principle in reverse: content explaining default minimum payments would have been immediately, practically valuable — precisely because he was living the consequences. Grace learned what compound interest was during the session and retained it because it connected to her student savings question. Enoch appreciated the lesson content because it covered "everyday financial decisions" rather than abstract principles.

This segment has the highest tolerance for depth and the lowest tolerance for oversimplification. Mohammad found individual lessons "too short for even a bite-size chunk," preferring 2–3 minute sessions. Zain (20) described the content as "scraping the top of things." Althea wanted to know not just that compound interest exists, but what she should specifically do with that knowledge in her situation. The most effective learning format for Young Adults is situational simulation: what happens when you do X, what should you check before signing up for Y, how do you think through decision Z. The app's scenario-based dialogue format was appreciated by this group — the constraint was that it didn't go deep enough once engagement was established.

# 3. What Young People Actually Want from Financial Education (JTBD)

**What are Jobs to Be Done?** JTBD is a framework for understanding why people engage with a product — not just what they do, but what they're trying to achieve. The core idea: people don't buy products, they "hire" them to get a job done. 

For Money Monsters, this means asking not "what does this user need to learn?" but "what is this user trying to accomplish in their financial life right now, and how can the app help them do it?" 

The answers differ meaningfully by segment — which has direct implications for content selection, lesson framing, and how the app positions itself to each audience. A 13-year-old who wants to save for a gaming console has a fundamentally different job to be done than a 23-year-old who just made a costly credit card mistake. Understanding this distinction is the difference between content that feels useful and content that feels irrelevant.

---

## 3.1 The Universal Truth: The Knowing–Doing Gap

Across all three segments there is a clear pattern emerged more consistently than any other: **young people broadly know what they should do with money, but consistently fail to do it.** This is the knowing–doing gap, and it is not a knowledge problem alone. It is a behavior design problem.

The gap shows up differently by segment, but the structure is the same. 

Young Teens know they should save rather than impulse-spend — but they don't. Sam (14) had been trying to save for a £550 gaming console for months, yet kept spending on individual games instead. 

Older Teens like Aiden (15) articulated saving principles clearly — and had successfully saved for both a PC and DJ decks — yet still described his relationship with money as: *"I see money and I want to spend it."* 

Young Adults exhibited the same pattern at higher stakes: Althea (19) described a repeating monthly cycle of overspending → end-of-month panic → resolve to change → forgetting → repeat. Mia (25) recognised her month-end stress pattern but had not broken it. 

**The implication for Money Monsters**

Significantly, **financial education alone will not close this gap.** What young people need — and what the app has the potential to provide — is the combination of knowledge and a system that makes acting on that knowledge easier. 

This means in the short term designing real-life scenarios that stand a better chance of prompting reflection at the point of decision, not just after the fact and exploring future goal-setting features that connect learning to something concrete and near-term; and a progression logic that builds habitual engagement rather than one-off learning events. 

The segments that responded most positively to the research stimuli did so precisely because the game mechanics created a behavioral loop — streaks, rewards, progression — that made acting easier. Where that loop felt too thin or too playful, engagement dropped.

## 3.2 Young Teens (13–15) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Teens are at an early stage of financial awareness. Most have limited, irregular income (pocket money, birthday cash, chores) and minimal exposure to formal financial products. Their financial world is small but emotionally significant — and what they want from financial education reflects that reality.

### 3.2.1 Get a head start on peers

> *"I want to feel like I'm already getting a head start."*

The most competitive and aspirational of the Young Teen jobs. This group is acutely aware of variation in their peer group — who's good with money, who isn't — and many want to be on the right side of that comparison. Hugo (13) rated himself 9/10 compared to friends who "know nothing." Zain (13) wanted to start learning now to position himself ahead for a future business. Sam (14) observed that friends who save well have "piles and piles of money" available for instant purchases and aspires to the same position.

This job is about status and self-concept as much as financial skill. The competitive and social mechanics of the app — streaks, comparing progress with friends, collecting more than peers — are not peripheral features for this segment. They are central to the value proposition. Making financial literacy feel like an advantage, rather than a duty, is what will drive early adoption and retention with Young Teens.

### 3.2.2 Make money last longer

> *"I want to know how to make my money last longer."*

Ranked as a top priority by the majority of Young Teens in the card sort, this job is grounded in a familiar and frustrating experience: money received disappears almost immediately. Lottie (13) describes herself as "very impulse driven." Zain (13) spent £11 at a shop "without thinking" while out with friends. Sam's money "only lasts very briefly after receiving it." Isaac (12) admits to buying sweets he doesn't really need.

The desire is concrete — it's about specific, near-term situations: going out with friends, not running out before the next birthday, being able to afford something without asking parents. Content for this job should be grounded in realistic everyday scenarios for this age group: small amounts, short time horizons, temptation in social contexts. Lessons on needs vs. wants, spending triggers, and simple mental budgeting rules will land best here.

### 3.2.3 Save towards something specific and near-term

> *"I want to get better at saving to afford something big."*

Consistently the top-ranked priority when asked. Every participant either had a recent example of goal-directed saving and critically, these goals are concrete, personal, and relatively near-term. Not "saving for the future" in any abstract sense.

The direct implication for content: abstract lessons about long-term financial futures are less likely to land. What works is connecting financial concepts to something the participant is already motivated by. Saving mechanics, simple compound growth (over months, not decades), and goal-tracking features all serve this job. 

### 3.2.4 Understand money stuff that no one explains

> *"I wish someone would explain money in a way that's actually about my life."*

Young Teens are routinely exposed to adult money conversations they don't understand — mortgages, taxes, savings accounts, interest rates — but have no accessible way to bridge that gap. Isaac (12) specifically mentioned mortgages, prompted by his parents' recent house move. Norah (13) overhears adult financial conversations and wants to feel less lost. Hugo (13) hears his dad discuss investing and wants to understand what that means. School is largely failing here: participants described school money education as either non-existent or overly academic ("economics rather than actual finance").

This job is partly about curiosity, partly about belonging — not feeling silly when money comes up. It doesn't require deep content, but it does require accessible language, relatable scenarios, and the consistent feeling that the app is speaking to them specifically, not to adults or older users. The guide character, scenario framing, and language register all need to serve this job.

## 3.3 Older Teens (15–18) — Jobs to Be Done

Older Teens occupy a transitional zone. They are starting to earn real income (part-time jobs, zero-hours contracts), approaching or already making significant financial decisions (apprenticeships vs. university, first credit products), and are more aware of the financial world — and its risks — than Young Teens. Their jobs to be done reflect this higher-stakes environment.

### 3.3.1 Make smarter decisions with existing money

> *"I want to make smart choices with the money I'm earning."*

Ranked the single most important job across all three Older Teen participants, this reflects a meaningful shift from the Young Teen position. These participants have real income — Levi (17) earns £12/hour on a zero-hours contract; Ovie (15) sells vintage clothes on Vinted; Aiden (15) recently saved for significant purchases. The job is no longer just "make my pocket money last" — it's "make sure I'm using my earnings well."

Smart choices are defined in practical terms: knowing the difference between a need and a want (Ovie), avoiding purchases that destroy your financial position (Aiden), ensuring spending is beneficial rather than random (Levi). Content must go beyond basic budgeting principles and engage with real decision-making scenarios at Older Teen income levels. The 50/30/20 rule landed well with both Aiden and Levi precisely because it gave them a usable heuristic for exactly this job.

### 3.3.2 Avoid scams and financial traps

> *"I want to know how I can avoid being scammed or ripped off."*

Scam awareness is the standout priority for Older Teens — and the one that requires care in how it's delivered. Aiden (15) described being influenced by YouTube content about sophisticated scam tactics and ranked this as his single most important financial concern, referencing typical losses of £4,000+. Ovie (15) ranked it in her top three. Enoch (18, YA) had a lived experience: he lost £100 to a sophisticated Telegram-based job scam requiring staged deposits.

What distinguishes this job is that Older Teens are starting to encounter real financial products and real risks for the first time, often without adult supervision. They're earning money, opening accounts, making purchases independently. Content here should focus on recognising manipulation patterns, understanding how financial fraud works in practice, and developing healthy scepticism — not just a checklist of things to watch out for, which participants found patronising.

### 3.3.3 Don't fall behind

> *"I want to feel like I'm getting ahead with money, not falling behind."*

All three Older Teen participants expressed some version of anxiety about being left behind — financially and in terms of knowledge. Levi (17) described his relationship with money as "on edge," specifically referencing discomfort in conversations with financially knowledgeable people. Aiden (15) framed it competitively: getting ahead rather than falling behind. Ovie (15) ranked "feel confident about money, not stressed or clueless" as a top priority.

Beneath the surface, this job is about the coming transition: all three are within 1–3 years of significant financial independence — university, apprenticeships, gap years, first proper employment. The horizon is visible and slightly daunting. Content for this job should focus on building readiness for that transition: what's coming, what you'll need to understand, and how to lay the foundations now. This is also the job that makes the "head start" framing resonate most strongly with this segment — it reframes preparation as an advantage rather than a burden.

## 3.4 Young Adults (18–25) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Adults are the segment for whom financial education is most immediately urgent — and yet also the segment the current app format serves least well. Their financial situations are real and live: rent, student loans, credit cards, mortgages, irregular income. Their jobs to be done reflect the pressure and complexity of that reality.

### 3.4.1 Not feel out of depth when real situations arise

> *"I want to feel ready for money situations, not out of my depth."*

This is the dominant job for Young Adults and the emotional core of their relationship with financial education. Grace (19) ran out of money in her first year of university — a formative experience that now drives her desire to feel "secure and able to predict money situations." Mohammad (23) made a costly credit card mistake because he didn't understand that his automatic payment defaulted to the minimum balance. Zain (20) is preparing himself for impending rent, mortgage, and bill responsibilities he can see approaching. Althea (19) is caught in an avoidance cycle triggered by exactly these moments of feeling out of her depth.

The word that recurs is *ready*. Young Adults don't want to be educated for education's sake — they want to feel equipped. The implication for lesson design is that content should be structured around situations: *What happens when you get your first credit card? What do you need to understand before you take out a student overdraft? What does running out of money actually look like, and what can you do?* Information delivered before the situation arises feels irrelevant; information that simulates or reflects a real situation the user is already in lands as essential.

### 3.4.2 Understand the mechanics behind financial products (credit, interest, debt)

> *"I want to deal with real things: rent, bills, credit scores, debt."*

Young Adults want to understand how things actually work — not just that compound interest exists, but how it affects the specific decisions they're making now. Mohammad didn't know that credit card autopay defaults to the minimum balance; he paid interest on a balance he could have cleared. Grace didn't understand compound interest before the app demo. Althea doesn't understand how savings accounts generate interest or how to access a better rate than a basic current account. Zain (20) is actively researching ISAs and LISAs but finds existing content either too generic or too shallow to be actionable.

This is distinct from the Young Teen job of "understand money stuff that no one explains." Young Adults don't need things simplified — they need things explained with enough depth to be usable. They're actively encountering specific financial products and need to know how those products actually behave. The mechanics of APR, the difference between fixed and variable costs, how credit scores are built and damaged — these aren't abstract for this group. They're live and consequential.

### 3.4.3 Avoid getting caught out by traps and hidden costs

> *"I want to know how to avoid traps — buy now, pay later products, hidden costs."*

Young Adults are more attuned to systemic traps embedded in mainstream financial products: the credit card minimum payment default, the 0% promotional rate that reverts without warning, the buy-now-pay-later product that accrues interest invisibly. These aren't scams — they're features of real products that most young adults encounter. Mohammad referenced a friend's experience with a 0% promotional credit card whose terms weren't read. His own credit card situation was a version of the same trap.

The job is about developing the critical reading skills and financial literacy to navigate these products without being caught out. Content for this job should combine product mechanics (how do these things work?) with decision frameworks (what should you check before signing up?). The emphasis is on building a habit of scrutiny, not teaching specific rules that may become outdated.

## 3.5 What This Means for Positioning, Content, and Lesson Design

Four cross-cutting implications emerge from the JTBD analysis:

{feedback for AI, this needs to be better grounded in the jobs to be done rather than the proposition, and use what is outlined in the rest of the same section as inputs}

**1. Ground everything in real situations, not abstract principles.** Every segment's jobs are rooted in concrete lived experience — a specific purchase goal, a past mistake, an approaching transition, a social fear. Content that connects to a real scenario will always outperform content that teaches concepts in isolation. The tested scenario-based lesson format rated well, the next focus is to ensure lesson design is specific and realistic enough for each segment.

**2. Meet people where they are, not where they should be.** The most resonant proposition across segments was *"You're already making money decisions — make better ones."* This phrasing works because it doesn't imply the user is ignorant; it acknowledges their existing agency and offers to improve it. Positioning and lesson design should follow the same logic: start from the user's current situation, not from a syllabus.

**3. The app must bridge knowing and doing — not just explain.** The knowing–doing gap is universal. Explanation is necessary but not sufficient. The app needs mechanisms that make the transition from knowledge to action easier: goal-setting tied to concrete targets, scenario prompts that echo real decisions, behavioral nudges embedded in the learning experience. This is as much a product design challenge as a content one.

**4. The same topic cannot serve all three segments in the same way.** Credit cards appear as a relevant topic across all segments — but the job they're hired to do is completely different. For a Young Teen, it's about understanding what adults talk about. For an Older Teen, it's about preparing for their first account. For a Young Adult, it's about not making a costly mistake with a product they already have. Lesson design should be segment-aware from framing through to question design.

# 4. Topics and Content: What to teach; based on research exploration and JTBD assessment

The research did not directly ask participants to list a curriculum. What emerged instead was a picture of financial lives — the decisions they face daily, the concepts they don't understand, the moments that stressed them, and the outcomes they want. Topics and suggested lessons below are derived from three inputs: what participants explicitly said they wanted to learn; what their behaviours and anxieties implied they needed; and the Jobs to Be Done that underpin each segment's relationship with financial education.

This is not an exhaustive list of everything that could be taught. It is a prioritised map of what is most likely to be relevant, engaging, and consequential for each group — now, at their current life stage.

## 4.1 Content Relevance by Segment; Mapped to Jobs to Be Done

### 4.1.1 Young Teens (12–15)

Young Teens' jobs to be done centre on four things: getting a head start on peers, making money last longer, saving towards something specific, and understanding money that no one explains. All four point toward foundation-level content — grounded in their current, small-scale financial world.

| Topic | Serves Which Job | Evidence from Research | Priority |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------|----------|
| Saving basics and goal-setting | Make money last; Save for goals | "I can't save money. I just spend it." (Olivia); console goal (Sam, 2 years); football boots (Isaac) | **High** |
| Needs vs. wants | Make money last longer | £11 at a shop "without thinking" (Zain); clothes impulse buys (Olivia, Lottie) | **High** |

## 4.2 Topic Breadth and Depth

### 4.2.1 Which topics are appropriate for which segment — and why

Not all financial topics belong in all segments. The same topic can be deeply relevant at one life stage and counterproductive at another. Below, the five most important topic areas are mapped across segments to show how depth and framing should shift.

**SAVING**

| Segment | Appropriate depth | Primary framing |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Young Teens | Mechanics of saving; goal-setting; resisting impulse | Near-term goals; small amounts (£5–50); why money disappears |
| Older Teens | Account types; interest rates; automating habits | Using the right tools on first real earnings |
| Young Adults | Savings optimization; compound growth; ISAs; emergency funds | Maximising returns; building independence; having a system |

Saving is the one universal topic — but the job it serves is completely different across segments. YT: behavioural (stopping impulse spending). OT: structural (using the right account). YA: mechanical (understanding how to optimize returns and build resilience). Teaching YTs about ISAs wastes a lesson. Teaching YAs about needs vs. wants wastes their time.

**INVESTING**

| Segment | Appropriate depth | Primary framing |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Young Teens | What is investing? Why do adults do it? Rough risk concept | Demystification; "your dad mentioned it — here's what it means" |
| Older Teens | Stock market basics; should I invest at 16?; how to start safely | Decision-readiness — not an instruction to act yet |
| Young Adults | Types of investments; risk vs. return; ISAs as vehicles; how to start | Actionable: enough for an informed first decision |

Investing must not be skipped at YT level. Hugo's curiosity is representative. Without any content, young people either dismiss it entirely or fall for YouTube get-rich schemes. The app should demystify without triggering premature action.

**SCAMS AND FRAUD**

| Segment | Appropriate depth | Primary framing |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Young Teens | Online safety basics; what scams look like; family examples | Pattern recognition; "what happened to Olivia's elderly relative" |
| Older Teens | Social engineering tactics; how real financial fraud works; the Telegram job scam anatomy | Sophisticated scenarios; manipulation mechanics |
| Young Adults | Investment scams; promotional rate traps; reading small print; identity theft | Critical evaluation; product scrutiny habits; systemic traps in mainstream products |

Scam content should escalate in sophistication across segments. Older Teens are the most financially vulnerable group in practical terms — they're beginning to transact independently and encountering real risks without adult supervision. Their scam content should be treated as the highest-stakes delivery challenge.

**CREDIT**

| Segment | Appropriate depth | Primary framing |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Young Teens | What is a credit card / loan / credit score? (vocabulary only) | "You'll hear these — here's what they mean" |
| Older Teens | Building a credit score before you need it; what affects it | Preparation: foundations now save pain later |
| Young Adults | Credit card mechanics; APR; minimum payments; autopay traps; buy now pay later | Prevention and recovery: real consequences, real decisions |

Credit should not be a core YT topic but can appear as vocabulary demystification. It becomes action-relevant at OT level (the score you build at 16 matters at 21) and urgent at YA level. Mohammad's credit card mistake — paying interest on a balance he could have cleared because autopay defaulted to the minimum — is a perfect scenario foundation.

**BUDGETING**

| Segment | Appropriate depth | Primary framing |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Young Teens | Simple mental rules; needs vs. wants; spending triggers | Why money disappears; how to slow it down |
| Older Teens | 50/30/20 rule; tracking; deliberate vs. impulsive | Using a real framework on real earnings |
| Young Adults | Systems for inconsistent income; monthly review habits; tracking categories | A system that fits your life, not a textbook |

The YA budgeting challenge is structurally different from YT or OT. Mia and Althea both have irregular tutoring income alongside parental support; Grace manages three irregular student finance payments plus bar work. Standard "divide your monthly salary" advice fails this group. Content should explicitly acknowledge income variability and offer frameworks that work without a fixed baseline.

### 4.2.2 Topics to advance, retire, or gate by segment

**Advance immediately** — high demand, clear knowledge gap, directly evidenced:
- Saving habits and impulse control **(YT)** — the single largest pain point; universal emotional resonance across 7 participants
- Cashback basics **(OT)** — specific, real knowledge gap; two direct flags from Aiden and Ovie; easy credibility win
- Credit card mechanics — minimum payments and autopay **(YA)** — live financial consequence; Mohammad's scenario is a natural lesson
- Compound interest **(YA)** — produced a genuine "aha" reaction from Grace in the app demo; needs a proper standalone lesson, not a passing reference
- Savings account types and ISAs **(OT/YA)** — Althea and Grace have explicit articulated gaps; Aiden and Ovie are uncertain about account options

**Develop but frame with care:**
- Investing (all segments) — high curiosity but requires calibration by segment. YT: conceptual only. OT: decision-readiness framing. YA: actionable. Do not skip it; do not overclaim.
- Scams (all segments) — should escalate in sophistication. Avoid condescending "be careful" framing; aim for pattern recognition and critical evaluation skills.
- Taxes (OT/YA) — relevant but best integrated into adjacent lessons (first payslip, self-employment) rather than as standalone abstract content.

**Gate or deprioritise for launch:**
- Mortgages and pensions **(YT)** — can appear as vocabulary and demystification ("here's what that word means"), not as taught actionable content
- Complex investment instruments **(YT, OT)** — too far removed from current financial reality to be useful or credible
- Long-term retirement planning **(all)** — no emotional proximity for any age in this sample; candidate for a premium YA module much later

## 4.3 Framing Topics in a Relevant Way

Topic selection determines relevance. Framing determines whether users feel it. The research consistently showed that the same concept lands as immediately essential or completely irrelevant depending entirely on how it is contextualised. Three principles emerged:

**1. Anchor every lesson to a situation the user is already in — or will be in very soon.**

Young Teens respond to scenarios from their current world: small amounts, social spending moments, near-term purchase goals. Abstract concepts failed; concrete scenarios landed. "What happens if you save your £20 birthday money for 6 months vs. spend it tomorrow?" works. "Compound interest over 30 years" does not.

Older Teens are approaching real financial independence. The most resonant framing for this group is *preparation*: help them feel ready for what's coming, not tested on what they should already know. "Should you be investing at your age?" works better than "here's how the stock market works." The former acknowledges their situation; the latter talks past it.

Young Adults want content that reflects real conditions, not ideal ones. Grace doesn't need "how to divide your salary" — she needs "how to manage three income sources that arrive at different times." Mia doesn't need a simple budget template — she needs a framework that doesn't fall apart when her tutoring income disappears for a month. Content that assumes regular monthly income will fail the majority of this group.

**2. Match the emotional weight of the framing to the stakes.**

Young Teens found the app's playful, light tone broadly appropriate — the financial stakes in their world are genuinely small (pocket money, small goals). A game-like framing works because money at this stage is important but not yet consequential.

Older Teens are at the edge of that tolerance. Ovie's comment — *"it's too playful, money is more serious than that"* — signals that framing needs to mature alongside content. This group wants to be taken seriously, not talked down to. The tone should feel like a knowledgeable friend explaining something real, not a children's game that happens to mention money.

Young Adults are explicit. Mia: *"There's a degree of seriousness because it's important."* Althea: *"It's more instruction. I like it to be more firm or clear in what I should do."* Grace: *"Too childish."* For YA, the implied relationship between app and user should feel like a capable, direct peer — not a cheerful mascot. This is as much a content-register decision as a design one.

**3. Use the language they already use — not the language of financial education.**

Young Teens talk about "making money last," "saving for something," "not wasting it." They don't use the word "budgeting" — but they understand the concept perfectly. Lesson titles should mirror their vocabulary: *"Make your money last longer"* lands better than *"Introduction to budgeting principles."*

Older Teens are beginning to use financial product vocabulary naturally — Revolut, savings pots, cashback, Vinted. Lessons can and should use this language without explanation; it signals that the app understands their world. Explaining what a Revolut savings pot is to someone who already uses one is an engagement killer.

Young Adults want precision over simplification. They've moved past "saving is good" and want to understand actual mechanics. The right register is something like: *"You know interest exists — here's how to actually use it to your advantage."* That approach respects existing knowledge while filling the gap, which is exactly what they asked for.

# 5. Proposition: How the Financial Literacy Solution Lands

## 5.1 The Three Propositions Tested

Three proposition concepts were developed before fieldwork and tested in each session using short stimulus cards. Participants were asked for an immediate instinctive reaction, then invited to engage more closely with their preferred option before reflecting on the alternatives.

**"Money Stuff Nobody Explains"**
Framing: *The basics other people seem to just know. We give you all of it — explained simply.*
Key benefits highlighted: How to make your money go further; Saving for what you actually want; Spotting scams and rip-offs; Stuff that catches people out before it catches you.

**"Train Your Money Brain"**
Framing: *You're already making money decisions. Make better ones.*
Key benefits highlighted: Spotting good vs. bad money choices; Saving for your future; Making your money last longer; Thinking ahead before you spend.

**"Real Money Skills for Life"**
Framing: *Simple money skills you can use straight away — starting from exactly where you are.*
Key benefits highlighted: Making your money last; Living independently with confidence; Understanding how money actually works.

---

## 5.2 What Resonated and Why

Whilst no single proposition dominated cleanly across all segments, preferences aligned to participant's relationship with money and learning attitudes. 

### 5.2.1 "Money stuff nobody explains" 

This direction's asset is its implicit framing of financial knowledge as *insider access* — things you were supposed to know, explained at last. This framing has a self-permission quality: it removes the judgment from engaging with financial education. You're not admitting ignorance; you're claiming your right to information that should have been available all along.

This resonated most directly with Young Teens and a portion of Young Adults. Sam (14) gave Blue a fire emoji, responding specifically to the promise of "practical skills usable straight away." Hugo (13) was drawn to the "mystery and exclusivity factor." Norah (13) chose Blue partly for "making money go further" — a concrete, present-tense benefit. Among Young Adults, Mia (25) connected with "stuff you're supposed to know" — the acknowledgement that legitimate foundational content is missing from most people's education. Grace (19) was drawn to "what catches people out" and "what's actually worth saving for." Zain (20, YA) chose Blue specifically for its pre-emptive logic — learning what catches people out *before it catches me*.

Where Blue fell short: among Young Adults with higher self-assessed financial literacy, the phrasing "stuff nobody explains" risks implying that the user hasn't been paying attention. Mohammad (23) noted this directly: *"For most money things, you've had exposure in the first place if you're paying attention."* The premise requires a degree of humility; for participants who pride themselves on self-directed learning, it can feel mildly accusatory. The proposition works best when the gap is framed as a structural failure of financial education — not a personal failure of the user.

### 5.2.2 "Train your money brain" (Green)

Green's strength is its behavioral framing. "You're already making money decisions. Make better ones." This lands because it doesn't position the user as ignorant — it acknowledges existing agency and offers an upgrade. The implication is not that you've been doing it wrong, but that you could be doing it better. This distinction matters particularly for segments that have some financial self-concept to protect.

Green resonated most strongly with Older Teens and a portion of Young Adults. Aiden (15) was drawn to it immediately for its visual boldness and rhyming headline, but the deeper appeal was its framing of younger audiences being trained for future readiness. Levi (17) found "train your money brain" motivating rather than try-hard. Among Young Adults, Enoch (18) gave Green a fire emoji — finding it more engaging than Pink's "boring professor" feel. Mohammad (23) chose Green for "think ahead before you spend," connecting to his view that sound money management is a habit rather than a knowledge exercise. Althea (19) connected with "you're already making money decisions, make better ones" — it felt firm and practical rather than aspirational.

Green's limitation: for Young Teens and younger participants, "train your money brain" requires committing to effort before the reward is visible. Zain (13, YT) found it leaning too "schoolish." For participants who experience money as stressful or confusing rather than interesting, being told to "train" implies work before play — and that sequencing doesn't activate engagement at the bottom of the age range.

### 5.2.3 "Real money skills for life" (Pink)

Pink's positioning — simple, accessible, immediately applicable — appeals to participants who find financial education intimidating, overly academic, or simply unappealing. Its clarity is a genuine strength for younger audiences: Lottie (13) responded immediately to "simple" and "easy." Olivia (14) connected with immediate applicability: *"when I get money, I just spend it."* For participants who need a low barrier to engagement, Pink delivers it.

However, Pink's directness becomes a liability across the upper age range. The "skills for life" phrasing triggers associations with generic educational language for older participants — overused to the point of credibility loss. Mohammad (23) called it "a bit cringe," noting that "skills for life" is something "everyone is just saying." Zain (20, YA) found it too "schooly" — the word "skills" making it sound academic and dull. Even among Older Teens, participants who were initially drawn to Pink (Ovie, 15) showed diminishing enthusiasm on deeper reading, pivoting toward Blue's more concrete benefits. Pink's instinctive simplicity is both its appeal and its ceiling.

---

## 5.3 Segment-Level Proposition Response

### 5.3.1 Young Teens

For Young Teens, **Blue and Pink split instinctive preference roughly evenly**, with Green landing least well. The underlying driver is the same in both cases: Young Teens want financial education to feel relevant to their life *right now*, not as preparation for some abstract future.

Blue appeals to those with a more curious, exploratory disposition (Hugo, Sam, Norah) — the framing of exclusive, unsaid knowledge activates their desire to be ahead of peers. Pink appeals to those who need reassurance that the content will be accessible and immediately useful (Olivia, Lottie, and Isaac as a secondary preference). Green's "train your money brain" reads too much like an externally imposed commitment for this group — it describes the outcome of engagement rather than the immediate reward.

The most important single element for Young Teens in any proposition is the implicit promise that *this is for someone like me*. Both Blue ("stuff nobody explains") and Pink ("starting from exactly where you are") carry this message. Generic educational framing — however well-intentioned — consistently failed to activate interest in this segment.

### 5.3.2 Older Teens

**Green landed most clearly with Older Teens.** All three participants either chose it outright or engaged most deeply with it on reflection. The behavioral framing — "you're already making money decisions, make better ones" — is well-matched to this group's developmental moment. They are earning real money and making real choices, and a proposition that acknowledges that agency, rather than positioning them as students receiving instruction, is more credible and more motivating.

Blue was an early instinctive choice for Ovie (15), but fell away on closer reading — its "insider knowledge" framing felt less urgent than the immediate practical relevance of Green's benefits. Pink rated consistently low: "cringey" in Ovie's assessment, visually and tonally less compelling than Green for Aiden and Levi.

The consistent requirement for this segment: a proposition must upgrade them, not instruct them. Older Teens are developing a financial identity and respond to content that treats them as emerging adults with stakes in real decisions — not young people who still need the basics explained.

### 5.3.3 Young Adults

**Young Adults split between Blue and Green**, with Pink largely irrelevant. The split maps onto a psychological dimension rather than a demographic one. Participants who are most aware of specific knowledge gaps and want to fill them — Grace (19), Mia (25), Zain (20) — gravitated toward Blue. Those more focused on behaviour change and decision-making habits — Mohammad (23), Enoch (18), Althea (19) — gravitated toward Green.

What both preferences share is a rejection of condescension. Mohammad's critique of Pink, Grace's desire to avoid "information she could already figure out," and Althea's preference for "firm and clear" rather than inspirational framing all point to the same underlying requirement: a proposition that treats them as capable adults with real financial stakes, not students receiving a lesson.

For Young Adults, credibility is the primary filter. A proposition that feels generic, oversimplified, or targeted at a younger audience will be dismissed before it has a chance to demonstrate its content.

---

## 5.4 What Should Come "Above the Fold"

Across all segments, three types of content consistently triggered the most positive, engaged responses during the proposition phase:

**1. Concrete, near-term benefit statements.** "How to make your money go further" (Blue), "making your money last longer" (Green), "saving for what you actually want" (Blue) — these activate engagement because they describe an immediate improvement to a situation participants already experience as frustrating. Abstract or long-term framing ("for life," "future-focused," "build financial confidence") consistently failed to generate the same response. The benefit must be recognizable from the user's current situation, not their future one.

**2. Agency-affirming language.** "You're already making money decisions. Make better ones." (Green) was the single most frequently referenced phrase across all three propositions. It works because it positions the user as already active — someone receiving an upgrade, not someone being taught. The contrast with framing that implies deficit ("skills you don't have," "basics other people already know") is emotionally significant, even where the information being delivered is identical.

**3. Specificity over aspiration.** Participants consistently responded more positively to proposition elements they could map onto their own specific situation. Sam (14) responded to "what you should actually save for." Mohammad (23) responded to "think ahead before you spend." Grace (19) responded to "stuff that catches people out." Broad aspirational benefits ("independence," "confidence," "financial wellbeing") were noted but did not motivate. The hook is specificity — a clear, recognizable situation the user is already in.

The opportunity in any final proposition and app store communication is to lead decisively with these three qualities, and reduce the generic educational language currently surrounding them. The raw material is present across all three propositions — the task is prioritization.

---

## 5.5 Language and Framing Principles

Several consistent language patterns emerged across all segments with direct implications for proposition copy, onboarding language, and lesson framing.

**Avoid "skills" as a framing word.** Across Young Adults and Older Teens, "skills" triggers associations with school, HR training, and overused self-improvement content. It is deployed so widely in educational contexts that it has lost persuasive value. Outcome language consistently outperforms it: "know exactly what to do when your first credit card bill arrives" lands better than "develop key financial skills." The test: can you point to a specific moment in the user's life where this applies? If yes, use that. If not, reframe.

**"Simple" works for Young Teens; it alienates older segments.** Pink's use of "simple money skills" resonated with Young Teens who need to know they won't be overwhelmed. For Older Teens and Young Adults, "simple" implies either that the content won't be worth their time, or worse, that the app assumes they need things dumbed down. Any segment-differentiated communication — app store descriptions, onboarding screens, lesson introductions — should use "simple" only when addressing younger audiences directly.

**Practical beats inspirational, consistently.** Across all three segments, concrete situation-specific language rated above inspiring or aspirational language. "Understand what your savings account is actually doing with your money" outperforms "build financial confidence." "Know what to check before you sign up for a credit card" outperforms "take control of your financial future." The emotional appeal that works is competence — feeling equipped for something specific — not aspiration toward an abstract financial destination.

**Situate the knowledge gap in the system, not the user.** Blue's approach of naming "stuff nobody explains" works when the implication is that the gap is structural — a failure of school, of financial product design, of the way adults talk about money. It risks falling flat when it implies the user should have known already. Language that names the systemic failure ("nobody teaches this in school," "the stuff that catches people out because no one warns them") performs better than language that implies personal inattention. The distinction is subtle but consistently affects how the proposition is received.

# 6. App Concept: First Impressions of stimuli and Visual Comprehension

## 6.1 Immediate Impressions & Expectations

The Money Monsters landing screen does something few finance apps achieve: it communicates its purpose and tone instantly. The level-map layout, the monster characters, and the unlockable progression structure combine to signal a gamified learning experience before a single word is read. Participants across all segments understood what they were looking at within seconds — and almost all reached for the same reference point: Duolingo.

This immediate recognition is a meaningful asset. Duolingo carries strong associations with accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a mental model well-suited to financial education. Lottie (13) made the comparison positively and unprompted. Norah (13) echoed it. Mia (25) recognised the format immediately and found it reassuring. Enoch (18) articulated the concept precisely: *"looks like a game but made to make learning about money easy."* The concept lands clearly.

The expectations set by the landing screen were also broadly accurate: participants expected progressive content unlock, some kind of streak or engagement mechanic, and monsters as a progress reward. These expectations were met by the research stimulus — expectation alignment at entry is a prerequisite for a satisfying first session, and this design achieves it.

The complication arrives when the question of *who this is for* enters the frame. The same visual language that makes the app immediately legible to a 13-year-old signals a mismatch to a 19-year-old. This is not a flaw in the design concept — it is a consequence of building a single aesthetic for an audience that spans six developmental years.

## 6.2 The Aesthetic Age-Gap Challenge

The app's current visual aesthetic is optimised for Young Teens. The monster characters, the colour palette, the game-style iconography, and the Duolingo-like layout all point to the same design sensibility: playful, accessible, safe. For the 12–14 age band this is a strength. For older users it becomes a progressively greater barrier — one that increases in severity with age and with financial seriousness.

### 6.2.1 Where the current design works

For Young Teens, the aesthetic functions as intended. Monster characters are clearly understood and welcomed. The level-map progression is immediately engaging. The gamified framing lowers the perceived difficulty of financial content — it signals "this will be manageable" before the first question appears. Hugo (13) found the monster name catchy and the design cool. Sam (14) described the app as "easy to use" and not intimidating. Isaac (12) noted that "monsters make it more interesting for young people than a plain interface." For this segment, the visual design does exactly what it should: it removes the barrier to entry for a topic that might otherwise feel adult, serious, and off-putting.

The structural conventions also work well across the full age range. The level map, the locked/unlocked progression logic, the streak concept, and the collection mechanic were understood and positively received even by participants who disliked the aesthetic. Enoch (18) and Zain (20, YA) appreciated the unlockable chunk structure despite finding the overall design "a bit young." The implication: the UX architecture beneath the visual layer is sound across all segments. The problem is not the model — it is how the model is dressed.

### 6.2.2 Where it starts to fail

For Older Teens, the design creates friction. Ovie (15) described the landing screen as feeling like "primary school in year seven." Aiden (15) described it as "immediately perceived as designed for primary school children." Levi (17) was more positive initially — the gamified structure engaged him — but later flagged that the monster characters would need to feel less toy-like for a 17-year-old audience to take the app seriously.

The core issue for this segment is not that they dislike gamification — they don't. Aiden, Ovie, and Levi all responded positively to streak mechanics, progression, and collection-based rewards. The issue is that the specific visual execution (monster characters, primary-palette colours, cartoon guides) reads as targeted at a younger audience. For a 15-17 year old beginning to form a financial identity and see themselves as a developing adult, the aesthetic implicitly undermines the credibility of the content before it has been seen.

### 6.2.3 Where it actively damages credibility

For a significant portion of Young Adults, the design actively works against engagement. Grace (19) described the landing screen as "childish" and "too playful," stating that the visual design "prevents serious engagement with financial content." Mohammad (23) went further: *"feels like you're just taking them, putting them into a child seat"* — the aesthetic communicating an assumption that users can't be trusted with serious content. Althea (19) identified the tension precisely: *"there should be a degree of seriousness — it's an important topic."* For these participants, playful design signals low stakes; financial education requires the opposite signal.

It is important to note that not all Young Adults rejected the design. Enoch (18) found it appealing and appropriate. Mia (25) responded warmly to the "simple, cute aesthetic." The credibility ceiling is most pronounced among participants who are further along toward financial independence and who bring a more serious, functional relationship with money to the experience. But for product purposes, the most sceptical users define the ceiling — and for Young Adults, that scepticism is meaningful enough to require a design response.

## 6.3 The Monster Characters

The monsters are the most debated element of the visual design and the point where the age-gap challenge is most acute. Their role is threefold: they are the collection mechanic (progress reward), the narrative vehicle in lessons (bringing financial scenarios to life), and the app's brand identity — the name is literally Money Monsters.

For Young Teens, the monsters work well. The collection concept is understood and motivating. Lottie (13) responded to the competitive collecting dynamic. Isaac (12) found monsters made the app feel more interesting than a plain interface. The association with collecting, competing, and unlocking aligns with the broader gaming sensibility of the 12–14 age group.

For Older Teens and most Young Adults, the monster aesthetic is the single most cited reason for the design feeling "childish." The principle this suggests is not that monsters should be removed — the collection mechanic and the progression logic they embody are sound — but that their visual execution needs to mature alongside the audience. Ovie (15) suggested badges as an alternative reward format for older users. Zain (20, YA) suggested money-themed tokens (coins) as a more credible equivalent. Aiden (15) preferred that the primary monster character be introduced and named earlier, creating attachment rather than confusion. The collection mechanic is wanted; the specific character design needs to be segment-sensitive.

One specific design problem emerged from Aiden's session: confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it. The principle extracted — independent of this specific design — is that the player/learner's relationship to in-app characters must be clearly established and consistently maintained. Ambiguity about identity within the learning experience breaks the scenario logic and reduces engagement.

## 6.4 Structural Visual Conventions: What Works

Beneath the character design debate, the structural visual conventions of the app commanded broad, cross-segment approval. The level-map layout — a vertically arranged sequence of lessons with locked/unlocked states — was understood by every participant and described positively by almost all. The visual distinction between current, available, and locked content was clear. The implied promise of progressive difficulty and reward for completion was motivating across all three segments.

Olivia (14) recognised game-like level progression as "making you want to keep going." Enoch (18) understood the progression logic immediately — *"green = next, gray = locked"* — and found the sequential completion structure reassuring rather than restrictive. Zain (20, YA) liked the bite-sized, unlockable chunk format even while finding the aesthetic too young.

The implication is significant: the app does not need to change its structural UX to serve older users. It needs a visual layer that can express the same structural logic in an aesthetic appropriate to each segment. A shared architecture with age-differentiated visual skins is the right direction — not a fundamental redesign of the learning model.

## 6.5 The Core Design Tension: One App, Multiple Audiences

The central finding from the visual comprehension work is that the app's current aesthetic is optimised for one segment and creates increasing friction for the other two. This is not a criticism of the current design — it is an inherent challenge in building a single product for users who span from 12 to 25.

The design choices that lower the barrier to entry for a 13-year-old are precisely the choices that raise the credibility barrier for a 23-year-old. There is no single aesthetic that satisfies both ends of the spectrum simultaneously. The practical implication for launch: **Money Monsters needs to be clear about which segment it is primarily designing for.**

If Young Teens are the launch bullseye — which the research supports as the strongest product-market fit — the current aesthetic direction is broadly right. Incremental maturation (less toy-like character design, slightly more sophisticated colour palette, reduced cartoon-heavy iconography) will address the edges of the Older Teen segment without alienating Young Teens. If the product aspires to serve Young Adults credibly, a meaningfully different visual presentation is required — not just tweaks, but a different aesthetic register. The same content, different skin; the structural model is sound enough to support it.

# 7. Onboarding

## 7.1 What the Onboarding Experience Involved

The research stimulus included a short quiz-based onboarding sequence designed to assess participants' financial knowledge and attitudes before directing them into personalised lesson content. The quiz comprised behavioural and scenario-based questions about money situations and spending habits, guided by Mr. Money — a human character who introduced the quiz and framed it as a way to understand the participant's starting point, not to test their competence. Upon completion, participants received a brief personalised welcome screen before entering the lesson flow.

*Note: the version of onboarding used in fieldwork was a research stimulus designed to convey the concept of personalised onboarding — not a final product specification. Participant responses reveal principles about what effective onboarding requires; they should not be read as direct feedback on final question content or quiz length.*

## 7.2 What the Research Reveals About Onboarding Design

### 7.2.1 Tone and non-judgment: broadly effective across all segments

The non-judgmental framing of the onboarding quiz received the most consistent positive response of any single design element in the research. Across all 16 participants, the principle that the quiz is assessing "where you are" — not grading whether you pass — landed clearly. This mattered most for participants carrying financial anxiety or low confidence, precisely those the app most needs to serve.

Enoch (18) appreciated that the interface explained *"we're only trying to see where you're at."* Mohammad (23) noted the reassuring message that some questions might seem challenging, connecting this to the broader reality that admitting financial difficulty carries social stigma: *"you wouldn't want to admit to someone else that you're having money problems."* Althea (19) described personalisation as exactly what she wanted — understanding her situation before tailoring content.

The principle: onboarding must signal from the first screen that it is not a test with right and wrong answers, but a calibration. This framing is not cosmetic — it determines whether participants with high financial anxiety are willing to answer honestly. Honest answers produce better personalisation; better personalisation produces more relevant content; more relevant content produces engagement. The non-judgmental tone is the gateway to everything that follows.

### 7.2.2 Length and the personalisation payoff

Responses to quiz length divided by segment. Young Teens broadly found the length appropriate — Sam and Lottie both described it as "just the right length," consistent with their shorter attention threshold. Older Teens and Young Adults consistently wanted more: Mia (25) found the quiz "too short to adequately assess her needs"; Mohammad (23) noted it "has the potential to be a bit longer"; Grace (19) could have handled 2-3 additional questions; Zain (20, YA) wanted more depth about spending habits and future goals.

The principle here is not simply that more questions are better. The underlying issue is that a short quiz cannot generate the signal needed to meaningfully differentiate content pathways — particularly for Young Adults whose financial situations are complex and varied. Several participants described finishing the onboarding without feeling that what followed was genuinely calibrated to their answers. When onboarding promises personalisation, the content that follows must visibly deliver it. If the quiz doesn't demonstrably change what users see, it becomes a friction point with no payoff.

The implication: quiz length should be calibrated to the segment's tolerance and the personalisation model it needs to serve. Young Teens benefit from brevity (5–7 questions). Older Teens and Young Adults can absorb more (8–12 questions) and will accept the investment if the personalisation payoff is visible and credible.

### 7.2.3 Question design principles

The question types that performed best across segments were **behavioural and situational** — questions about how participants actually behave with money, not what they know. Ovie (15) appreciated that questions revealed "what type of person you are with money." Levi (17) preferred scenario-based questions over direct knowledge tests. Althea (19) stated explicitly that *"knowing a definition doesn't mean you have relevant experience or real-world need for it"* — and therefore preferred questions that assessed situation over terminology.

Issues arose where specific question content used financial product names unfamiliar to younger participants (ISAs, cryptocurrency terminology), or where scenario amounts were unrealistic for a younger participant's actual income level. The principle extracted: **onboarding questions should use age-appropriate amounts, terminology, and scenarios.** A question about handling a £500 windfall lands differently for a 13-year-old than a 23-year-old. Onboarding questions should ideally be drawn from segment-calibrated pools, or dynamically scaled based on early-response signals.

A further principle emerged from Althea's session: participants wanted to understand *why* each question was being asked. Brief contextual framing per question — "this helps us understand your saving habits" — increases perceived relevance and likely increases honesty. The quiz is a conversation, not a form.

## 7.3 From Onboarding to Personalised App Experience

### 7.3.1 Universal design requirements

Across all segments, effective onboarding needs to satisfy four conditions:

**1. Establish non-judgment from the first screen.** The tone set in onboarding determines whether participants with financial anxiety are willing to reveal their actual situation. The framing — that the app is learning about the user, not assessing them — should be explicit, not implied.

**2. Make the purpose of the quiz clear.** Participants want to understand the value of the quiz before completing it. A brief statement upfront — "we'll use your answers to choose content that's relevant to where you are right now" — increases motivation to complete it honestly, particularly among older participants who are skeptical of generic educational tools.

**3. Use situational questions, not knowledge tests.** Scenario-based and behavioural questions consistently outperformed direct financial knowledge tests across all segments in producing relevant, honest responses. Questions like "when you get money, what do you typically do first?" reveal more about a participant's real needs than "do you know what an ISA is?"

**4. Deliver visibly differentiated content.** The personalisation promise is only credible if participants can see that different answers produce different starting points. If the quiz doesn't change what users see, it becomes friction with no value — and will breed scepticism about the app's ability to serve them.

### 7.3.2 Onboarding design by segment

**Young Teens:** Keep the quiz short (5–7 questions). Focus on money personality questions (spender vs saver tendencies, what motivates saving) and near-term goal questions (what are you currently saving for?). Avoid financial product terminology. Use amounts and scenarios from their actual financial world: small income, social spending, gift money. The guide character should feel close to the participant's own age — not an adult authority figure. The onboarding should feel like meeting a knowledgeable friend, not taking a test.

**Older Teens:** A slightly longer quiz (7–10 questions) is appropriate and expected. Include questions that acknowledge real income: "do you currently earn money?" opens different content pathways. Questions about impending transitions — sixth form vs apprenticeship, first accounts, plans for after school — allow the app to surface genuinely relevant content rather than defaulting to basics. The character framing should shift toward peer-level: someone figuring it out alongside them.

**Young Adults:** Prioritise life-stage and behavioural questions over knowledge tests. Key dimensions to surface: living independently or supported (renting? home?), student or employed, current financial pain point. These dimensions drive genuinely different content priorities — a Young Adult managing credit card debt needs different first lessons from one who is building a savings buffer. The quiz should feel like the beginning of a conversation; the guide character should feel like a peer with relevant experience, not an instructor. The payoff from completing onboarding — visibly tailored content from the first session — is non-negotiable for this segment's continued engagement.

# 8. Lesson Design: Learning Mechanics and Comprehension

## 8.1 The Creative Vehicle: Dialogue vs. Lecture

The research stimulus used a dialogue-based lesson format: financial scenarios played out through character conversations, with the learner prompted to help a character make a decision, choose between options, or identify the right approach. This format stands in deliberate contrast to the more common educational pattern of information-then-quiz: present facts, then test recall.

### 8.1.1 Why the dialogue/scenario format is resonating

The scenario format works because it inverts the typical relationship between information and decision. In a lecture model, the user is given information and then asked to recall it. In the dialogue model, the user is presented with a situation and asked to reason through it — the information emerges from the reasoning process rather than preceding it. This creates two advantages that were consistently visible across participants.

First, it reduces the felt difficulty of the content. Levi (17) noted he "always found a relatable answer among the options" in scenario questions. Sam (14) appreciated that questions felt "basic but at the same time, stuff that your parents haven't told you about." The scenario format makes financial content feel navigable because participants reason from their own experience rather than from memorised definitions.

Second, it creates emotional engagement that pure information delivery cannot. Enoch (18) contrasted the app's approach with a platform that "throws information at you" — the dialogue format felt like a conversation rather than a download. Levi (17) specifically appreciated the "help this person" framing over a direct quiz format: helping someone else make a decision is a lower-stakes framing than being tested on whether you know the right answer yourself.

The principle: the dialogue/scenario format should be the primary creative vehicle for Money Monsters lessons. This is not a stylistic preference — it is the mechanism by which the app creates the conditions for the knowing–doing gap to close. Presenting information in the context of a decision is categorically more effective than presenting information before a decision.

### 8.1.2 The role of character-based scenarios

Characters serve a function beyond visual appeal: they provide the emotional and cognitive distance that makes it easier to engage with difficult financial situations. "Help this person" is a less threatening framing than "what would you do?" — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment. Mohammad (23) captured this precisely: *"adding different third persons… attaches you towards the subject."*

The research revealed a specific design principle about character consistency. Aiden (15) described confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it — a character identity problem that broke the scenario logic. The principle: lesson characters must have stable, clearly established identities that persist throughout a session. The learner should always know whether they are inhabiting the character or advising one. Mixing these positions — even unintentionally — undermines the decision-making frame that makes the format effective.

A second principle concerns character relevance. Sam (14) was drawn to Mr. Money because a human character who has "done this as well" carries more credibility than one simply presented as knowledgeable. Isaac (12) wanted a guide closer to his own age. For scenarios that participants are asked to navigate, the characters' age, income level, and life situation should approximate those of the target user. A café worker saving for headphones is a credible scenario for Young Teens. For older segments, scenarios need to reflect the actual financial complexity of their lives — rent decisions, credit card choices, income from real employment — otherwise the learning feels abstract rather than rehearsal for a real situation.

## 8.2 Question Design

### 8.2.1 How questions are presented: what works

The most effective question formats across segments shared several characteristics: **scenario framing, relatable answer options, appropriate contextual detail, and an outcome orientation** (what should this person do?) rather than a knowledge orientation (what is the correct definition of X?).

Scenario questions — where participants are placed in a situation and asked to reason through a decision — consistently outperformed direct knowledge tests. Levi (17) preferred "a mate talking about crypto" scenarios over formal financial knowledge questions. Enoch (18) valued the explanatory context provided in brackets alongside each answer option, noting they were "particularly helpful for understanding the choices." Sam (14) appreciated that questions felt realistic and didn't make him feel he was being tested on things he should already know.

The quality of answer options matters as much as the question itself. Effective multiple choice requires that all options be plausible — not that one answer is obviously right and three are obviously wrong. When wrong answers are too easily dismissed, the question has no learning value; the participant is pattern-matching rather than reasoning. The most effective questions in the research stimulus were those where participants had to genuinely consider before choosing, and where choosing the wrong answer felt informative rather than humiliating.

### 8.2.2 Try-again and feedback mechanics

The retry mechanism — where a wrong answer is not simply marked incorrect and moved past, but requires the participant to engage again — was one of the most consistently appreciated mechanics in the app. Enoch (18) valued that "the app doesn't move on until the question is answered correctly." The principle this embodies is sound: in financial education, the goal is not to score a pass but to understand a concept well enough to apply it. Allowing users to move on after a wrong answer without understanding why produces a false sense of completion — the learning that could have happened didn't.

The explanation provided after both correct and incorrect answers adds further value. Mia (25) appreciated "explanations provided after both correct and incorrect answers." The principle: every question should end with understanding, not just a score. Whether the answer was right or wrong, the feedback screen should include a brief, plain-language explanation of the underlying principle — and that explanation should feel like a natural continuation of the conversation, not a teacher marking a test.

### 8.2.3 UX principles for learning: keeping reference information accessible

A specific and actionable UX problem emerged from multiple participants: reference information presented earlier in a lesson was no longer visible when participants reached the question that required it. Althea (19) described reading about compound interest and being unable to recall the specific detail when asked to apply it moments later. Mohammad (23) described closing explanation overlays quickly and then being unable to reference them when answering. Both identified the same structural failure: the lesson treated comprehension and recall as equivalent, when they require different design affordances.

The principle: **relevant reference information should remain visible — or immediately accessible — at the point of decision.** The question screen should show, or link directly to, the key fact being tested. Designing for perfect retention assumes an ideal user who doesn't exist. Most participants are completing lessons on mobile, with divided attention, in short bursts. Designing for accessible reference respects actual use conditions; designing for recall assumes conditions that rarely hold.

A secondary UX principle: where content spans multiple screens, the critical decision-relevant detail should be on the screen where the decision is made — not the screen before it. Mohammad noted this specifically: *"where content is split over a couple of screens, it's really important that the key detail is on the second screen."*

## 8.3 Lesson Length and Session Duration

### 8.3.1 Current length: broadly appropriate for YT/OT; format mismatch for YA

The lesson length in the research stimulus — approximately 60–90 seconds per lesson — was broadly appropriate for Young Teens and most Older Teens, and insufficient for Young Adults.

For Young Teens, the bite-sized format matches both attention span and usage pattern. Isaac (12) believed daily engagement was possible. Sam (14) suggested slightly longer lessons (one additional question) but found the overall format right. The short-session structure creates the habit loop that makes consistent engagement plausible for this group.

For Older Teens, the format holds — with nuance. Levi (17) found lesson length appropriate and envisioned "every other day for 10 minutes" as a realistic usage pattern. Ovie (15) would use daily with notifications. The expectation of daily use may be optimistic for this segment; every-other-day engagement is a more realistic planning assumption.

For Young Adults, the format creates a specific frustration: lessons end before the concept is adequately explored. Mohammad (23) described individual lessons as "too short for even a bite-size chunk," wanting 2–3 minute sessions that combine multiple current lessons into a coherent conceptual unit. Zain (20) described the content as "scraping the top of things." Althea (19) and Grace (19) both described a situational rather than daily usage pattern — reaching for the app when a specific financial question arose, not as a daily habit.

The principle: lesson length is not a single design decision. It is a function of segment, usage model, and content depth required. For Young Teens, short-and-frequent is the right model. For Young Adults, the question is whether to lengthen individual sessions (supporting a habit model with deeper engagement) or to shift toward a situational reference architecture (supporting pull-based usage but requiring a different content structure). These are different product decisions that the team needs to make deliberately — the right answer likely differs by segment.

### 8.3.2 The mobile context: commuting, idle time, short-burst use

Across all segments, participants described usage as fundamentally mobile and opportunistic. Enoch (18) described the lesson length as "perfect for bus journeys." Sam (14) envisioned checking the app in the morning as part of a daily routine. Ovie (15) connected daily engagement to notification-driven habit formation. The usage context shapes the conditions under which learning must occur — and the design must serve those conditions, not hypothetical ones.

Mobile, short-burst learning has a specific implication for content design: **every lesson must be self-contained.** A user who opens the app on a commute and completes one lesson should leave with a complete, usable piece of knowledge — not a fragment of a concept that requires the next lesson to make sense. The current structure broadly achieves this, but the quality of the "complete thought" per lesson needs to match the depth expectation of the segment completing it. For Young Teens, a single concrete principle (the 50/30/20 rule, explained and applied) is a complete thought. For Young Adults, the same principle without its mechanism, real-world implications, and at least one worked example is not.
