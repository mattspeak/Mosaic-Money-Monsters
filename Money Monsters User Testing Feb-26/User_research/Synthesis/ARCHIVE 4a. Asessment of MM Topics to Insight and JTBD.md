It gives a quick read on where the curriculum has natural fit — and where it needs reframing, depth adjustment, or is simply not relevant to a given age group. Section 4.2 provides lesson-level detail.

 ## 4.2 Topic Breadth and Depth

### 4.2.1 Lesson-level assessment: appropriate depth and framing by segment

For each level of the existing lesson plan, the tables below assess appropriate depth and framing by segment. Where a topic was not raised in research sessions or falls outside participants' current financial reality, this is noted as **Not appropriate**.

---

**Level 1 — Money Basics**

| Lesson | Young Teens | Older Teens | Young Adults |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1–1.2 What is money / money history | Appropriate as engaging entry point | Not appropriate | Not appropriate |
| 1.3 How we use money | Appropriate — earns/saves/spends framing works | Not appropriate | Not appropriate |
| 1.4 Why money matters | Appropriate — goal-setting foundation | Not appropriate | Not appropriate |
| 1.5–1.6 Needs vs wants | **High priority.** Core YT theme; directly evidenced across all seven YT participants | Revision only | Not appropriate |
| 1.7 Making smart money choices | Appropriate — habits framing works | Not appropriate | Not appropriate |

*YT framing:* Ground all scenarios in small amounts (£5–20), social spending moments, and near-term purchase goals. Money history works as fun world-building rather than core learning.

### 4.2.2 Lessons to advance, retire, or gate by segment

**Advance immediately** — high demand, clear knowledge gap, directly evidenced in research:
- **L1.5–1.6 Needs vs wants** *(YT)* — the single most universal pain point; evidenced across all seven Young Teen participants
- **L2.3 Bank account types** *(YT/OT)* — direct evidence in both segments; live decision for OT
- **L2.7/L6.2 Credit cards** *(YA)* — minimum payment and autopay mechanics; Mohammad's scenario is a natural lesson foundation
- **L4.2 The 50/30/20 rule** *(OT)* — landed with both Aiden and Levi; immediately usable heuristic for real earnings
- **L4.7 SMART goals** *(YT/OT)* — every YT participant had a specific saving goal; first earnings give OT a meaningful context
- **L5.3 Interest rates / compound interest** *(YA)* — Grace's reaction is the clearest "aha" moment in the research
- **L5.4 Specialised savings accounts** *(OT/YA)* — Aiden, Ovie, Althea, and Grace all flag explicit knowledge gaps
- **L5.5 ISAs** *(YA)* — Zain actively researching; Althea saving inefficiently without knowing ISAs exist
- **L6.5–6.6 Credit scores and building credit** *(OT/YA)* — approaching first credit products; readiness gap universal across both segments

**Develop but frame with care:**
- **L8 Investing** *(all segments)* — high curiosity but requires segment calibration. YT: conceptual demystification only. OT: decision-readiness, not an instruction to act. YA: actionable with honest risk framing. Do not skip it; do not overclaim.
- **L2.10/L6.8 Scams and fraud** *(all segments)* — content should escalate in sophistication by segment. Avoid checklist framing; build pattern recognition. OT is the highest-stakes delivery challenge.
- **L7.3/L7.7 Life stage planning and major life events** *(OT/YA)* — relevant but requires realistic scenario anchoring; do not make abstract.

**Gate or deprioritise for launch:**
- **L3 Currency and travel** *(all)* — not evidenced in research; not a priority job for any segment. Can remain as optional or bonus content
- **L7.4 Retirement planning** *(all)* — no emotional proximity for any participant; candidate for a later premium module
- **L7.6 Estate planning** *(all)* — not appropriate for any age band in scope
- **L8.3 Bonds / L8.6 Portfolio building** *(YT/OT)* — too far removed from current financial reality to be credible or useful
- **L5.8 Financial protection schemes** *(all)* — not raised by any participant; deferred until evidence of need emerges
