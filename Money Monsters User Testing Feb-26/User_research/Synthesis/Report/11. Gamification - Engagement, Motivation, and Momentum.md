# 10. Gamification: Engagement, Motivation, and Momentum

## What Gamification Features Are in the App

Multiple participants across all three segments compared the app to Duolingo without prompting. The gamification architecture has four elements:

- **Monster collection** — completing a lesson unlocks a named character (Sammy Savings, Tracy Tracking, etc.) representing a financial theme
- **Progression map** — locked and unlocked lessons arranged in a visual path
- **Streaks** — consecutive-day tracking that makes continuity its own reward
- **Character guidance** — Mr. Money and lesson-specific characters provide pedagogical scaffolding, with headroom to be gamified further

## What's Working
{include following as 3 headers on a single slide}

### Monster collection: incentive that lands with Young Teens

- Collection provides a concrete reward structure — an object to acquire, a collection to build — that translates lesson completion into something tangible. Sam (14): *"makes it seem like you're working towards something."*
- The monsters are distinct named characters rather than generic badges, giving the collection texture beyond points accumulation.
- Appeal has some cross-segment reach — Enoch (18) and Mia (25) were both positive — but Young Teens are where the mechanic functions most reliably as a motivator.

### Progress and progression maps: understood and motivating

- The visual path with locked and unlocked states communicates structure, progress, and aspiration simultaneously.
- Enoch (18) understood it as part of a coherent gamified system; Zain (20) appreciated the *"bite-sized, unlockable chunk layout."*
- Locked content functions as aspiration rather than frustration — participants noticed what was coming and used it to orient themselves.

### Streaks: recognised, but contingent on habits that may not exist

- Duolingo has done the education: no participant needed the mechanic explained. Recognition was universal.
- Younger participants were enthusiastic — but their confidence about daily use should be read cautiously; habitual engagement is easy to imagine in a session and harder to sustain in practice.
- For older users, engagement is more likely to be situational — triggered by a financial question or moment, not a daily routine. Grace (19): *"assumes I already have these kind of daily habits."*
- The streak mechanic rewards exactly the behaviour that is least likely for a significant portion of the audience. Users who engage situationally will break streaks, lose momentum, and have less reason to return.
- This is the sharpest expression of a broader tension the product needs to resolve — addressed in [[7. The Key Design and Product Tensions|Section 7]].

## What's Missing or Underdeveloped
{include following as headers on a single slide}

### Expanding gamification: a clear expectation from all segments

- The single most consistent finding is that the current gamification layer is insufficient. Participants import the full Duolingo mental model and find the current implementation shallow against that benchmark.
- The gap takes different forms by segment: deeper gameplay for Young Teens; XP with jeopardy for Older Teens (Enoch, 18); content and leaderboard access for Young Adults (Zain, 20).
- The common thread: completion doesn't feel sufficiently valuable.

### Consequence-based gamification: desire for more purpose in collecting monsters

- The structural problem: monsters are earned but, once collected, they do nothing.
- Althea (19): *"Monster Collected"* felt like a gimmick because she *"didn't understand where it was leading"* — she wanted gamification that *"builds toward something meaningful, not just awards trophies."*
- Zain (20) expected monsters to *"unlock app features, avatar customisations, or leaderboard abilities."*
- Whether the second layer means trading, upgrading, or content-gating matters less than the principle: collection without consequence exhausts itself quickly.

### Social and competitive features: leaderboards and peer comparison

- The social layer is effectively absent — flagged as a significant gap across all three segments.
- Levi (17): *"competitive elements with friends — comparing streaks, earning more monsters"*; Ovie (15): *"a competitive element to collect more than friends"*; Zain (20): *"public leaderboards."*
- Levi made the dependency explicit: *"If other people use it, I would definitely start using it."* Social proof isn't just a feature preference — it's a description of the adoption mechanism.
- Without a social layer, retention falls entirely on content and individual mechanics: a harder load to carry.

## Where Gamification Fails
{include following as 2 headers on a single slide}

### Young Teens: desire for greater gameplay depth

- The failure for Young Teens is not rejection but disappointment — this is the segment most aligned with the monster-collection model, yet the current layer falls short of their expectations.
- Isaac (12) — enthusiastic and generous in his rating — wanted gameplay as *the priority*: spendable points, monster trading, map-based mini-games with financial consequences.
- Lottie (13) arrived from a different direction: the text-heavy format places too much weight on mechanics that aren't deep enough to carry it, in direct competition with TikTok and Instagram.
- The risk: Young Teens engage briefly, find the game layer too thin, and don't return — not because they disliked the concept, but because the execution didn't match the proposition.

### Older Teens: aesthetic registers younger than the audience

- The primary failure for Older Teens is aesthetic: the visual framing reads younger than the segment.
- Aiden (15) perceived the app as *"designed for primary school children"* and would not continue in current form.
- Ovie (15) separated mechanics from aesthetics cleanly: she wanted the collecting impulse preserved but the surface changed — *"replace monsters with badges or more age-appropriate rewards."*
- Levi (17) was more tolerant but still noted the design *"feels targeted at 12–14 rather than 16–18."*
- The fix is an age-up that preserves the motivational architecture without compromising audience credibility.

### Young Adults: narrower appeal — and that is okay with the right positioning

- Most Young Adults won't respond to the current gamification style — but the right response is precise positioning, not alarm.
- Grace (19, 4/10): game elements were *"inappropriate for a 19-year-old"* and blocked engagement with financial content. Mohammad (23): *"feels like you're just taking them, putting them into a child seat."*
- Althea (19) identified the subtler version: not that gamification is wrong, but that the current implementation is decorative rather than purposeful — *"there should be a degree of seriousness — it's an important topic."*
- The segment is not monolithic: Enoch (18) was broadly positive, and Mia (25) loved the collection system — spontaneously citing Plum (4M+ users) as evidence that warm, character-driven design can work for older users when the underlying utility is clear.
- The recommendation is not to chase broad Young Adult appeal — that would require compromising the product's core identity. A Money Monsters that leans into its personality with intent can find a genuine audience in this cohort. It will be a smaller slice, and that's a position worth owning.
