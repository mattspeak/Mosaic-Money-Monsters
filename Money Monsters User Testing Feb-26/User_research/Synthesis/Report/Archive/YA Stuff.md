
PORTRAITS

## Young Adults (18–25) — The Archetype

**Aisha, 21.** Aisha left school at 18 and has been in the same admin role for two years — steady income, living at home, saving slowly toward moving out. One account, one small savings pot, no debt. She keeps a rough mental track of her balance. She's never had a crisis. She's never had a plan.

She uses Duolingo most nights before sleep — not because she's serious about Spanish, but because it closes the day, a small streak she won't break. She connects with the soft, cute characters as a welcome break from the seriousness of other daily apps.

**One moment that stayed.** A credit card charge she hadn't anticipated. A month that got tight faster than expected. She didn't talk about it. But it's still there: *I should probably understand this better.*

---
### Learning Style

**Passive accumulation, not active study.** Aisha isn't looking for a course. She's looking for something that makes her feel quietly smarter without the effort of trying. Bite-sized scenario, real decision, three minutes. She will not maintain a habit that requires effort to start. She will maintain one that costs nothing to continue.

**Decompression is the access point.** The moment an app feels like serious concentration rather than something to pass 10mins, it's over. The learning only lands when it's wrapped in something she'd open.

---
### Relationship with Money

**Switched off by finance.** Every time she's tried to engage properly, something has failed her. The YouTube video assumed a salary she doesn't have. The Martin Lewis article needed three prior articles to understand. Nothing has made financial knowledge feel like something that accumulates.

**Arm's-length by habit.** Aisha's baseline is mild avoidance. Engaging with detail usually leads to confusion or background anxiety, so she keeps it at distance. She knows roughly what's in her account. She's not in trouble. She's just not in control.

**Readiness, not wealth.** Financial competence for Aisha isn't about getting rich. It's about not being caught out. Not going blank when someone mentions pensions or how to not get stung by a credit card.

**Every mainstream channel has failed her.** Google gives too much or too little. YouTube assumes an income she doesn't have. ChatGPT comes closest — specific, tailored — but the knowledge doesn't accumulate. Each session starts from scratch.

---
### Money Monsters' Appeal

**She arrives in the right mode.** On the sofa, not at her desk. Her fluency with Duolingo means the game frame isn't a barrier — it's what makes the format legible. She already knows what daily accumulation feels like.

**The mechanic *is* the habit.** Bitesized lessons and nudges accumulates without administration, the next session costs nothing to begin. That's precisely where every other financial format has failed her.

**A product problem, not a user problem.** Aisha isn't disengaged from money. She's disengaged from every format it's been handed to her in — and that's the specific gap this app is positioned to close.
