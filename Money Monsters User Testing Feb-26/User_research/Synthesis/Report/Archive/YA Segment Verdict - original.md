## Young Adults (18–25): Right Content, Wrong Vehicle

### What's working

- **Scenario content resonates.** Compound interest, credit card behaviour, savings account yield — specific lesson content was valued precisely because these are live concerns, not hypothetical ones
- **A minority found genuine appeal.** Enoch (18) broadly positive; Mia (25) responded warmly to monster collection, citing Plum as a comparable product — rejection is not universal; charm can work when underlying utility is clear
- **Proposition has reach.** "Money Stuff Nobody Explains" connected with gap-aware participants; "Train Your Money Brain" with those focused on behaviour change — both work; generic educational framing did not
- **Content topics match real urgency.** Credit mechanics, debt traps, hidden costs, savings products — Young Adults face these as live situations, not future preparations

### Structural misfit: the delivery model doesn't match the usage pattern

- Money Monsters is built for daily sequential use — streaks, locked progression, and incremental completion all assume a user who shows up every day and works through content in order
- Young Adults use financial content situationally: Grace (19) wanted to "predict money situations before they happen"; Althea (19) would reach for the app "when a specific financial question arose"; Zain (20) wanted to jump directly to relevant content
- **Streaks penalise absence; locked progression blocks situational users** — both mechanics serve habit-forming engagement and fail pull-based engagement
- The product is designed for *what do I unlock next?*; Young Adults arrive asking *does this apply to me right now?* — only one of these is currently served

### What a YA-appropriate product could look like

- **Add a situational entry point** alongside the daily-use features — topic index, searchable glossary, or "what do you need today?" routing so users can jump to what's relevant to them
- **Make the visuals feel more grown-up** without abandoning the brand — playfulness should feel intentional, not childish
- **Deepen content** for situations Young Adults already face: minimum payment traps, 0% rates that revert, BNPL mechanics, credit score implications
- **Lead with credibility signals** — demonstrate serious financial expertise before asking users to trust the format
- **Give collection mechanics purpose** — rewards that unlock content or features, not decorative trophies

### Launch readiness rating and conditions

**Not the primary launch audience — but a viable niche with the right positioning.**

- The recommendation is not to chase broad Young Adult appeal — that would require compromising the product's core identity. A Money Monsters that leans into its personality with intent can find a genuine audience in this cohort. It will be a smaller slice, and that's a position worth owning.
