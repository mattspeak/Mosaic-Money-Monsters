## Young Teens (13–15)

13 respondents: 7 UK (Isaac, Lottie, Norah, Sam, Zain, Hugo, Olivia), 6 CZ (Franta, Jaroslav, Ladislav, Lily, Marie, Veronika). Ladislav: no card sort, interests noted separately.

| Job | Most Important | Medium | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manage my money wisely / make it last | 10/12 | 2/12 | Universal — strongest signal in the segment. Impulse control in social situations (canteen, spending with friends) rather than abstract budgeting |
| Save toward a meaningful goal | 9/12 | 2/12 | Near-term concrete targets (console, phone, trainers, Korea trip). Hugo (UK) only Don't Care — prefers buffer over named goal |
| Feel confident and not stressed or embarrassed about money | 3/12 | 5/12 | Isaac (UK), Franta + Veronika (CZ) Very Important; split otherwise. Shame framing ("not feel silly") dominant at this age |
| Fill knowledge gaps / understand money in ways relevant to me | 5/12 | 3/12 | Three overlapping cards: personalised explanation (5/12 VI, CZ-weighted); master what others find confusing (3/12 VI, 7/12 KI); understand adults talking about money (3/12 VI, 5/12 KI) |
| Protect myself from financial traps and manipulation | — | — | Not in YT card set. Low signal — implicit in wanting to understand what others find confusing, but not a named job at this age. Ladislav (CZ, no card sort) mentioned scams as an informal interest |
| Get a head start on peers *(Segment Specific JTBD)* | 4/12 | 7/12 | UK-weighted — Zain + Hugo Very Important; CZ lean lower, some explicit rejection of competitive framing. Not present in OT or YA |
| Not be stressed by money talk at home *(Segment Specific JTBD)* | 2/12 | 4/12 | Environmentally triggered — household money anxiety. Norah (UK) + Marie (CZ) Very Important; majority Don't Care |

### What the most important jobs tell us

**Make money last / control spending** — the most universal job in the entire YT segment. Every respondent who sorted cards rated it Very Important or Kinda Important; no one placed it in Don't Care. The core tension is impulse control in social situations — spending with friends, the canteen, small habitual purchases — rather than abstract budgeting. Zain (UK) describes losing £11 "without thinking" with friends; Lottie is "very impulse driven"; Ladislav (CZ, no card sort) cited it as a primary interest. The job is the same cross-market; the specific scenarios differ only slightly.

**Save toward something specific** — top-ranked across UK and close behind in CZ. Every participant had a concrete, near-term goal (console, phone, trainers, Korea trip, sports equipment). Hugo (UK) is the only Don't Care — his preference is to maintain a buffer rather than save toward specific targets, which is a distinct but related orientation. Abstract future-focused saving lands poorly; connecting lessons to a named goal the participant already has is the activation key.

**Get a head start on peers** — secondary but meaningful, with a clear market split. In the UK, Zain and Hugo rate it Very Important — for Zain it's about positioning himself ahead for a future business; for Hugo it's about knowing more than peers about investing. In CZ, the job barely clears Kinda Important and is explicitly rejected by Lily and Ladislav as irrelevant. CZ YTs are motivated by personalised relevance, not competitive status — this has direct implications for how gamification and social comparison mechanics are framed for each market.

**Explain money in a relevant / personalised way** — rated Very Important by five respondents, with CZ weighting (Jaroslav, Marie, Veronika). Norah and Hugo in the UK also rate it highly. The need is for content that connects directly to their specific situation — their goal, their income, their spending context — not generic financial principles. In CZ, this is the alternative motivational driver to peer competition.

## Older Teens (16–18)

11 respondents: 5 UK (Aiden, Ariana, Dulcie, Levi, Ovie), 6 CZ (Honza, Jan, Katka, Lukáš, Niky, Vilma). Only Levi (UK) has a full formal sort; UK data for others is stated priorities.

| Job | Most Important | Medium | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manage my money wisely / make it last | 10/11 | 1/11 | Near-universal. Shift from pocket money to real income — part-time earnings, zero-hours, Vinted. Jan (CZ) only Kinda Important. Also: make money last longer (CZ-only card: 3/6 VI, 3/6 KI — Jan, Katka, Lukáš) |
| Save toward a meaningful goal | 2/6 | 3/6 | CZ-only card ("save toward a significant goal without giving up"). Honza + Jan Very Important; Niky Don't Care. Not in UK card set for this segment. UK verbal evidence (all 5 OT UK) confirms this is not a priority: Levi keeps money "available for opportunities" rather than targeting named goals; Ovie has no long-term saving goals — automated micro-saving only; Aiden's past saving (PC, DJ decks) is complete and he deprioritises it for now ("can learn later"); Ariana's Monzo account is rarely used, current goal is a £30 school bag; Dulcie has no saving habits or active goals. Saving toward goals has been displaced by managing real income at this life stage. |
| Feel confident and not stressed or embarrassed about money | 6/11 | 4/11 | Ariana + Ovie (UK) stated Very Important; Dulcie (UK) Kinda Important. 4/6 CZ Very Important, connected to approaching independence within 1–3 years |
| Fill knowledge gaps / understand money in ways relevant to me | 5/11 | 4/11 | Named specific topics — credit, student loans (Ariana, UK); RPSN/APR, compound interest, credit registries (CZ). Surfaces even for self-rated confident participants |
| Protect myself from financial traps and manipulation | 9/11 | 1/11 | Highest-consensus job in the segment. CZ unanimous (6/6 Very Important). Also: recognise bad value / being overcharged (CZ-only card: 2/6 VI, 3/6 KI — Niky + Vilma). Levi (UK) only Don't Care |
| Prepare for living independently *(Segment Specific JTBD)* | 1/8 | 6/8 | Transitional — independence 1–3 years away for most. Majority Kinda Important; Ariana (UK) Very Important (imminent). Katka Don't Care |

### What the most important jobs tell us

**Smart money management** — the dominant job for the segment, cross-market. The shift from YT is clear: this is no longer about pocket money. These respondents have part-time earnings, zero-hours contracts, Vinted income, or cash from chores — and the job is to make those real earnings count. Levi earns £12/hour and wants to ensure it goes on useful things. Aiden has saved for a PC and DJ decks and frames smart choices as avoiding decisions that "destroy" his financial position. All CZ OT respondents with income rank it Very Important.

**Scam protection** — the highest-consensus card across the entire CZ OT group (6/6 Very Important) and confirmed as a top-three priority for the majority of UK OT. The lone outlier is Levi, who rated it Don't Care — he doesn't feel he makes financial mistakes and this feels distant. All other UK OT respondents who stated priorities placed it in their top three. Aiden was influenced by YouTube content about scammers; Dulcie has a strong aversion to being ripped off; Ovie recognises scams are sometimes non-obvious. In CZ, Honza's teacher was scammed; Jan's grandmother works in insurance and emphasised security; the school IT curriculum includes scam awareness for several participants.

**Feel confident / not stressed or embarrassed** — a primary job cross-market (6/11 Very Important). Ovie's framing is the sharpest: she doesn't want to appear uninformed because social pressure already implies teens should know about money. Ariana wants certainty she won't be "held accountable for doing something wrong later." In CZ, four of six rated it Very Important, connected to approaching financial independence within 1–3 years.

**Save toward a meaningful goal** — low signal in OT and notably absent from the UK card set. The CZ card ("save toward a significant goal without giving up") returned 2/6 Very Important and 3/6 Kinda Important — not negligible, but not a top-ranked job. UK verbal evidence confirms this is not a priority for the segment: none of the five UK OT participants named a named saving goal as a priority. The pattern is consistent — at this life stage, having real income for the first time makes managing and stretching that income the primary financial job; saving toward named targets has been displaced. This is a sharp contrast with YT, where saving toward a goal was the second-strongest job in the segment. The reactivation point for this job is likely when OT participants approach independence and start thinking beyond month-to-month.

**Understanding unexplained topics** — Ariana is the UK signal here: student loans are an immediate, real gap because she's actively applying to university via UCAS. For CZ, the gap is broader — RPSN (APR), compound interest, how credit registries work — surfacing during the app walkthrough even for participants who rated themselves financially confident.

## Young Adults (18–25)

12 respondents: 6 UK (Althea, Enoch, Grace, Mia, Mohammad, Zain), 6 CZ (Anna, Bara, Daniel, Jan, Lukáš, Natalie). Mia, Mohammad, Zain (UK) and all CZ have formal sorts. Althea, Enoch, Grace are stated priorities.

| Job | Most Important | Medium | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manage my money wisely / make it last | 10/12 | 2/12 | Strongest signal in segment. Emotional dimension as prominent as practical — Mia's end-of-month cycle, Mohammad's dinner bill anxiety, Zain's inflation worry. Universal UK Very Important; CZ 4/6 |
| Save toward a meaningful goal | 5/10 | 4/10 | Long-term frame — future security, investing, savings optimisation. Zain (UK) + Bara, Jan, Lukáš (CZ) Very Important; Althea stated; Daniel + Natalie Don't Care |
| Feel confident and not stressed or embarrassed about money | 6/10 | 3/10 | Readiness and preparedness framing — not out of depth when situations arise. CZ particularly strong (4/6 Very Important). Grace ran out of money in Year 1; Daniel builds a 6–7 month buffer |
| Fill knowledge gaps / understand money in ways relevant to me | — | — | Not a distinct card in YA set; embedded in "feel ready / not out of depth" and in how participants engaged with app content |
| Protect myself from financial traps and manipulation | 6/10 | 3/10 | Shifts from scam focus (OT) to system traps — BNPL, hidden costs, product defaults. Mohammad + Enoch (UK) + Bara, Daniel, Jan (CZ) Very Important; Mia + Natalie Don't Care |
| Stop avoiding money and start handling it *(Segment Specific JTBD)* | 7/12 | 1/12 | UK-weighted (5/6 UK Very/Ranked Important). CZ largely Don't Care — already managing. Signals motivated non-managers: Mia's month-end cycle, Althea's overspend→panic loop |
| Deal with real adult responsibilities (rent, bills, credit, debt) *(Segment Specific JTBD)* | 5/9 | 4/9 | Life-stage specific — not applicable at YT or most OT. Anticipatory for some (Anna, CZ — still at home); current reality for others (Bara, Daniel, Natalie) |

### What the most important jobs tell us

**Feel in control / not anxious** — the highest-signal job in the YA segment, rated Very Important by all three formal UK sorts and four of six CZ. The emotional dimension is as important as the practical one: Mia has a repeating end-of-month stress cycle; Mohammad wants to be able to go out without worrying about the dinner bill; Zain is anxious about savings losing value to inflation. Althea describes a cycle of overspend → panic → resolve → repeat, driven by "not knowing where to start." This is not a knowledge gap alone — it is an anxiety and avoidance pattern that financial literacy content can partially address.

**Feel ready / not out of depth** — particularly strong in CZ (4/6 Very Important) and confirmed by Grace and Zain in the UK. Daniel builds a 6–7 month expense buffer specifically to never be caught out. Jan is constantly weighing saving vs. investing options. Grace ran out of money in her first year at university and wants to avoid a repeat. The job is situational and preparatory — users want confidence that when real situations arise, they won't be lost.

**Stop avoiding / start handling** — dominant in the UK (5/6 Very or Ranked Important) but splits sharply in CZ, where 4/6 rated it Don't Care. The CZ Don't Cares (Anna, Jan, Lukáš, Natalie) all have functional, active engagement with their finances — this job simply doesn't apply to them. The UK profile includes more avoidance patterns: Mia's month-end cycle, Mohammad's career-hunting avoidance, Zain's deferred savings optimisation. This job signals which YA users the app can reach — those who are motivated to act, not those who are already managing.

**Deal with real things (rent, bills, credit, debt)** — Mohammad's framing is exact: "That's just basic how life goes — you need to get sorted first before you think about anything else." Four CZ respondents rated it Very Important, predominantly those who are already managing these realities (Bara: rent + daily costs; Daniel: rent + business loan; Natalie: mortgage on parental leave). Anna, who still lives at home, rated it Very Important as anticipatory rather than current.

**Avoid traps** — a secondary job but with a clear split. UK: Mohammad has direct experience of a credit card autopay default (charged interest despite having funds); Enoch lost £100 to a Telegram scam. CZ: Bara, Daniel, and Jan value practical protection. Mia and Natalie both Don't Care — Mia has a strong cultural taboo against debt and BNPL in her Belfast community; Natalie has established bills and a mortgage advisor, so this feels irrelevant. This is not a universal YA job — content must be targeted.
