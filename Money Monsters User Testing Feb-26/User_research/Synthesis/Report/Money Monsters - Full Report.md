## The Three Biggest Things to Act On Before Launch

### 1. Commit to Young Teens (13–15) as the v1 launch segment

The app spans a 13–25 age range containing three audiences with fundamentally incompatible expectations. The design choices that work for Young Teens are the same ones that undermine credibility for Young Adults. Ratings reflect this: Young Teens 8–9/10; Older Teens 7–8.5/10; Young Adults 4–7/10.

**This doesn't require three separate products — it requires a deliberate choice about primary audience.** Young Teens represent the clearest fit: aesthetic lands, gamification resonates, proposition connects. All content, scenarios, characters, and tone decisions should centre on their financial reality — pocket money amounts, social spending moments, near-term saving goals.

### 2. Deepen The Learning Loop — collection without consequence exhausts itself

The single most consistent finding across segments: the current gamification layer is too thin. Participants import the full Duolingo mental model and find the implementation shallow against that benchmark. Monsters are earned but, once collected, they do nothing.

**For Young Teens this is the primary risk.** Even well-designed Q&A formats will disappoint if the game loop fails to sustain engagement. Quick wins for v1:
- XPs alongside streaks — a cumulative progress layer that survives a missed day
- Monsters that unlock something (bonus content, a cosmetic, a feature) rather than simply appearing in a grid
- Preview the next monster at lesson completion to sustain forward pull

Social and competitive features (friend streaks, leaderboards) should be high-priority post-launch additions — peer comparison is a primary motivator for this segment.

### 3. Get the scenario-based learning mechanics right — this is the product's strongest asset

The "help this person make a decision" format works across all segments. It reduces perceived difficulty and invites reasoning rather than recall. This is how the app creates the conditions for the knowing–doing gap to close — learning presented in the context of a decision.

**Critical requirements:**
- Characters must match the target segment: age-appropriate income, situations, and decision contexts
- The key detail needed to answer a question must be on the same screen as the question — not recalled from before
- Every answer needs a brief explanation, right or wrong — being told why you're right is as valuable as being told why you're wrong
- Vary input modalities where feasible; multiple choice alone asks users to pattern-match rather than reason

## What's Working

**The Duolingo mental model transfers.** Almost all participants reached for Duolingo as their reference point, unprompted and positively. The level-map layout, locked/unlocked progression, and streak concept carry associations of accessible, habitual, low-stakes learning — a strong foundation for financial education.

**Scenario-based lessons are the right creative vehicle.** The dialogue format feels like a conversation, not a download. The "help this person" framing is lower-stakes than direct testing — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment. Participants across all segments engaged more deeply with scenario questions than conceptual ones.

**The proposition connects — particularly "Money Stuff Nobody Explains."** This framing positions financial knowledge as insider access — things you should have been told, not things you missed. It situates the knowledge gap in the system, not the user. For Young Teens it activates curiosity; for gap-aware Young Adults it earns credibility.

**Content topics are real and relevant.** For Young Teens: social spending, impulse buying, goal-directed saving. For Older Teens: scam avoidance, first earnings, the 50/30/20 rule. For Young Adults: compound interest, credit card traps, savings products. The research surfaced specific topic priorities by segment that map directly to their actual Jobs to Be Done.

**The underlying app design works.** The progression map with locked and unlocked states, the bite-sized lesson chunks, the try-again mechanic, the post-answer explanations — these work across all segments. The problem is not how the app functions; it's the visual style and the gamification layer that hasn't yet been built on top.

## The Core Insight: The Knowing–Doing Gap

One pattern emerged more consistently than any other: **young people broadly know what they should do with money, but consistently fail to do it.** This isn't a knowledge problem — it's a behaviour problem. Understanding compound interest and applying it in the moment are two different things.

**The implication for Money Monsters:** Financial education alone will not close this gap. What the app can realistically contribute is **preparation and repetition** — scenario-based content that builds decision-making muscle, and habitual engagement driven by streaks, rewards, and progression that turns occasional learning into something that could shape behaviour over time.

## Participant Context and Money Habits

### Money in their life — income, spending, saving

- **Young Teens:** Income from pocket money (£5–15/week or fortnight), birthday cash, and occasional chores. Most spend quickly after receiving. Impulse control is the main challenge — not budgeting. Some have saved toward specific goals (e.g., a PC over two years; football boots), showing motivation-based saving is achievable when tied to something concrete.
- **Older Teens:** Starting to earn real money — e.g., hourly wages from events work, selling vintage clothes online, paper rounds, or actively seeking a first part-time role. Goal-directed saving is happening: saving for a car, a school bag, specific experiences. But impulse tension remains the central challenge — several described knowing they should save while admitting they struggle to follow through.
- **Young Adults:** Real complexity. Some live independently on near-minimum wage managing rent; others balance student loans, part-time work, and parental support. One participant owns a home and has a mortgage; another lives paycheck to paycheck. Stakes are real and consequential.
### How they manage their money

- **Young Teens:** Child-specific accounts (e.g. Monzo, NatWest Rooster) or cash (especially for 12–13 year olds). "Managing" means checking a balance and self-regulating spending.
- **Older Teens:** Accounts range from basic current accounts (Nationwide, NatWest Adapt) to digital-first tools (Revolut, Monzo, Santander). The more financially active participants had developed informal account architecture as a substitute for discipline: Ariana (17) deliberately split money across three accounts — Santander for day-to-day spending, Monzo for longer-term savings, and PayPal for online purchases. Ovie (15) used Revolut's automatic weekly saving sweep to move money without having to consciously decide. Both reflect an instinct to engineer separation — removing the temptation by making savings slightly harder to reach. Others hadn't reached this point: Levi (17) relied on a single account and mental monitoring of his balance; Aiden (15) had a savings account set up by his parents but couldn't access it — savings infrastructure imposed from outside rather than self-managed. The variation within this group is striking: some are already designing their own financial systems; others haven't started.
- **Young Adults:** Widest range — and most dissatisfied with their current systems. Some track mentally with no system; others use spreadsheets for major decisions or split money across multiple accounts to enforce separation (e.g., bills vs. discretionary). Some use passive savings apps (e.g., Plum) but find them too easy to override. Consistent theme: existing tools are too manual, too passive, or too easy to circumvent.

## What They Know About Money

### Confidence and knowledge self-rating

- **Young Teens:** Understand basics (saving, spending) but have no real knowledge of financial products, interest, or what financial life looks like later. Financial confidence at this age is often relative and socially constructed. Typical self-rating: 5–7/10.
- **Older Teens:** Know the saving principles but admit execution is the weak point. The real gap is confidence in adult financial conversations, not day-to-day mechanics. Typical self-rating: 6–7/10.
- **Young Adults:** The most variable group — and the most revealing. Some rate themselves as highly competent while making costly product mistakes that show confidence outrunning understanding (e.g., paying avoidable credit card interest). Others describe their relationship with money as "helpless". Self-ratings range 4–8/10, with emotional state often a better predictor of behavior than score.
### Where they learned about money

- **Family first** — almost universal. Parents are the dominant channel across all segments: saving habits, investing awareness, understanding what's possible. For YA's this extends to wider family and friends.
- **School is consistently described as inadequate** — too academic, too basic, or simply absent. e.g., described as "more like economics than actual money"; school lessons on bank accounts that simply repeated what parents had already taught.
- **Self-directed digital learning** grows with age — e.g., learning from TikTok and AI; researching intensively when a situation demands it; following Martin Lewis and Money Saving Expert. Quality and relevance vary — YouTube content often assumes income levels young people don't have.
### What they wish they understood better 

*Asked before research stimuli was shown*

- **Young Teens:** Adult concepts they overhear — e.g., mortgages (prompted by a parent's house move), currency exchange (ahead of foreign travel). Not urgent financial need — more about not feeling left out of conversations.
- **Older Teens:** Practical knowledge for decisions approaching in 1–3 years — e.g., housing and mortgages, whether to invest now, how cashback works, taxes.
- **Young Adults:** Mechanics behind products they already hold or decisions they're actively making — e.g., how credit card minimum payments and autopay defaults work, ISA and compound interest gaps, how LISAs work for first-time buyers. These aren't abstract — they're live and consequential.

## Learning Styles of Young People

### Introduction to learning styles

How these young people learn matters as much as what they need to know. Three well-established theories from learning science help explain what we observed — and why a single approach won't work across all three segments.

- **Motivation shifts with age.** Younger teens are driven by rewards and social comparison; older teens by personal relevance; young adults by immediate, real-world need. Gamification that works for one group will feel patronizing to another.¹
- **Working memory has limits.** When information arrives too densely or too abstractly, learners disengage before anything sticks — and this effect is strongest where knowledge is thinnest.² Format matters as much as content.
- **Real situations make learning stick.** Learning attached to authentic decisions — real money, real consequences — is retained far better than abstract instruction, at every age.³

Together, these explain why what motivates engagement, how much depth is tolerable, and what format works all shift predictably across our three segments.

*¹ Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan, 1985 · ² Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller, 1988 · ³ Situated Learning Theory — Lave & Wenger, 1991*

## Young Teens (13–15) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Teens are at an early stage of financial awareness. Most have limited, irregular income (pocket money, birthday cash, chores) and minimal exposure to formal financial products. Their financial world is small but emotionally significant — and what they want from financial education reflects that reality.

### Get a head start on peers

- About status and self-concept as much as financial skill. 
- Young Teens are acutely aware of who's good with money and who isn't — and want to be on the right side of that comparison. 
- The competitive and social mechanics of the app — streaks, comparing progress, collecting more than peers — are central to the value proposition for this segment, not peripheral features.

**Examples:**

- Hugo (13) rated himself 9/10 vs. friends who "know nothing."
- Zain (13) wants to start learning now to position himself ahead for a future business.
- Sam (14) observed that friends who save well have "piles and piles of money" available instantly and aspires to the same.

### Make money last longer

- The desire is concrete and situational: not running out before the next birthday, surviving a social outing, being able to afford something without asking parents.
- Content should be grounded in realistic everyday scenarios — small amounts, short time horizons, temptation in social contexts — with tools like needs vs. wants, spending triggers, and simple mental budgeting rules.

**Examples:**

- Lottie (13) describes herself as "very impulse driven."
- Zain (13) spent £11 at a shop "without thinking" while out with friends.
- Isaac (12) admits to buying sweets he doesn't really need.

### Save towards something specific and near-term

- Abstract lessons about long-term financial futures land poorly here. What works is connecting concepts to something the participant is already motivated by.
- Saving mechanics, simple compound growth over months, and goal-tracking features all serve this job directly.

**Examples:**

- Consistently the top-ranked priority across all Young Teen participants.
- Every participant had a recent example of goal-directed saving — a gaming console, a PC, trainers — all concrete, personal, and near-term, not abstract future plans.

### Understand money stuff that no one explains

- Partly curiosity, partly belonging — not feeling silly when money comes up.
- Doesn't require deep content, but does require accessible language, relatable scenarios, and the consistent feeling that the app is speaking to them specifically.
- The guide character, framing, and tone of voice all serve this job.

**Examples:**

- Isaac (12) mentioned mortgages specifically, prompted by his parents' house move.
- Norah (13) overhears adult financial conversations and wants to feel less lost.
- Hugo (13) hears his dad discuss investing and wants to understand what it means.

## Older Teens (15–18) — Jobs to Be Done

Older Teens occupy a transitional zone. They are starting to earn real income (part-time jobs, zero-hours contracts), approaching or already making significant financial decisions (apprenticeships vs. university, first credit products), and are more aware of the financial world — and its risks — than Young Teens. Their jobs to be done reflect this higher-stakes environment.

### Make smarter decisions with existing money

- A meaningful shift from the Young Teen position: these participants have real income and the job is no longer "make my pocket money last" — it's "make sure I'm using my earnings well."
- Content must go beyond basic budgeting and engage with real decision-making scenarios at Older Teen income levels.

**Examples:**

- Levi (17) earns £12/hour on a zero-hours contract.
- Ovie (15) sells vintage clothes on Vinted.
- Aiden (15) recently saved for significant purchases.

### Avoid scams and financial traps

- The standout priority for Older Teens — and one that requires care in delivery.
- These participants are starting to encounter real financial products and real risks for the first time, often without adult supervision.
- Content should focus on recognising manipulation patterns and developing healthy scepticism, not a checklist of things to watch out for.

**Examples:**

- Ovie (15) ranked it in her top three.
- Enoch (18) had a lived experience: he lost £100 to a sophisticated Telegram-based job scam requiring staged deposits.

### Don't fall behind

- Beneath the surface, this job is about the coming transition: all three are within 1–3 years of significant financial independence.
- The horizon is visible and slightly daunting.
- Content should focus on building readiness — what's coming, what you'll need to understand, and how to lay the foundations now.

**Examples:**

- Levi (17) described his relationship with money as "on edge," particularly in conversations with financially knowledgeable people.
- Aiden (15) framed it competitively: getting ahead rather than falling behind.
- Ovie (15) ranked "feel confident about money, not stressed or clueless" as a top priority.

## Young Adults (18–25) — Jobs to Be Done

Young Adults are the segment for whom financial education is most immediately urgent — and yet also the segment the current app format serves least well. Their financial situations are real and live: rent, student loans, credit cards, mortgages, irregular income. Their jobs to be done reflect the pressure and complexity of that reality.

### Not feel out of depth when real situations arise

- The dominant job for Young Adults. Content should be structured around situations, not principles.
- *What happens when you get your first credit card? What does running out of money look like, and what can you do?*
- Information that simulates a real situation lands as essential; information delivered before the situation arises feels irrelevant.

**Examples:**

- Grace (19) ran out of money in her first year at university — a formative experience now driving a desire to "better predict money situations."
- Mohammad (23) made a costly credit card mistake because he didn't know autopay defaulted to the minimum balance.
- Zain (20) is preparing for impending rent and mortgage responsibilities.
- Althea (19) is caught in an avoidance cycle triggered by exactly these moments of feeling out of her depth.

### Understand the mechanics behind financial products (credit, interest, debt)

- Young Adults don't need things simplified — they need them explained with enough depth to be usable.
- They're actively encountering specific financial products and need to know how those products actually behave.
- APR, compound interest, credit score mechanics — these aren't abstract for this group. They're live and consequential.

**Examples:**


- Grace didn't understand compound interest before the app demo.
- Zain (20) is actively researching ISAs and LISAs but finds existing content too generic to be actionable.

### Avoid getting caught out by traps and hidden costs

- These aren't scams — they're features of real mainstream products most young adults encounter: the minimum payment default, the 0% rate that reverts without warning, the BNPL product that accrues interest invisibly.
- Content should combine product mechanics with decision frameworks, building a habit of scrutiny rather than teaching rules that may become outdated.

**Examples:**

- Mohammad (23, UK) didn't know credit card autopay defaults to the minimum balance and paid avoidable interest.

## What This Means for Positioning, Content, and Lesson Design

**1. Ground everything in real situations, not abstract principles.** Every segment's jobs are rooted in concrete lived experience — purchase goals, past mistakes, transitions, social fears. Scenario-based content outperforms teaching concepts in isolation. The tested lesson format rated well; ensure future lesson designs are specific and realistic for each segment.

**2. The app must bridge knowing and doing — not just explain.** Explanation alone won't close the knowing–doing gap. Build features that help users act on what they learn: realistic goal-setting, scenarios that mirror real decisions, prompts that encourage follow-through. It's as much product design as content.

**3. The same topic cannot serve all three segments the same way.** Credit cards could appear across all segments — but the job differs. Young Teens need to understand what adults discuss; Older Teens prepare for their first account; Young Adults avoid costly mistakes with products they hold. Lesson design must be segment-aware throughout.

## Framing Topics in a Relevant Way

Topic selection determines relevance. Framing determines whether users feel it. Three principles emerged consistently across segments:

**1. Anchor every lesson to a situation the user is already in — or will be in very soon.** Abstract concepts fail; concrete scenarios land. Young Teens respond to small amounts and near-term goals. Older Teens want preparation framing ("should you be investing at your age?"), not theory. Young Adults need content that reflects real conditions — irregular income, multiple income streams — not ideal-world assumptions.

**2. Match the emotional weight of the framing to the stakes.** Playful works for Young Teens, where financial stakes are genuinely small. Older Teens are at the edge of that tolerance — they want to be taken seriously. Young Adults are explicit: the right tone is a capable, direct peer, not a cheerful mascot.

**3. Use the language they already use — not the language of financial education.** Young Teens understand "making money last" but not "budgeting." Older Teens use Revolut, savings pots, and cashback naturally — explaining these is an engagement killer. Young Adults want precision: *"you know interest exists — here's how to actually use it"* respects existing knowledge while filling the gap.

## Young Teens (13–15) — The Archetype

### Portrait

**Jordan, 13.** Jordan plays Roblox after school. He's got a 40-day Duolingo streak he's not going to break. He earns about £8 a fortnight doing chores, and it's usually gone by the weekend — on snacks, a game top-up, something with his friends. He's not bad with money; he just doesn't think about it. Why would he? Nothing bad has happened yet.

**Knows, doesn't act:** He knows, vaguely, that he should probably save some of it. His mum tells him. He agrees. Then he spends it anyway. This isn't a character flaw — it's that nobody has ever given him a system, a reason, or a moment where saving felt more interesting than spending.

**Game first:** Jordan does not want to learn about money. He wants to beat his mate's streak. He wants to collect the next monster. He wants to reach the next level before he has to put the phone down for dinner. If he absorbs what interest means along the way — fine. But it cannot be the reason he opens the app. The game has to be the reason.

**Money Monsters works for Jordan if it feels like something he'd pick up to unwind — not something he'd open to improve himself.**

---

### Learning Style

**Seconds, not minutes:** Jordan's attention is earned fast and lost faster. Shaped by TikTok, Roblox, and Duolingo, he's used to content that moves quickly, rewards constantly, and never asks him to sit with difficulty for long. The moment Money Monsters feels like a lesson, he checks out. The moment it feels like a game he's winning, he wants one more round.

**Decisions, not concepts:** Scenarios pull him in; explanations push him away. Every lesson should be a decision he's helping someone like him make. The Tom scenario — a character his own age, a real budget, a choice with stakes — was the most recalled content across the research. Jordan didn't notice he was learning. That's the design target.

**Streak as product:** Progress needs to be visible and immediate. A completed level, a new monster, a streak held — these are not extras, they are the product. With them, he'll come back to protect what he's built and rope in a mate to compete against. Once one friend has a streak, the rest want one too.

**Tone tightrope:** The guide character matters. Jordan is at the edge of thinking Duolingo's owl is a bit babyish — credible enough not to feel talked down to, playful enough not to feel like school.

---

### Emotions Around Money

**Not anxious — indifferent:** Jordan is not worried about money. That's a design constraint, not a problem. An app that leads with "you'll need to understand this when you're older" will lose him instantly. He's 13. Later is not now.

**Status, not stress:** What Jordan does respond to is the low hum of not wanting to look stupid. When adults talk about mortgages, he goes quiet. When his friends compare savings, he notices. He doesn't want to be the one who doesn't get it — he just hasn't found a way in that feels worth the effort.

**The penny dropping:** The emotion Money Monsters should deliver is the quiet click of something previously adult and opaque suddenly making sense. The post-answer explanation — *why* he was right or wrong — is the moment the app earns his respect. Not because he cares about the content, but because it makes him feel like he's on the inside of something. That feeling is the hook.

---

### Sources of Financial Knowledge

**Thin and adult-framed:** Jordan knows what his parents have told him (save some; don't get scammed) and what school has vaguely gestured at (taxes exist; mortgages are a thing). Neither source speaks to this week. Neither tells him what to do with the £8 in his Monzo right now.

**Jordan's world only:** **This is the gap Money Monsters fills: not adult financial education, but Jordan's financial education.** Content, characters, and scenarios need to live inside his world — pocket money amounts, cinema decisions, saving for something he actually wants. The moment the lesson assumes an adult income, he mentally files it under "not for me."

**Curiosity is fragile:** Something triggers it — a price that's gone up, a conversation he half-overheard — and then it runs out of steam because nothing meets it at the right level. Money Monsters needs to be the app that was already waiting when that curiosity arrived.

> *"I like how it has storylines to everything — rather than just questions."* — Lottie (13, UK)

## Young Adults (18–25) — The Archetype

### Portrait

**Aisha, 21.** Aisha left school at 18 and went straight into work — she's been in the same admin role for two years, steady income, living at home, saving slowly toward moving out. She uses Duolingo most nights before sleep — not because she's serious about learning Spanish, but because it's a habitual thing she does, like scrolling. She has a 67-day streak and she's not breaking it. She plays mobile games on the bus. She likes the feeling of progress, the small win before she puts her phone down.

**Switched off:** She finds finance a switch-off. Not because she's irresponsible — she keeps a rough mental track of what's in her account — but because every time she's tried to engage with it properly, the content has assumed something she didn't know, or just been relentlessly dull. She's watched a Martin Lewis clip. Started a YouTube video about investing. Used ChatGPT to ask about ISAs. None of it stuck.

**One moment:** She's had one moment that spooked her — a credit card charge she didn't see coming, a month that got tight faster than expected. She didn't talk about it much. But it's still there, quiet, in the back: *I should probably understand this stuff better.* She just hasn't found a way in that doesn't feel like work.

> **Money Monsters works for Aisha if it meets her in decompression mode — when she's on the sofa, not at her desk. If it asks her to concentrate, she'll close it. If it feels like a game she's already winning, she'll keep going.**

---

### Learning Style

**Failed by content:** Aisha has been let down repeatedly by financial content that doesn't know who it's talking to — YouTube videos that assume a salary she doesn't have, explainers that need Googling, tone that sounds like a stern uncle or a patronising children's presenter. She's not looking for a course. She's looking for content that makes her feel like she's quietly getting smarter without the effort of trying to get smarter.

**Game, not lesson:** The format that works is exactly what Money Monsters already has: a bite-sized scenario, a real problem, a decision with stakes she recognises. Under three minutes. Completable on the bus. Peer-to-peer tone — someone who's figured this out and is sharing it, not an expert here to educate.

**Streak logic:** She will sustain a daily habit only if there is a tangible reward worth protecting. Her Duolingo streak isn't really about Spanish — it's about the streak. Money Monsters needs the same logic: a visible, accumulating thing that feels like a loss to abandon.

---

### Emotions Around Money

**Low-grade avoidance:** Aisha's baseline is arm's-length. She's learned that engaging with the detail usually leads to confusion or mild anxiety, so she keeps it at a distance. She knows roughly what's in her account. That's enough, most of the time.

**The near-miss:** What breaks through the avoidance is the moment the systems she's inside turned out to have rules nobody told her. That stings differently from just not knowing something — it's the feeling of having played a game without being told all the rules. Money Monsters should speak to that feeling: not by warning her, but by making her feel like she's the kind of person who now knows what's going on.

**Readiness, not wealth:** Financial competence for Aisha is not about money — it's about not being caught out. Not going blank when someone mentions an ISA. Not having to borrow from a parent. The right payoff after a session is not *I learned something today*. It is *I'm the kind of person who does this.*

> *"I want to feel ready for money situations and not out of my depth."* — Grace, 19

---

### Sources of Financial Knowledge

**Mainstream failed her:** Google gives too much or too little. YouTube assumes an income bracket she doesn't have. Martin Lewis is for people who already know enough to know what to ask. She defaults to ChatGPT because at least she can make the question specific — the closest thing to a friend who's already figured it out.

**Friction kills habit:** The products she's tried and abandoned all had one thing in common: they required effort to maintain. Friction is fatal. Money Monsters' edge is that the game mechanic *is* the habit — the streak sustains itself, the progress accumulates without administration, the next session costs nothing to start.

> *"I don't know where to go to, to really learn about money."* — Zain (20, UK)

Aisha isn't disengaged from money. She's disengaged from the way money has been taught to her. That's a product problem, not a user problem — and it's exactly what Money Monsters is positioned to solve.

# 7. The Key Design and Product Tensions

These are not feature-level problems — they are structural tensions in the product's identity. Resolving one without attending to the others risks creating new contradictions.

---

## One App, Three Audiences

The app spans a 13–25 age range containing three audiences with fundamentally incompatible expectations:

- **Young Teens (13–15):** Want a game that teaches. Low financial anxiety, high tolerance for playful aesthetics. Monster theme lands; gameplay depth doesn't — yet.
- **Older Teens (16–18):** Acutely sensitive to anything that positions them as younger. Accept the gamification architecture but reject the visual register.
- **Young Adults (18–25):** Want a credible reference. Playful design lowers barriers but sacrifices the credibility that makes engagement worthwhile.

Ratings reflect this: Young Teens 8–9/10; Older Teens 7–8.5/10; Young Adults 4–7/10. The design choices that work for Young Teens are the same ones that undermine credibility for Young Adults.

> *"I really like it because it makes it seem like you're working towards something."* (Sam, 14, UK)
>
> {OT / YA quote rejecting design}

This doesn't require three separate products — it requires a deliberate choice about primary audience and accepting the consequences. Young Teens represent the clearest product-market fit, but that has implications for positioning, distribution, and the school-partnerships model.

---

## Playful vs. Credible

Playfulness and credibility pull in opposite directions. Young adults especially believe that the topic of money has specific credibility requirements that other learning domains don't — getting savings or debt decisions wrong has real consequences.

- The monster aesthetic signals approachability but undermines trust for older users
- "Money Monsters" commits to a playful register at the identity level — visual and content design carries the full burden of communicating seriousness
- For Young Teens this trade-off is acceptable; for Young Adults it's decisive
- A resolution exists: mature the visual layer while preserving the gamification architecture — decouple the playful aesthetic from the structural mechanics

> *"There should be some seriousness — it's an important topic."* (Althea, 19, UK)
>
> *"If something's important, you need to do it — you should be able to do it without giving you a sense of dopamine rush."* (Mohammad, 23, UK)

---

## Daily Habit vs. Situational Reference

The Duolingo comparison participants reach for is instructive — but misleading:

- Language learning is **evergreen**: daily practice always adds value, content is inexhaustible, and the skill degrades without use — streaks fit this naturally
- Financial literacy is **front-loaded**: once you understand compound interest or how an ISA works, you don't need to revisit it daily — the application is situational, triggered by life events not calendar habits
- Younger participants' confidence about daily use should be read cautiously — habitual engagement is easy to imagine in a session; harder to sustain when the content runs out

Money Monsters is built for daily use — streaks, progression maps, and locked content all assume habitual engagement. This works for Young Teens, but a significant portion of the intended audience uses apps differently.

- **Habit-based users (Young Teens):** Forward-looking orientation — what comes next, what do I unlock? Streaks and progression create momentum.
- **Pull-based users (Young Adults):** Situational and event-triggered — does this apply to me right now? The streak mechanic penalises absence; locked progression excludes users arriving with a specific question.
- The two models aren't mutually exclusive, but require different design features to coexist
- A situational entry point — topic index, searchable glossary, or "what do you need today?" routing — would serve pull-based users without dismantling the streak architecture

> *"It makes it seem like you're working towards something."* (Sam, 14, UK — on daily streaks)
>
> *"I would use it when facing specific financial decisions — not every day."* (Grace, 19, UK)

Without a situational entry point, a significant slice of the intended audience is structurally excluded from meaningful engagement.

---

## Guided Learning Path vs. User-Driven Topic Selection

The app's sequential learning architecture assumes users want to be taken on a guided journey — but many users, particularly older ones, want to navigate directly to what matters to them right now.

Our working assumption on planned experience:
- Lessons unlock in sequence, creating a linear progression
- Content is gated — you must complete earlier stages before accessing later material
- The learning path is determined by the app, not the user's immediate needs

This works well for **younger teens**, who appreciate structure and enjoy the sense of progression toward unlocking new content, but a significant portion of users — especially **older teens and young adults** — resist this model:

- They arrive with **specific questions** triggered by real situations: "I'm buying a car, how do I compare finance options?" or "What's the difference between an ISA and a savings account?"
- They want **topic-based navigation**: the ability to browse by theme (savings, borrowing, investing) and drill into granular subtopics
- Linear progression feels **restrictive** when they already understand some concepts but not others — forcing them through basics to reach advanced content creates friction
- The locked structure **excludes situational users** who need an answer today, not after completing five prerequisite lessons

> *"I dislike being restricted or needing to complete things to move forward — I want to jump to what's relevant to me."* (Zain, 20, UK)
>
> *"Topic categories with clickable lessons — so I can address specific situations I'm facing."* (Grace, 19, UK)

Ultimately, to appeal to a broad audience base Money Monsters must offer  **two pathways**:
- **Guided path**: For users who want to build knowledge systematically and enjoy unlocking progression (younger teens, habit-based learners)
- **Topic index/search**: For users who arrive with a specific question and need direct access to relevant content (older users, situational learners, those with uneven knowledge)

> *"It's quite good. It's like not super eye-catching, though, because the colours are quite plain almost. It makes you want to like keep going."* (Olivia, 14, UK)

### OTs: Where it starts to fail

- Older Teens do not reject gamification — they respond well to streaks, progression, and collection rewards
- The friction is the specific visual execution: monster characters, primary-palette colours, and cartoon guides read as targeted at a younger audience
- For 15–17 year olds beginning to form a financial identity, the aesthetic undermines the credibility of the content before it has been seen

> {participant quote}

### YAs: Limited to Niche appeal

- For the majority of Young Adults, the playful design signaled young/easy content
- A number of participants highlighted this playfulness was at odds with their expectations of money being a serious/sober topic
- However, the segment is not monolithic: Enoch (18, UK) was broadly positive, and Mia (25, UK) loved the collection system — spontaneously citing Plum (4M+ users) as evidence that warm, character-driven design can work for older users when the underlying utility is clear.

> *"Money is an important topic. It should be more serious."* Althea (19, UK)

- The monsters are central to brand identity — the app is literally called Money Monsters — but their visual execution creates the primary aesthetic age-gap
- The current design (cartoon style, bright colours, toy-like characters) works for Young Teens but reads as "childish" to progressively older users
- Two distinct visual issues emerged: (1) the aesthetic style feels too young, and (2) the learner's relationship to the monsters is visually ambiguous — unclear whether you play *as* or *collect* them

> {quote}
## Structural Visual Conventions: What Works

- The way the app shows progress, status, and what's locked/unlocked worked well across all segments
- The level-map layout, the locked/unlocked visual distinction (green vs gray), and the implied promise of progressive difficulty were universally understood and positively received
- The implication: the app doesn't need to change how it works to serve older users — it just needs visuals that look less childish
- A shared architecture with age-differentiated visual skins is the right direction; not a fundamental redesign of the learning model

>{quote}# 9. Onboarding

## What the Onboarding Experience Involved

The research stimulus included a short quiz-based onboarding sequence designed to assess participants' financial knowledge and attitudes before directing them into personalised lesson content.

The quiz comprised five behavioural and scenario-based questions about money situations and spending habits, guided by Mr. Money — a human character who framed it as a way to understand the participant's starting point, not test their competence.

---

## Design Principles for Onboarding

**Behaviour-based questions** — Questions should ask how participants actually behave with money, not what they know. Across all segments, participants preferred situational questions over knowledge tests — when asked if they'd prefer concept-anchored alternatives, nearly all chose the situational format. As Althea (19) put it: *"knowing a definition doesn't mean you have relevant experience or real-world need for it."*

**Frame as personalisation, not assessment** — The framing that the app is learning about the user, not testing them, must be explicit from the first screen. A brief statement — "We'll use your answers to choose content that's relevant to where you are right now" — increases honest engagement, particularly among older participants sceptical of generic educational tools. All segments understood that the quiz was assessing their situation, not their intelligence.

**Age-calibrated scenarios and amounts** — Questions referencing unfamiliar products (ISAs, cryptocurrency) or unrealistic amounts alienated participants. A £500 windfall lands differently for a 13-year-old than a 23-year-old. Asking for user's age and then drawing from segment-calibrated question pools mitigates user dropouts.

**Quiz length matched to segment** — Young Teens found 5–7 questions appropriate. Older Teens and Young Adults wanted more depth (7–10 questions) to surface genuinely relevant content; several felt fewer questions would leave the app unable to distinguish their situation. Over-shortening for older segments risks the quiz feeling superficial and the personalisation feeling hollow.

**Friendly guiding character, not authority** — Mr. Money was well-received by Young Teens, who valued his credibility as a human guide: *"it makes it feel like that person who is helping has done this as well."* Older Teens and Young Adults found the character created an unwanted teacher-student dynamic. Positioning the guide explicitly as someone who has been through this — rather than as an instructor — broadens appeal; character customisation could extend it further for older segments.
# 10. Lesson Experience: Learning Mechanics and Comprehension

## The Creative Vehicle: Dialogue vs. Lecture

The research stimulus used a dialogue-based lesson format: financial scenarios played out through character conversations, with the learner prompted to help a character make a decision, choose between options, or identify the right approach. 

### Why the dialogue/scenario format is resonating

The scenario format works because users learn by making choices, not by reading explanations first. This creates two advantages:

1. **Reducing perceived difficulty.** The scenario format makes financial content feel navigable — participants reason from experience rather than memorised definitions. Levi (17, UK) "always found a relatable answer among the options."

> *"It's making you think about real life situations and how you would react... It's better than some apps who don't use real life."* (Olivia, 14, UK)

2. **Creating emotional engagement.** The dialogue format feels like a conversation, not a download (Enoch, 18). The "help this person" framing is lower-stakes than direct testing — it invites reasoning rather than self-judgment.

> *"I like how it has storylines to everything. Rather than just questions."* (Lottie, 13, UK)

> **Key Principle:** the dialogue/scenario format should be the primary creative vehicle for Money Monsters lessons as learning through decisions, not just explanations is **how the app helps close the** **knowing–doing gap**. 

### The role of character-based scenarios

Two design principles emerged from the research.

**Character consistency:** characters must have stable, clearly established identities that persist throughout a session. Aiden (15) described confusion about whether he was playing *as* the monster character or *collecting* it — a character identity problem that broke the scenario logic. 

**Character relevance:** characters' age, income level, and life situation should approximate those of the target user. For older segments, scenarios need to reflect real financial complexity — rent decisions, credit card choices, actual employment income — otherwise the learning feels abstract rather than rehearsal for real life.

> *"I think the choice of Mr. Money is very good. Because it makes it seem real... It makes it feel like that person who is helping him has done this as well."* (Sam, 14, UK)

## Question Design

### How questions are presented: what works

Research stimuli question formats combined **scenario framing, appropriate contextual detail, and an outcome orientation** (what should this person do?) rather than a knowledge orientation (what is the correct definition of X?). 

- **Specificity matters.** The key detail a learner needs to answer a question must be present on the same screen as the question — not the screen before it. Questions that embed the relevant information in the scenario itself consistently performed better than those requiring participants to recall from an earlier screen.
- **Answer option quality matters as much as the question.** Effective multiple choice requires all options to be plausible. When wrong answers are too easily dismissed, the question has no learning value — the participant is pattern-matching rather than reasoning.
- **Input modality should not default to multiple choice.** Even well-designed MCQ presented in an engaging format (e.g. logging into a simulated bank account) benefits from being mixed with other response types.

> *"It was very just clicking a bunch of options. If it had different stuff, like you might have to collect something and then exchange money or something... if it had different tasks."* (Zain, 13, UK) 

> **Key Principle:**  v1, establish which input modalities are technically feasible — and designing the question mix to use them. Varied modalities sustain attention and better reflect how financial decisions are actually made.
### Try-again and feedback mechanics

- **The retry mechanic** — requiring re-engagement rather than simply moving past a wrong answer — was consistently appreciated. Moving on without grasping why produces a false sense of completion.
- **Post-answer explanation** adds further value whether the answer was right or wrong (Mia, 25). The principle: every question should end with understanding, not just a result. Keep explanations brief, plain-language, and conversational.

> *"The app doesn't move on until the question is answered correctly."*(Enoch (18, UK)

> *"I like how it gives you facts once you do a question rather than just saying yes."* (Lottie, 13, UK)

### UX principles for learning: keeping reference information accessible

- **Tap for more detail**: responded well to access to deeper definitions and worked examples at the point of learning. Tapping the *"What is compound interest?"* button in the answer feedback overlay — which broke down the concept further — was a well-received mechanism.
- **Ensure users don't have to press back to recall detail**: Where content spans multiple screens, the critical decision-relevant detail must be on the screen where the decision is made. 

 > *"It was hard to remember the detail in the question because I had to go back to the previous screen."* (Mohammad, 23, UK)

## Lesson Length and Session Duration

### Current length: broadly appropriate for YT/OT; format mismatch for YA

The research stimulus was designed to give respondents a flavor of the learning material, not to precisely test lesson duration — so findings on length should be treated with some caution. 

- Short-form lessons (e.g. 10 questions) worked for Young Teens and Older Teens, where it matched attention span and usage habit. This group also, envisaged longer app usage if there was greater elements of gamification (see gamification section).
- For Young Adults, there was clearer frustration — lessons felt too brief to adequately explore a concept. 
### The mobile context: commuting, idle time, short-burst use

Across all segments, participants described usage as fundamentally mobile and opportunistic. Enoch (18, UK) described the lesson length as "perfect for bus journeys." Sam (14, UK) envisioned checking the app in the morning before school.

## What Features Are in The Learning Loop

Multiple participants across all three segments compared the app to Duolingo without prompting. The gamification architecture has four elements:

- **Monster collection** — completing a lesson unlocks a named character (Sammy Savings, Tracy Tracking, etc.) representing a financial theme
- **Progression map** — locked and unlocked lessons arranged in a visual path
- **Character guidance** — Mr. Money and lesson-specific characters guide users through content, with room to be gamified further
- **Streaks** — consecutive-day tracking that makes continuity its own reward

---

## What's Working

### Monster collection: incentive that lands with Young Teens

- Collection provides a concrete reward structure — an object to acquire, a collection to build — that translates lesson completion into something tangible. 
- Distinctly named characters provides additional appeal — though their linked domain (e.g. 'Tracking') was not always understood (see 4. Topics)
- Appeal has some cross-segment reach — Enoch (18) and Mia (25) were both positive — but Young Teens are where the mechanic functions most reliably as a motivator
- *Note: The visual execution of monsters (aesthetic age-appropriateness) is addressed in [[8. First Impressions & Visual Comprehension|Section 8]]. This section focuses on collection as a behavioral mechanic.*

> {quote}
### Progress and progression maps: understood and motivating

- The visual path with locked and unlocked states communicates structure, progress, and aspiration simultaneously.
- Enoch (18) understood it as part of a coherent gamified system; Zain (20, UK) appreciated the *"bite-sized, un-lockable chunk layout."*
- Locked content functions as aspiration rather than frustration — moderators observed participants tapping on greyed out levels and who wished to find out more.

> *"It's making you want to keep going because you can like collect the maps... probably something more younger people do than older people."* (Olivia, 14, UK)

---

## What's Missing or Underdeveloped

### Expanding The Learning Loop: a clear expectation from all segments

- The single most consistent finding is that the current gamification layer is insufficient. Participants import the full Duolingo mental model and find the current implementation shallow against that benchmark.
- The gap takes different forms by segment: deeper gameplay for Young Teens; tangible rewards and customization for Older Teens; unlocking more-sophisticated content and leaderboard access for Young Adults.

**For YTs this presents a disproportionate risk; low gameplay depth risks limiting repeat usage**

> *"They could improve the gameplay...spendable points, monster trading, map-based mini-games with financial consequences."* (Isaac, 13, UK)

- Young Teens had the highest expectations of gameplay and interactivitym and the monster-collection layer falls short of their expectations of what it could entail.
- The risk is that even creatively-written question-answer formats will disappoint many YTs. Similarly, text-heavy questions (even broken down across screens) will strain attention spans.
- The risk: Young Teens engage briefly, find the game layer too thin, and don't return — not because they disliked the concept, but because the execution didn't match the proposition.


### Consequence-based mechanics: desire for more purpose in collecting monsters

- Monsters are earned but, once collected, they do nothing.
- Althea (19, UK): *"'Monster Collected' felt like a gimmick because I didn't understand where it was leading"* — she wanted gamification that builds toward something meaningful, not just awards trophies.
- Ariana (17, UK) referenced the WeWard app model (steps converted to points redeemable for discounts) as what tangible payoff could look like; customization unlocks for a personal avatar was another route.
- Whether the second layer means trading, upgrading, or content-gating matters less than the principle: collection without consequence exhausts itself quickly.

> {OT quote}
### Social and competitive features: leaderboards and peer comparison

The social layer is absent and when increased gameplay/fun was explored, participants expected competitive elements with friends or public leaderboards — comparing streaks, earning more monsters.

> Levi (17, UK) highlighted *"If other people use it, I would definitely start using it."* suggesting that social proof could be a habit-forming mechanism.

---

## Where to proceed with caution

### Streaks: recognized, but contingent on sufficient content to sustain habit

 *Referencing the broader tension addressed in [[7. The Key Design and Product Tensions|Section 7]]: Daily Habit vs. Situational Reference*
 
- Duolingo has shaped expectations; recognition was universal.
- Though unlike languages, sustained daily usage or knowledge building may be harder to sustain in practice.
- YA engagement is more likely to be situational — triggered by a financial question or moment, not a daily routine. 
- To this end, the development resource required to implement streak mechanism would be best redeployed to other gameplay features.



# 12. Segment Verdicts

## Young Teens (13–15): Best Bet for Launch

### What's working

- **Aesthetic lands.** Monster characters, bright palette, and Duolingo-style level map are understood and welcomed — playful design removes the barrier to entry for a topic that might otherwise feel adult and off-putting
- **Gamification resonates.** Monster collection is legible and motivating (Sam, 14: "working towards something"); streaks welcomed; progression map creates forward-looking orientation with locked content reading as aspiration not frustration
- **Proposition fits.** "Money Stuff Nobody Explains" and "Real Money Skills" both activate interest; the app communicates *this is for someone like me, right now*
- **Scenario-based lessons work.** "Help this person make a decision" format is engaging and manageable; try-again mechanic appreciated; short-form length matches attention span

> *"It's good at explaining it in different words for people that don't understand it."* (Olivia, 14, UK)

- **Content matches their financial lives.** Social spending, impulse buying, goal-directed saving — the scenarios reflect what this segment actually experiences

### What needs fixing

- **Gamification layer is too thin.** Monsters are earned but do nothing once collected — collection without consequence exhausts motivation quickly; needs a second layer (XPs, features unlock, monsters to trade, content gates)
- **No social or competitive features.** Peer comparison is a primary motivator for this segment; without it the app relies entirely on individual mechanics.
- **Lesson interactivity is limited.** Overreliance on multiple choice places too much weight on a gamification layer that isn't yet deep enough to compensate
### Launch readiness rating and conditions

**Rating: Launch in v1 with a clear content journey.**

- Core concept works, aesthetic is right, content serves real needs
- **Condition:** Gamification must be improved otherwise that fades once the game loop fails to sustain engagement
- Social and competitive features should be high-priority post-launch additions, not long-term nice-to-haves

---

## Older Teens (16–18): The Retention Problem

### What's working

- **Scenario-based learning resonates.** Dialogue format suits a segment already making real money decisions; content feels applicable, not hypothetical
- **Content topics are real and urgent.** Earning income wisely, scam avoidance, 50/30/20 rule — live concerns that give them usable heuristics for situations they're already in. 
- **Proposition lands.** framing acknowledges gaps without being condescending
- **In-app experience praised.** Dulcie (16): "I love it... I would actually want to learn so much about banking"; Ariana (17) gave 7/10 — "genuinely helpful"

> *"Money stuff nobody explains... that's what really caught on to me"* (Ariana, 17, UK)
### What needs fixing

- **Aesthetic polarises within the cohort, not uniformly by age.** Hard blocker for some 15-year-olds ("designed for primary school children") but Dulcie (16) welcomed monsters as a "teacher/mentor" — visual appeal isn't the primary retention barrier
- **Gamification lacks payoff.** Monster collection registered as "random orange thing on my face" (Ariana, 17) without consequence. This segment wants tangible rewards — customisation unlocks, real-world perks (Ariana referenced WeWard's redeemable points model), or features that build toward something meaningful
- **No compelling reason to return — even when they love it.** Dulcie rated the app 6-7/10 and said "I love it" but still predicted she'd "probably start it and then just not want to do it because it's based around money"
- **Money lacks the pull of language-learning.** Duolingo benefits from social/cultural motivation; financial literacy is felt as necessity, not skill. The topic itself creates friction that gamification must actively overcome
- **Linear progression frustrates.** Users who know the basics don't want to unlock content sequentially; they want to jump to what's relevant

### Launch readiness rating and conditions

**Rating: Launchable, but retention requires gamification with consequence.**

- Aesthetic age-up is worth doing for style-conscious 15s, but opinion splits within the cohort — some 16–17s respond well to the current design
- **Visuals aren't the core barrier.** Participants who loved the look still doubted they'd return — the topic itself lacks pull
- **Sustained engagement needs tangible payoff**: customisation unlocks, real-world rewards (WeWard model), or collection mechanics that build toward something — plus topic-based entry points and social hooks

---

## Young Adults (18–25): Right Content, Wrong Vehicle

### What's working

- **Scenario content resonates.** Compound interest, credit card behaviour, savings account yield — specific lesson content was valued precisely because these are live concerns, not hypothetical ones
- **A minority found genuine appeal.** Enoch (18) broadly positive; Mia (25) responded warmly to monster collection, citing Plum as a comparable product — rejection is not universal; charm can work when underlying utility is clear
- **Proposition has reach.** "Money Stuff Nobody Explains" connected with gap-aware participants; "Train Your Money Brain" with those focused on behaviour change — both work; generic educational framing did not
- **Content topics match real urgency.** Credit mechanics, debt traps, hidden costs, savings products — Young Adults face these as live situations, not future preparations

### Structural misfit: the delivery model doesn't match the usage pattern

- Money Monsters is built for daily sequential use — streaks, locked progression, and incremental completion all assume a user who shows up every day and works through content in order
- Young Adults use financial content situationally: Grace (19) wanted to "predict money situations before they happen"; Althea (19) would reach for the app "when a specific financial question arose"; Zain (20) wanted to jump directly to relevant content
- **Streaks penalise absence; locked progression blocks situational users** — both mechanics serve habit-forming engagement and fail pull-based engagement
- The product is designed for *what do I unlock next?*; Young Adults arrive asking *does this apply to me right now?* — only one of these is currently served

### What a YA-appropriate product could look like

- **Add a situational entry point** alongside the daily-use features — topic index, searchable glossary, or "what do you need today?" routing so users can jump to what's relevant to them
- **Make the visuals feel more grown-up** without abandoning the brand — playfulness should feel intentional, not childish
- **Deepen content** for situations Young Adults already face: minimum payment traps, 0% rates that revert, BNPL mechanics, credit score implications
- **Lead with credibility signals** — demonstrate serious financial expertise before asking users to trust the format
- **Give collection mechanics purpose** — rewards that unlock content or features, not decorative trophies

### Launch readiness rating and conditions

**Not the primary launch audience — but a viable niche with the right positioning.**

- The recommendation is not to chase broad Young Adult appeal — that would require compromising the product's core identity. A Money Monsters that leans into its personality with intent can find a genuine audience in this cohort. It will be a smaller slice, and that's a position worth owning.

# 13. Commercialization Routes

The research explored two commercialization routes: **premiumization of the app** (users paying directly) and **commercial partnerships** with financial/educational institutions (via a question of 'Where would you expect Money Monsters to live?')

---

## Route 1: App Premiumization

**Is the app premium-ready?** Not yet. Resistance to paying runs deeper than price sensitivity — a money education app charging subscriptions could itself be the kind of financial trap it warns against (genuine brand risk). Credibility barriers also block conversion. But three consistent premium hypotheses emerged worth building toward.

**Prerequisites:** (1) Free tier must establish genuine value first; (2) gamification layer must be more developed — premium mechanics presuppose a game loop that doesn't yet exist; (3) credibility must precede conversion.

---

### What Users Would Pay For

**AI-powered personalised Q&A** (OT, YA)
- Most consistent premium signal: pressing financial questions are situation-specific, not generic
- Practical financial tools: subscription cancellation guidance, real-time product comparison (YA)
- Personal financial advisor or chat support responsive to individual situations (OT)
- Explicitly preferred over generic AI advice

**Freemium energy/lives model** (OT, YA)
- Free tier with daily limits, premium removes them (Duolingo model)
- Only viable once core game loop is compelling enough to create genuine demand

**Ad removal** (OT, YA)
- Lighter premium tier removing friction rather than adding features
- No philosophical conflict with educational mission
- Could serve as gateway to higher-tier engagement

**Social and competitive features** (OT, YA)
- Public leaderboards, comparing streaks with friends, inter-user statistics
- Longer-term opportunity requiring social layer to exist in free tier first

**Tiered content progression** (OT)
- Initial levels free, content beyond level 5 paid
- Only acceptable when framed as progression unlocks, not knowledge purchases

---

### What Doesn't Work

**Content paywalls** (OT, YA)
- Belief that money education should be free; alternative resources available online
- Some felt 'game play' premiumization is a want not a need — subscribing is itself financially unsound
- Values alignment problem, not pricing objection

**Subscription model** (YA)
- Specifically rejected on principle — seen as structurally ironic for an app about avoiding financial traps
- Preference for one-time purchase (£20–25) or freemium over recurring subscription

**Low-value additions**
- Gamification cosmetics (OT)
- Extra levels or games as premium content (OT)
- Topic expansions or educational depth (OT) — would rather research independently
- Generic AI advice (YA)

---

### Segment-Specific Notes

**Young Teens:** Monetisation was not explored with YT participants.

**Financially literate users (OT):** Content flexibility (non-linear navigation, ability to skip known concepts) is a more pressing gap than premium features. Navigation model may need resolving before premium lands for this sub-group.

**Young Adults:** Showed most variation — from hard refusal to genuine purchase intent. Strongest conversion signal is personalised, situational support. Freemium is more commercially straightforward than subscription.

## Route 2: Commercial Partnerships with Financial Institutions

Multiple participants positioned the app within banking contexts when asked where they'd expect to find it — suggesting partnerships with financial institutions as a credible alternative distribution model.

**Evidence:**
- Ovie (16): *"banks as soon as a teen gets their card... should tell people how to use it"* — financial literacy education should come from banking partners at the moment of first card receipt
- Multiple participants use digital banks (Monzo, Starling), showing they're already embedded in banking ecosystems where the app could integrate

**Strategic advantages:**
- Bypasses consumer payment friction — no "paying for education" conflict
- Reaches users at point of need (account opening, first card)
- Banking endorsement resolves trust barriers
- Scalable distribution without individual acquisition costs

**Recommendation:** Further explore pilot partnerships with digital banks  integrated into youth banking propositions.

# 14. Prioritised Recommendations

## Primary Recommendation: Launch for Young Teens (13–15)

The research establishes Young Teens as the **segment for launch**. With this segment, the aesthetic lands the most strongly, the initial, linear learning journey resonates, the gamification resonates.

Older Teens (16–18) need some work to convert; Young Adults (18–25) have structural mismatches that position them as a later audience (see [[12. Segment Verdicts and Launch Readiness]]).

**Therefore: v1 of Money Monsters should be optimised specifically for 13–15 year olds.** All content, scenarios, character situations, and tone decisions should center on this segment's financial reality — pocket money, social spending, near-term saving goals.

---

## v1 of Money Monsters: Launching with Young Teens

### 1. Topics and content

**Prioritise topics by importance to 13–15 year olds.** Content priorities differ meaningfully by age band (see section 4). For the YT segment, what appears first sets the expectation for everything that follows.

**Ensure lesson titles speak to a 13–15 year old's life.** Titles must signal relevance, not just label content. *"Why does my money keep disappearing?"* communicates more than *"Budgeting with Budgie."* 

**On topic navigation for v1.** A fully topic-first experience (lesson browsing, non-linear access) is not realistic for the v1 build though the team should make their best effort to allow users to navigate/revisit through topics/lessons/key definitions 

**Preview locked content.** The progression map signals future, more-advanced topics to encourage exploration. Add a brief content teaser (e.g. lesson start page) to each locked lesson so users have a reason to progress.

### 2. Lesson framing

**Make scenario-based, dialogue-driven lessons the primary creative vehicle.** This is the product's strongest asset. The "help this person decide" structure reduces perceived difficulty and invites reasoning rather than recall. 

**Characters and scenarios must reflect 13–15 year old lives.** Characters' age, income level, and life situation must match the target user. E.g. pocket-money amounts (£5–£20 decisions), social spending moments, near-term saving goals.

**Clarify character identity from the first interaction.** Players must know whether they are inhabiting or advising the character — and this must hold consistently. Mr. Money works as an app guide but lesson characters are just as important. 

**Start with a personalisation quiz, not an assessment.** Use behavioural and situational questions over definitional tests. For 13–15 year olds this means: questions about pocket money, social spending decisions, and saving habits — not abstract defintions, credit, or formal income scenarios.

**Keep the onboarding quiz to 5–7 questions for Young Teens.** Brief enough to avoid abandonment before users reach the app's core experience. Position Mr. Money explicitly as a reassuring/friendly guide.

### 3. Lesson experience

**Key information at the point of decision.** The critical detail a learner needs to answer a question must be on the same screen as the question — not recalled from the screen before.

**Explain every answer, right or wrong.** Brief, plain-language explanations should follow every response. Being told why you're right is as valuable as being told why you're wrong.

**Avoid calculation-heavy questions.** 13–15 year olds engage better with reasoning and choice than arithmetic. Build any numerical context into the scenario framing itself rather than requiring mental maths to answer.

**Tap-for-definition across the app.** Users should never need to leave the app to understand a word in it. Extend the existing overlay mechanic consistently to onboarding, lessons, and the topic map. (Critical need evidenced in CZ — financial terminology confusion appeared across multiple interviews.)


**Quick-win gamification mechanics:**

- **XPs alongside streaks** — a cumulative progress layer that survives a missed day. Visible and accumulating is enough for v1; they don't need to be spendable yet. (Particularly valued in CZ — one participant independently proposed coin-based customization system.)
- **Monsters with consequence** — a collected monster that unlocks something (bonus content, a cosmetic, a feature) is more compelling than one that simply appears in a grid. (Strong evidence in CZ — participants wanted monsters to do something, not just collect.)
- **Progress indicator on the onboarding quiz** — a simple completion bar. Minor implementation effort; disproportionate impact on abandonment.
- **Preview the next monster at lesson completion** — show name and appearance to sustain forward pull. A display change, not a system change.

### 4. Development approach

Before committing to new prototypes or builds, work through the core design decisions in low-fidelity first — a digital whiteboard session rather than a coded prototype. Get the following right for 13–15 year olds, then test with users from this segment before moving into development:

- **Topic selection and sequencing** — which topics matter most to 13–15 year olds, in what order
- **Lesson framing** — characters and scenarios that reflect their financial reality (pocket money, social spending, near-term goals)
- **Decision design** — choices that match their actual money decisions and reasoning patterns

This is a faster, cheaper route to getting the fundamentals right — and the fundamentals are what the research shows matter most.

---

## Queue for Version 2+

These are genuine product priorities, clearly evidenced by the research. They represent expansion beyond the 13–15 year old core — either deepening engagement mechanics for that segment or extending reach to Older Teens (16–18) and Young Adults (18–25). Organised in the same sequence: content and navigation first, then lesson experience, then broader engagement and reach.

### Topics: non-linear access and situational routing

Add a situational entry point — topic index, search, or "what do you need today?" routing — as a parallel path alongside the progression map. The two models are not mutually exclusive; they require different front-door design.

Connect quiz answers to genuinely differentiated content pools: a student sees overdraft and budgeting content; a first-job user sees payslips and tax basics; a user moving out sees renting and utility costs. The quiz already asks the right questions — v2 makes the personalisation real. Extend quiz length adaptively for older segments (7–10 questions for 16–18 and 18–25 users) so the personalisation is both deeper and more credible.

### Lesson experience: more interactive question formats

Build feasible input modalities into the question mix: drag-and-drop allocation, sliders, simulated product interfaces, tap-to-reveal. Multiple choice alone asks users to pattern-match rather than reason. These are learning design improvements, not aesthetic ones. (Appetite for this confirmed in CZ — at least one YT participant requested typed answer options to break MCQ monotony.)

### The Learning Loop and social layer

Mature the v1 mechanics into a system: a spend layer for XPs (cosmetics, content unlocks, competitive features), and consequence mechanics that make monster collection matter beyond the grid. (Strong demand in CZ for streak protection/freeze feature to prevent abandonment after missed days.)

Add the social layer in sequence: streak comparison with friends first, then friend group leaderboards, then public competitive features. Basic social features should be free — they are adoption and retention drivers. Competitive depth can be a premium differentiator.

### Age-differentiated visual themes and guide options

Develop a visual skin for the 16–18 segment — more minimal, less toy-like — that preserves the progression and collection architecture under a different aesthetic register. This would materially expand the addressable audience without a structural redesign.
