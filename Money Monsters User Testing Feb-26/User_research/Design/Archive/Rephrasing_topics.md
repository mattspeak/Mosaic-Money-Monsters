# Rethinking Learning Topics for Money Monsters

**Date:** February 2026
**Context:** User-centred topic framework informed by landscape analysis and engagement levers

---

## Current Status

### Original Topics (Money-Centred)
Abstract concepts that don't centre the user's relationship with money:
- Budget
- Currency
- Tracking
- Vault
- Credit
- Foresight

### Proposed Topics (User-Centred)
Action-oriented verbs representing what users DO with money:
- **Earn** — Getting money
- **Spend** — Using money
- **Save** — Keeping money
- **Borrow** — Using money you don't have yet
- **Protect** — Keeping money safe
- **Grow** — Making money work for you

---

## Assessment of Proposed Framework

| Topic | Strength | Consideration |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| **Earn** | ✓ Universal, action-oriented | Solid across all ages |
| **Spend** | ✓ Most relatable entry point | Could lead — it's where teens are now |
| **Save** | ✓ Core skill, clear | Well understood |
| **Borrow** | ✓ Important for 16+ | Less relevant for 13-15s |
| **Protect** | ⚠ Slightly defensive/negative | Could reframe as "Keep it safe" or "Guard it" |
| **Grow** | ⚠ Slightly abstract | "Make it work" or "Grow it" feels more active |

**Overall:** This framework is strong because it:
- Uses verbs/actions — things YOU do with money
- Maps to the user's real relationship with money
- Is simple and intuitive
- Scales across age groups (lessons within can differ by age)

---

## Alternative Framing Considerations

### Option 1: Reorder by User Journey
Rather than starting with "Earn" (which young teens have little control over), consider leading with **what they're already doing** — **Spend**:

**Spend → Save → Earn → Borrow → Protect → Grow**

Rationale: Meet users where they are (spending pocket money/birthday money) before teaching earning

### Option 2: User Mindset Progression

| Stage | User Mindset | Topic |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| 1 | "I have some money" | **Get it** (Earn) |
| 2 | "I want to use it" | **Use it** (Spend) |
| 3 | "I want to keep some" | **Keep it** (Save) |
| 4 | "I need more than I have" | **Borrow it** |
| 5 | "I don't want to lose it" | **Guard it** (Protect) |
| 6 | "I want more over time" | **Grow it** |

This uses even simpler, more active language and makes the progression clearer.

## Recommendations

### ✅ Keep the Framework
The **Earn, Spend, Save, Borrow, Protect, Grow** structure is valid and user-centred. It aligns with:
- **Proposition:** Clear outcomes ("what's in it for me?")
- **Relevance:** Connects to user's real money relationship
- **Capability:** Simple language, sophisticated concepts

### 🔄 Consider These Refinements

1. **Soften/Activate Language:**
   - "Protect" → "Guard it" or "Keep it safe"
   - "Grow" → "Grow it" or "Make it work"

2. **Entry Point by Age:**
   - **13-15:** Could start with **Spend** (what they're doing now) or **Save** (immediate value)
   - **16-18:** Could start with **Earn** (getting their first jobs)
   - **18-25:** Could start with **Spend** (budgeting reality) or **Borrow** (credit matters)

3. **Layer Lessons by Age:**
   - Same topics across all ages
   - Different lessons unlocked based on age/progression
   - Avoids "too childish" for older users while maintaining structure

4. **Lead with Outcomes:**
   - Every topic intro: "By the end of [TOPIC], you'll know how to..."
   - Every lesson: Clear tangible skill or knowledge gained
   - Frame as empowerment, not education

### 🎯 Next Steps for User Testing

To test proposition and relevance:
1. **Test topic names** — Do users instantly understand what each topic is about?
2. **Test lesson titles** — Do they sound relevant to each age segment?
3. **Test entry points** — Which topic should come first for each age band?
4. **Test outcome framing** — Does "By the end you'll know how to..." resonate more than "Learn about..."?

## Content Principles to Apply

Based on landscape analysis engagement levers:

### 1. PROPOSITION — "What's in it for me?"
Lead with tangible outcomes, not abstract concepts
- ❌ "Learn about interest"
- ✅ "Know how to make your savings grow faster"

### 2. RELEVANCE — "Connect it to my world"
Frame in user's context, not textbook definitions
- ❌ "Interest is the cost of borrowing money"
- ✅ "When your £10 becomes £11, that's interest working"

### 3. CAPABILITY — "Can I understand this?"
Translate complexity, don't remove it
- ❌ Remove "compound interest" because it's hard
- ✅ Reframe as "Growth on top of growth — the snowball effect"

### 4. UTILITY — "Does this have a use?"
Link to functional outcomes
- ❌ Abstract quiz: "Should you save money? True or false"
- ✅ Scenario-based: "You just got £20 for your birthday. What's the smartest thing to do?"

## Summary

**The proposed framework is sound.** It centres on the user's relationship with money through action-oriented topics. The key now is:

1. Ensuring lessons within each topic are **age-appropriate and relevant**
2. Leading with **clear outcomes** ("By the end, you'll know how to...")
3. Using **relatable language** and scenarios, not textbook definitions
4. Testing whether the **topic order** should vary by age segment

This structure positions Money Monsters to deliver on the engagement levers that make financial education stick: clear proposition, genuine relevance, appropriate capability framing, and utility that connects to real life.
