# Young Teens Session Guide (Ages 13-15)
## Money Monsters User Testing — Tailored Research Protocol

A specialised session design for the youngest segment, prioritising engagement, clarity, and age-appropriate techniques.

---

## Segment Profile

| Aspect | Young Teens (13-15) |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Money reality** | Limited autonomy; pocket money/allowances; savings for specific items; dependent on parents |
| **Functional job** | Understand money in ways that connect to my life |
| **Emotional job** | Feel smart about something adults find hard |
| **Social job** | Not be embarrassed to have this on my phone |
| **Attention span** | ~8 seconds average; need quick wins and visible progress |
| **Key risk** | Feels like school → instant disengagement |

### Moderator Reminders

1. **Never like homework** — keep it playful, not academic
2. **Frequent variety** — switch activities every 5-7 minutes maximum
3. **Visual and tactile** — drawing, pointing, ranking > long verbal answers
4. **Peer validation** — what would friends think matters enormously
5. **Respect their intelligence** — provide structure without coming across as condescending

---

## Session Flow (60 min)

| Section                        | Time   | What We're Finding Out                                                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------------------ | ------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Warm-up                        | 5 min  | Building rapport with money drawing exercise + getting to know them. Essential for teens to feel comfortable giving honest feedback.                                               |
| Money mindset profile          | 3 min  | Understanding their attitudes towards money (prudent vs reckless, confident vs anxious, etc.) through age-appropriate attitudinal statements.                                      |
| Relationship with money        | 3 min  | Their baseline reality: income sources, saving habits, parental influence, school lessons — dovetailing with their drawing to avoid duplication.                                   |
| Confidence and learning        | 5 min  | Where they're confident vs. uncertain, current sources of guidance (parents, school, self-directed), what they actually remember learning.                                         |
| Jobs-to-be-done prioritisation | 4 min  | What matters most to them: functional, emotional, and social outcomes they want from a money learning tool.                                                                        |
| Expectations                   | 2 min  | Quick check: barriers/scepticisms about money learning tools; what would put them off?                                                                                             |
| Proposition                    | 6 min  | Does the proposition land instantly? Do they see themselves as the target? How does it compare to alternatives they'd actually use?                                                |
| Money Monsters app walkthrough | 30 min | Observing real behaviour — where they get stuck, what they understand, learning format, gamification effectiveness (min 5min), topic relevance (min 5min).                         |
| Close                          | 2 min  | Honest verdict, what's missing, one thing to change — buffer time included.                                                                                                        |

## 1. WARM-UP: YOU AND MONEY (5 min)

### Aim

- Break "interview" formality / reduce nerves
- Build rapport and trust
- Get initial baseline of their relationship with money

### The Exercise

**Setup (30 sec)**
> "Before we get into anything, I want to start with something a bit different. I want you to draw something for me — and you don't need to be good at drawing."

**Draw You and Money (2 min)**
> "Draw me a picture about you and money. Show me: how you earn it, what you spend it on, and where you keep it. Stick figures are totally fine. You've got 2 minutes."

While they draw, prompt gently if needed:
- "Where does the money come from?"
- "What happens to it?"
- "Where does it live?"

**Show and Tell (2 min)**
> "Brilliant — hold it up to the camera and talk me through it."

Listen actively, then probe:
- "What's the most recent thing here that happened?"
- "Is there anything about money you wish was different in this picture?"

**Quick Get-to-Know-You (30 sec)**
> "Quick ones about you..."

- "What are you into at the moment?" / "What apps do you use most?"
- "How do you usually hear about new stuff — friends, videos, just trying things?"

## 2. MONEY MINDSET PROFILE (3 min)

### Aim

- Understand their attitudes and behaviors with money
- Map their position on key spectrums: prudent vs impulsive, confident vs anxious, present vs future-focused
- Done through relatable statements, not abstract questions

### The Exercise

> "I'm going to read out some statements about money. For each one, tell me if it sounds like you, not really like you, or somewhere in the middle. There's no right answer — just what feels true for you."

**Read each statement and capture their response:**

1. **Spending style:** "When I get money, I usually spend it pretty quickly"
   - Probe if middle: "What makes you hold onto it vs spend it?"

2. **Planning ahead:** "I prefer buying stuff I want now, rather than saving up for something bigger later"
   - Probe if middle: "Give me an example of each"

3. **Confidence:** "I feel pretty confident when I'm spending my own money"
   - Probe: "What makes you feel that way?" / "When do you feel less sure?"

4. **Impulse control:** "I've bought things and then regretted it straight after"
   - Probe if yes: "What kind of things?" / "Why the regret?"

5. **Money awareness:** "I usually know roughly how much money I have without checking"
   - Probe: "Do you track it, or just remember?"

6. **Goal-setting:** "I've got something specific I'm saving up for right now"
   - Probe: "What is it?" / "How's it going?"

**Quick summary back to them:**
> "So it sounds like you're [describe their pattern] — does that feel right?"

## 3. RELATIONSHIP WITH MONEY (3 min)

### Aim

Fill in the practical details from their drawing — without duplicating what they already shared. Focus on:
- Amounts and frequency of money
- Saving patterns and goals
- Parental/school influence
- What actually sticks from money education

### The Questions

> "I want to dig into a few more details from your drawing..."

**Reference their drawing specifically** to avoid repetition:

- "You mentioned [pocket money/job/gifts] — roughly how much do you get, and how often?"
  - If they didn't mention: "So do you get regular money, or is it more random?"

- "Do you have a bank account?"
  - Probe: "Is it one you can access yourself, or do your parents control it?"
  - If yes: "Current account, savings, or both?"

- **Saving (only if not fully covered in drawing):**
  - "Are you actively saving for something right now, or just spending as you go?"
  - If saving: "What for? How close are you?"
  - If not: "Is it more that you don't have money left over, or just no reason to save?"

- **Parental influence:**
  - "Do your parents get involved with what you spend money on, or is it up to you?"
  - "Do they give you any rules about money?"

- **School money lessons:**
  - "Have you done anything about money at school?"
  - If yes: "What do you actually remember from it?"
  - If no or vague: "So would you say you know about money from family, or have you taught yourself?"

## 4. CONFIDENCE AND LEARNING (5 min)

### Aim

- Understand where they're confident vs uncertain with money
- Map their learning sources: school, parents, self-directed, nowhere
- Assess knowledge retention and gaps
- Gauge appetite for learning more

### Part A: Self-Assessment (1 min)

> "If you had to rate yourself with money..."

- "1-10, how good would you say you are with money?"
  - Probe: "What makes you say [number]?"
  - Probe: "What would make it higher? What would you need to know or be able to do?"

### Part B: Learning from School (1.5 min)

> "Let's talk about where you've learned about money..."

- "Has your school taught you anything about money?"
  - If yes: "What have they covered?"
  - **Recall probe:** "Give me an example of something specific you remember"
  - "Did it actually help, or was it just... there?"
  - "Was it interesting or boring?"
  - If no/vague: Move to parents

### Part C: Learning from Parents (1.5 min)

- "What about your parents or family — do they talk to you about money?"
  - If yes: "What kind of things do they say?"
  - "Do they try to teach you stuff, or is it more just rules?"
  - **Recall probe:** "What's something specific they've told you that stuck?"

### Part D: Self-Directed Learning (1 min)

- "Have you ever looked things up about money yourself?"
  - If yes: "Where did you look?" (YouTube, TikTok, Google, Reddit, etc.)
  - "What were you trying to figure out?"
  - "Did you find what you needed?"
  - If no: "If you didn't understand something about money, what would you do?"

> "Is there anything about money you wish you understood better right now?"

## 5. JOBS-TO-BE-DONE PRIORITISATION (4 min)

### Aim

Understand what outcomes matter most to them when it comes to learning about money — functional, emotional, and social jobs.

### Setup

> "I'm going to show you some different reasons why someone your age might want to learn about money. None of them are wrong — they're just different. I want to know which ones matter most to you."

Present cards/list with these jobs. Ask them to rank or sort into **High / Medium / Low importance** to them personally.

### The Jobs

**Functional Jobs:**
1. "Stop making dumb mistakes with money"
2. "Know how to make my money last longer"
3. "Understand the money stuff adults talk about"
4. "Be able to save up for the things I actually want"
5. "Make better decisions about what to buy"

**Emotional Jobs:**
1. "Feel more confident when spending or saving money"
2. "Not feel stupid or left behind when it comes to money"
3. "Feel like I'm getting better at being independent"

**Social Jobs:**
1. "Not be embarrassed if friends see me learning about money"
2. "Be someone my friends come to for money advice"
3. "Keep up with what my friends already know"

### The Exercise

**Option 1 (if remote):** Share screen with list, ask them to name their top 3 and bottom 2.

**Option 2 (more engaging):** Read them aloud, get an immediate reaction for each ("High, medium, or low?").

### Debrief (1 min)

> "What made [top choice] the most important?"
> "Why was [bottom choice] less important to you?"

## 6. EXPECTATIONS (2 min)

### Aim

- Quick check on barriers and deal-breakers before seeing the app
- What would immediately turn them off

### The Questions

> "Before I show you what we've been working on, quick reality check..."

- "If someone made an app to help you learn about money, what would make you delete it straight away?"
  - Probe: "What would feel wrong about it?"

- "What would make you actually keep using it?"
  - Probe: "Like, what would need to be in it for you?"

- "Would you want friends to know you're using something like that, or keep it private?"

---

## 7. PROPOSITION (6 min)

### Blink Test (2 min)

**Setup**
> "Now I'm going to show you something we've been working on. But I'm only showing it for about 5 seconds. I'll ask you what stood out, and then we'll look at it again."

1. Show landing page screenshot for ~5 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   - "What did you spot?"
   - "What stood out to you?"
   - "Who do you think it's for?"
   - "Would you download it? Quick answer, yes or no."
   - "Why?"

### First Impressions — Emoji Board (1 min)

Show them the landing page/home screen and ask:
> "Pick emojis that match your reaction"

Offer options:
- 🔥 = fire, this is good
- 🤔 = not sure yet
- 😬 = bit cringe
- 👶 = feels too young for me
- 🧠 = feels smart
- 😴 = looks boring
- 🎮 = feels like a game
- 📚 = feels like school

> "Why those ones?"

### Deeper Read (3 min)

> "Looking at it in a bit more detail, what stands out?"
> "TO ADD PROBES WHICH ARE SPECIFIC TO THE PROPOSITION ONCE DEFINED BY MM TEAM"
> "What do you think will be inside the app?"

## 8. APP WALKTHROUGH (30 min)

### Aim

- Observe real behavior — where they get stuck, what they understand
- Response to the learning format
  - Navigation clarity and intuitive understanding
  - Content comprehension and age-appropriateness
  - Engagement level throughout
  - Pain points and confusion
  - Gamification effectiveness
  - Topic relevance and gaps

| Step | Time | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| Figma setup & priming | 4 min | Get them ready to think aloud |
| First impressions | 2 min | Capture instinctive reaction |
| Onboarding quiz | 3 min | Observe how questions land |
| Onboarding debrief | 3 min | Emotional capture while fresh |
| Lesson completion | 6 min | Does the learning format work? |
| Gamification check | 5 min | Rewards, progress, motivation to return |
| Learning topics | 5 min | Which topics resonate? Gaps? |
| Free exploration | 2 min | Let them poke around naturally |

---

### 5.1: Figma Setup & Priming (4 min)

**Technical Setup**
- Share Figma link via Zoom (screen share with remote control, or link as backup)
- Moderator loads the prototype ready

**Priming the Think-Aloud**
> "I'm going to share my screen and show you an app. I'll let you play with it. What's super important is that you tell me what you're thinking as you're playing — even if it's 'I don't get this'. It's not fully finished yet so it won't 100% work and we can change it with your ideas."

**Practice Run (for younger participants)**

To them comfortable narrating before they see the app.

If they seem unsure, do a quick practice:
> "Let's try it. I'll demonstrate with something simple. I'm going to describe having a drink out of this tea. "
> "Now you try — describe doing and thinking about brushing your teeth"

---

### Step 2: First Impressions (2 min)

**Resources:** Screen 1 (Welcome/landing screen), Screen 2 (Modified homepage)

**Approach**
1. Show the welcome/landing screen
2. Silent observation for 15-20 seconds — let them look
3. Then ask:

> "What's your first impression?"
> "What would you tap first?"
> "What do you think about the design?"

**Watch for:** Confusion, recognition, interest, dismissal. Note what they look at first.

---

### Step 3: Onboarding Quiz (3 min)

**Resources:** Onboarding quiz intro + tailored questions for segment

**Approach**
> "Go ahead and go through this. Tell me what you're thinking as you do."

**What to observe:**
- Do questions feel judgemental or empowering?
- Does difficulty feel right? Too easy? Intimidating?
- Any anxiety, hesitation, sighs, smiles?
- Speed of completion

**Minimal intervention** — only prompt if truly stuck. Note what they say naturally.

---

### Step 4: Onboarding Debrief (3 min)

**Resources:** None — conversation only. Stop remote control to focus participant.

Immediate emotional capture while it's fresh.

> "How did that feel?"
> "You said [X] when you were going through it — how come?"
> "Did you understand why it was asking you those questions?"
> "Did the result feel accurate?"

---

### Step 5: Lesson Completion (6 min)

**Resources:** Full lesson flow (Lesson intro → Lesson screens → Lesson finished screens)

**Approach**
> "Okay, now try one of the lessons. Keep telling me what you're thinking."

**Minimal intervention** — let them work through. Only prompt if truly stuck.

**What to observe:**
- Confusion, re-reads, skips
- Engaged body language vs. fidgeting/looking away
- Do they actually learn something?
- Is content pitched right for their level?

If they go quiet, gentle prompts:
- "What are you thinking?"
- "Does that make sense?"
- "What do you think that means?"

---

### Step 6: Gamification Check (5 min)

**Resources:** Rewards screens, progress indicators, achievement systems

**Stop remote control for conversation.**

**Part A: Lesson Debrief (2 min)**

> "During the lesson you said [X] — how come you said that?"
> "What did you just learn? Can you explain it in your own words?"

**Part B: Rewards & Progress (3 min)**

> "What did you just earn?"
> "Show me where you can see your progress"

Probes:
- "Does that make you want to keep going?"
- "What would make you come back tomorrow?"
- "Is this the kind of thing that would get boring after a while, or keep you interested?"
- "If you came back in a week, what would you expect to see?"
- "What would make you feel like you're actually getting somewhere?"

**Compare to their expectations:**
> "Earlier you said [reference their JTBD priorities]. Does this give you that, or not really?"

---

### Step 7: Learning Topics (5 min)

**Resources:** Topics screen showing different monsters/topics

> "Talk me through what you're seeing here."
> "What do you think each of these is about?"

**Let them explore for 1-2 minutes, then probe:**

> "Where would you tap first? Why?"
> "Which of these feels important to you right now?"
> "Which of these feels like it would actually help you in real life?"
> "Any that feel totally un-important or irrelevant?"

**Gap analysis:**
> "Is there anything missing? Like a topic you'd want to learn about that isn't here?"
> "If you could only do three of these, which three would you pick?"

**Compare to their JTBD priorities:**
> "Earlier you said [their top JTBD] was important. Do any of these topics connect to that?"

**What you're learning:**
- Do they understand the monsters/topics structure?
- Which topics resonate with their life right now?
- Are topics clearly understood or confusing?
- Any gaps — something they'd want that isn't there?

---

### Step 8: Free Exploration (2 min)

**Resources:** Full prototype access

> "You've got two minutes — go wherever you want. I'll just watch and see where you go naturally."

**Observe silently. Only intervene if completely stuck.**

**What to watch for:**
- What do they gravitate towards?
- What do they ignore?
- Any moments of confusion or delight?
- Natural navigation patterns

---

## 9. CLOSE (2 min)

### Aim

Final honest verdict. Buffer time included for overruns.

### The Questions

> "Last couple of questions..."

**The Big One:**
> "If you could change ONE thing to make this actually good, what would it be?"

**Quick-fire reactions:**
> "I'll start a sentence, you finish it with your gut reaction..."

- "The best thing about it is..."
- "The worst thing about it is..."
- "I would use this if..."

**Final word:**
> "Anything else you want to say that we haven't talked about?"

Thank them genuinely.
Confirm incentive.

## Moderation Tips for Young Teens

### Do

- **Use their language** — mirror back the words they use
- **Keep energy up** — vary your tone, keep things moving
- **Praise the honesty** — "That's really useful" when they're critical
- **Give choices** — "Want to keep going or try something else?"
- **Use visuals** — show, point, rank rather than just talk
- **Normalise not knowing** — "A lot of people find this confusing"

### Don't

- **Don't use "so..." leading phrases** — they'll agree with whatever follows
- **Don't explain or defend the app** — if they're confused, note it, don't fix it
- **Don't fill silences too fast** — but don't let them last more than 5-7 seconds
- **Don't be too formal** — this isn't an exam
- **Don't say "great job"** — stay neutral, not encouraging/discouraging

### Recovery Moves

| Situation | What to Do |
|-----------|-----------|
| They shut down | Switch to something visual: "Let's try something different — look at these screens" |
| One-word answers | Offer options: "Is that like, boring-boring, or just not-for-me?" |
| They're confused | Normalise: "That's the point — we need to know what's unclear" |
| They're bored | Speed up: "Quick fire round — give me one word for each of these" |
