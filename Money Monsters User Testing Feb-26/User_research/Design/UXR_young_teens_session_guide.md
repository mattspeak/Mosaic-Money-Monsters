# Young Teens Session Guide (Ages 13-15)
## Money Monsters User Testing — Tailored Research Protocol

A specialised session design for the youngest segment, prioritising engagement, clarity, and age-appropriate techniques.

---

## Segment Profile

| Aspect | Young Teens (13-15) |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Money reality** | Limited autonomy; pocket money/allowances; savings for specific items; dependent on parents |
| **Functional job** | Understand money in ways that connect to my life |
| **Emotional job** | Feel smart about something adults find hard |
| **Social job** | Not be embarrassed to have this on my phone |
| **Attention span** | ~8 seconds average; need quick wins and visible progress |
| **Key risk** | Feels like school → instant disengagement |

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Never feel like homework** — keep it playful, not academic
2. **Frequent variety** — switch activities every 5-7 minutes maximum
3. **Visual and tactile** — drawing, pointing, ranking > long verbal answers
4. **Peer validation** — what would friends think matters enormously
5. **Respect their intelligence** — scaffold without condescending

---

## Session Flow (60 min)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  YOUNG TEENS SESSION: 60 min (50 min content + 10 min buffer)   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

1. ICEBREAKER & DRAW YOUR MONSTER (8 min)          ← Creative warm-up
2. QUICK-FIRE MONEY VIBES (7 min)                  ← Low-pressure context
3. DREAM MONEY APP (5 min)                         ← Expectations via creation
4. BLINK TEST & FIRST REACTIONS (8 min)            ← Proposition discovery
5. APP WALKTHROUGH — NARRATED PLAY (15 min)        ← Think-aloud, gamified
6. RAPID REACTIONS & RANKING (7 min)               ← Quick exercises
7. WRAP-UP & FINAL WORD (5 min)                    ← Close on their terms
BUFFER: 5 min
```

## 1. ICEBREAKER & DRAW YOUR MONSTER (8 min)

### Why Start with Drawing

Drawing is disarming. It:
- Breaks the "interview" formality immediately
- Gives them something to do with their hands (reduces nerves)
- Creates a reference point we can return to throughout
- Is unexpected — signals this won't be boring

### The Exercise

**Setup (1 min)**
> "Before we get into anything, I want to start with something a bit different. This app is called Money Monsters — it's all about learning money stuff. So before you even see it, I want you to draw something for me."

**Draw your Money Monster (4 min)**
> "If money was a monster — like a creature that represents how you feel about money — what would it look like? Just grab a pen and paper and sketch it out. Doesn't have to be good. Stick figures totally fine. You've got 3 minutes."

While they draw, prompt gently:
- "Is it scary? Friendly? Confusing?"
- "What kind of creature is it?"
- "What's it doing?"

**Debrief the drawing (3 min)**
> "Tell me about your monster"
- "What makes it [scary/friendly/weird]?"
- "Has it always looked like this, or has it changed?"
- "If you could tame this monster, what would you need?"

### What You're Learning

| Monster Type | What It Suggests |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Scary/threatening** | Anxiety about money; may need reassurance-focused messaging |
| **Confusing/weird** | Lack of understanding; clear explanations critical |
| **Small/manageable** | Reasonable confidence; check for overconfidence vs. genuine grasp |
| **Friendly** | Positive relationship; may be less motivated to learn |
| **Blank/reluctant** | Avoidance; money isn't on their radar yet |

### Transition
> "That's brilliant. Keep that monster in mind — we'll come back to it later. Now I want to understand a bit more about you..."

## 2. QUICK-FIRE MONEY VIBES (7 min)

### Why Quick-Fire

Long open questions overwhelm young teens. Short, rapid questions:
- Keep energy high
- Feel more like a game
- Get authentic, less rehearsed answers

### Part A: About You Speed Round (2 min)

> "Okay, quick-fire round. Just say the first thing that comes to mind."

- "What's your favourite app right now?"
- "What's the last thing you watched that was actually good?"
- "How do you usually learn new stuff — videos, trying things, asking someone?"
- "School lesson you hate the most?"
- "School lesson you actually don't mind?"

### Part B: Money Speed Round (3 min)

> "Same thing, but money edition."

- "Do you have your own money?"
- "Cash, card, or app?"
- "Where does your money come from?"
- "Last thing you spent money on?"
- "Last thing you saved up for?"
- "1-10, how much do you think about money?"
- "1-10, how good are you with money?"
- "Who taught you about money?" (If they say no one, follow up: "Where do you pick stuff up from?")

### Part C: Emoji Money Check (2 min)

> "If you had to pick one emoji that describes how you feel about money, what would it be?"

Show them options on screen or let them pick from their phone:
😎 💪 🤔 😬 🫤 😴 😰 🤷

> "Why that one?"

### What You're Learning

- Current engagement level with money
- Confidence vs. knowledge gap
- Emotional baseline
- Learning preferences (videos/interactive/asking)

---

## 3. DREAM MONEY APP (5 min)

### Why Create Before Viewing

Having them design their ideal solution:
- Surfaces expectations we can compare the app against
- Reveals what features/styles matter to them
- Gives them ownership before critique

### The Exercise

**Option A: Quick Sketch (if drawing went well)**
> "Imagine the perfect app for learning money stuff. Not boring, not like school. Draw me what the home screen would look like. What would be on it?"

**Option B: Would You Rather Build (if they're less visual)**
> "Let's build the perfect money app. Quick choices — pick A or B."

| Choice A | Choice B |
|----------|----------|
| Quick 2-minute lessons | Longer deep-dive lessons |
| Badges and achievements | Streaks and scores |
| Bright colours and characters | Clean and minimal |
| Learn by reading | Learn by doing |
| Start from basics | Jump to what you want |
| See how friends are doing | Keep it private |
| Serious tone | Fun/silly tone |

**Follow-up:**
> "What would make you actually open it every day?"
> "What would make you delete it?"

### What You're Learning

- Format preferences (short vs. long, game vs. course)
- Social features appetite
- Retention drivers and deal-breakers

---

## 4. BLINK TEST & FIRST REACTIONS (8 min)

### Blink Test (3 min)

**Setup**
> "Now I'm going to show you what we've been working on. But I'm only showing it for a few seconds, then I'm taking it away."

**Execute**
1. Show app store screenshot for 5-8 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   - "What was that?"
   - "Who do you think it's for?"
   - "In one word, what's the vibe?"
   - "Would you download it? Quick answer, yes or no."
   - "Why?"

### First Impressions — Emoji Board (3 min)

Show them the landing page/home screen and ask:
> "Pick emojis that match your reaction"

Offer options:
- 🔥 = fire, this is good
- 🤔 = not sure yet
- 😬 = bit cringe
- 👶 = feels too young for me
- 🧠 = feels smart
- 😴 = looks boring
- 🎮 = feels like a game
- 📚 = feels like school

> "Why those ones?"

### Friend Test (2 min)

> "If this appeared on your phone and a friend saw it, what would they think?"
> "Would you care? Would you hide it?"

### What You're Learning

- Instant proposition clarity
- Age-appropriateness perception
- Social acceptability (critical for this segment)

---

## 5. APP WALKTHROUGH — NARRATED PLAY (15 min)

### Framing as Play, Not Test

> "Okay, now you're in control. I want you to play with this like you'd play with any app you just downloaded. Tell me what you're thinking as you go — even if it's 'this is boring' or 'I have no idea what this is.'"

### Warm-Up for Think-Aloud (1 min)

> "Quick practice: pick up something near you and describe putting it down while you do it."

(Gets them comfortable narrating actions)

### Task 1: Free Exploration (5 min)

> "You just downloaded this. Show me what you'd do."

Prompts if they go quiet:
- "What are you looking for?"
- "What do you think that does?"
- "What's going through your head?"

### Task 2: Find Something (3 min)

> "You want to learn something about saving money. Find somewhere that helps with that."

Observe:
- Where do they look first?
- Do they understand the navigation?
- How quickly do they find it?

### Task 3: Try a Lesson or Quiz (5 min)

> "Go ahead and try that — talk me through it as you do."

After each screen/question:
- "What do you think of that question?"
- "Was that easy or hard?"
- "Did that make sense?"

### Comprehension Check (2 min)

After they complete something:
> "In your own words, what did you just learn?"
> "Could you explain that to a friend?"

### What You're Learning

- Navigation clarity
- Content comprehension
- Engagement level (are they bored? interested?)
- Pain points and confusion

---

## 6. RAPID REACTIONS & RANKING (7 min)

### Quick Ranking Activity (3 min)

Show 4-5 screenshots from different parts of the app. Ask them to rank:

> "Put these in order: favourite to least favourite"

Then:
> "Why is that one top?"
> "Why is that one bottom?"

### This or That (2 min)

Quick-fire comparisons:
- "This app or TikTok for learning money stuff?"
- "This app or asking ChatGPT?"
- "This app or asking your parents?"
- "This app or learning at school?"

> "Why?"

### Fix One Thing (2 min)

> "If you could change ONE thing to make this actually good, what would it be?"

### What You're Learning

- Relative value of features
- Competitive positioning
- Priority fixes

---

## 7. WRAP-UP & FINAL WORD (5 min)

### Monster Return (1 min)

Go back to their drawing:
> "Look at your money monster from the start. Would using this app help you tame it? Why or why not?"

### Sentence Completion (2 min)

Quick-fire endings:
- "Using this app would make me feel..."
- "I would use this if..."
- "The best thing about it is..."
- "The worst thing about it is..."
- "I would tell my friends it's..."

### Final Word (2 min)

> "One piece of advice for the people making this — what should they know?"

> "Anything else you want to say that we haven't talked about?"

Thank them genuinely. Confirm incentive.

## Activity Bank: Additional Exercises

Use these as alternatives or if you have extra time.

### Meme It (3 min)
> "If you had to make a meme about this app, what would it be?"

Let them describe or sketch the meme. Reveals honest perception in their native language.

### TikTok Pitch (3 min)
> "You're making a 15-second TikTok about this app. What do you say? What's the hook?"

Tests: Can they articulate the value? In what language?

### Red Flag / Green Flag (3 min)
Show features or screenshots:
> "Red flag or green flag?"
> "Why?"

Quick way to identify what works vs. what kills interest.

### Secret Diary Entry (4 min)
> "Imagine you used this for a week. Write a one-sentence diary entry about it."

Gets at emotional experience and likely behaviour.

### Friendship Grouping (3 min)
> "Think of your different friend groups. Which group would actually use this?"

Probes social context and perceived target audience.

## Moderation Tips for Young Teens

### Do

- **Use their language** — mirror back the words they use
- **Keep energy up** — vary your tone, keep things moving
- **Praise the honesty** — "That's really useful" when they're critical
- **Give choices** — "Want to keep going or try something else?"
- **Use visuals** — show, point, rank rather than just talk
- **Normalise not knowing** — "A lot of people find this confusing"

### Don't

- **Don't use "so..." leading phrases** — they'll agree with whatever follows
- **Don't explain or defend the app** — if they're confused, note it, don't fix it
- **Don't fill silences too fast** — but don't let them last more than 5-7 seconds
- **Don't be too formal** — this isn't an exam
- **Don't say "great job"** — stay neutral, not encouraging/discouraging

### Recovery Moves

| Situation | What to Do |
|-----------|-----------|
| They shut down | Switch to something visual: "Let's try something different — look at these screens" |
| One-word answers | Offer options: "Is that like, boring-boring, or just not-for-me?" |
| They're performing | Lean into hypotheticals: "What would your most cynical friend say?" |
| They're confused | Normalise: "That's the point — we need to know what's unclear" |
| They're bored | Speed up: "Quick fire round — give me one word for each of these" |

## Key Signals to Watch For

| Behaviour | What It Might Mean |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Immediate engagement, exploring freely** | Good intuitive navigation, proposition landed |
| **Hesitation, waiting for instruction** | Unclear what to do; needs better onboarding |
| **"This is like school"** | Fatal positioning problem for this segment |
| **"My friends would think..."** (negative) | Social acceptability concern — design/branding issue |
| **Quick answers, not reflecting** | Disengaged; might need to re-energise |
| **Comparing to Duolingo positively** | Gamification working; familiar mental model |
| **"I already know this"** | Content might be too basic; check if true or overconfidence |
| **Fidgeting, looking away** | Bored; switch activity |
