# Jobs to Be Done Analysis: Money Monsters

## Framing

"Improving financial literacy" is not a Job to Be Done — it's an aspiration, and one that researchers and educators project onto young people. The target audience (particularly those from lower-income households) are not seeking financial education. They're navigating a relationship with money that is often stressful, confusing, and socially loaded.

Jobs should be framed as the progress someone wants to make when they *hire* this product — specific enough to test with prototype screens and inform proposition, onboarding, and content decisions.

---

## Young Teens (13-15)

**Context:** Limited money (pocket money, birthday gifts). No real financial responsibility but *aware* of household money stress. Forming identity; peer belonging is critical. Learning happens informally through observation and experience.

### Functional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Help me understand money stuff that actually connects to my life right now" | Does content reference pocket money, saving for things they want, situations they recognise? |
| Secondary | "Help me figure out how to make my pocket money go further" | Do they see practical value in the content, or does it feel abstract? |

### Emotional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Make me feel smart and capable about something adults find hard" | Does completing content make them feel clever, or patronised/bored? |
| Secondary | "Help me feel less confused about the money stuff I hear about at home" | Does the app demystify concepts they've overheard but not understood? |

### Social Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Be something I wouldn't be embarrassed to have on my phone" | Would they use this openly? Does the visual design feel age-appropriate? |
| Secondary | "Be something I could mention or show friends if it's actually good" | Is there social upside, or only risk? Would they share/recommend? |

---

## Older Teens (16-18)

**Context:** Earning real money (part-time work, ~£300-400/month). Facing real decisions: university debt, driving lessons, budgeting wages. Higher skepticism; acutely aware of being marketed to. Want to be treated as near-adults, not children.

### Functional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Help me make smart decisions about real money situations I'm facing now" | Does content address part-time wages, student loans, first bank accounts? |
| Secondary | "Help me build knowledge I'll need for what's coming next" | Does the app signal progression toward adult financial competence? |

### Emotional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Help me feel confident and prepared, not overwhelmed or talked down to" | Does completing content build confidence? Or does it feel like homework/lecture? |
| Secondary | "Help me feel protected — like I won't get caught out or taken advantage of" | Does the app help them feel savvier about scams, bad deals, traps? |

### Social Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Respect my intelligence — feel credible and mature, not childish or preachy" | Does the tone feel appropriate for a 17-year-old? Or aimed at younger kids? |
| Secondary | "Be something I could recommend without looking like I need basic help" | Would they tell a mate about this? Or would that feel exposing? |

---

## Young Adults (18-25)

**Context:** Fully financially independent with real constraints (rent, bills, limited income). Many feel stuck or behind. Know they have gaps from poor/absent financial education. Skeptical of institutions, time-poor, often in "survival mode" cognitively.

### Functional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Help me finally learn the money basics I missed, fast and without fuss" | Does content feel efficient and gap-filling? Or slow and redundant? |
| Secondary | "Help me handle specific real situations I'm actually dealing with" | Does content address rent, payslips, credit scores, BNPL traps? |

### Emotional Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Help me feel more in control, not more anxious or judged" | Does engaging with the app reduce stress? Or highlight how little they know? |
| Secondary | "Help me feel like I'm making progress even when the bigger picture feels stuck" | Does completion/gamification create genuine sense of momentum? |

### Social Jobs

| Priority | Job | Testable Via |
|----------|-----|--------------|
| **Primary** | "Recognise my actual reality — don't assume wealth, graduates, or easy paths" | Does content feel inclusive of different income levels and routes? Or middle-class? |
| Secondary | "Be something I can use as an adult without it feeling juvenile" | Does the monster/gamification aesthetic work, or feel embarrassing for a 22-year-old? |

---

## Summary: Priority Jobs by Segment

| Segment | Top Functional | Top Emotional | Top Social |
|---------|---------------|---------------|------------|
| **Young Teens** | Understand money in ways that connect to my life | Feel smart about something adults find hard | Not be embarrassed to have this on my phone |
| **Older Teens** | Make smart decisions about real situations I'm facing | Feel confident and prepared, not lectured | Feel credible and mature, not childish |
| **Young Adults** | Learn the basics I missed, fast | Feel more in control, not more anxious | Recognise my actual reality |

## Implications for Research

### Proposition Testing
- Does the value proposition signal the primary functional job for each segment?
- Which framing makes them feel "this is for me" vs "this is for someone else"?

### Onboarding
- Does early content deliver on the primary emotional job (feeling smart/confident/in control)?
- Or does it undermine it (feeling patronised/overwhelmed/judged)?

### Content Relevance
- Does content connect to situations they actually face?
- Or does it feel abstract, school-like, or aimed at a different life stage?

### Social Acceptability
- Would they use this openly, or hide it?
- Does the aesthetic (monsters, gamification) work across all segments, or alienate older users?

### Competitive Frame
- Why would they choose this over ChatGPT (instant, personalised), TikTok (entertaining, relatable), or doing nothing?
- What unique value does a structured, gamified app provide that alternatives don't?

---

## The "Doing Nothing" Competition

For lower-income young people, the real competition is often:

- **Avoidance:** "I'll deal with money when I actually have some"
- **Denial:** "It doesn't matter, I'll never have enough anyway"
- **Shame suppression:** "I don't want to think about this"
- **TikTok/AI:** "I'll just ask when I need to know something specific"

An app positioning as "education" triggers the same response as homework. An app positioning around the primary jobs (feeling smart, prepared, in control) may break through.
