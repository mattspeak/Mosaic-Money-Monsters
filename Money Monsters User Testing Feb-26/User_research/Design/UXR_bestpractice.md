## Research Objectives Recap

This isn't just usability testing — it's product-market fit validation. Every exercise should help answer:

| Objective | What We Need to Know |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Proposition clarity** | Do they instantly get what this is and why it matters to them? |
| **Triggers & barriers** | What would prompt them to try/return vs. drop off? |
| **Usability & comprehension** | Can they navigate and understand without help? |
| **Engagement & motivation** | Does gamification create momentum? |
| **Competitive choice** | Why would they choose this over alternatives? |

### Jobs to Be Done Focus

Exercises should probe whether the app delivers on the primary jobs for each segment:

| Segment | Functional Job | Emotional Job | Social Job |
|---------|---------------|---------------|------------|
| **13-15** | Understand money in ways that connect to my life | Feel smart about something adults find hard | Not be embarrassed to have this on my phone |
| **16-18** | Make smart decisions about real situations I'm facing | Feel confident and prepared, not lectured | Feel credible and mature, not childish |
| **18-25** | Learn the basics I missed, fast | Feel more in control, not more anxious | Recognise my actual reality |

## Think-Aloud Protocols

### What It Is
Participants verbalise their thoughts while performing tasks — what they're looking at, thinking, doing, and feeling. Nielsen calls this "the #1 usability tool."

### Two Approaches

| Aspect | Concurrent Think-Aloud (CTA) | Retrospective Think-Aloud (RTA) |
|--------|------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **When** | During task performance | After task completion |
| **Pros** | Real-time reactions; detects navigation issues | No interference with natural behaviour |
| **Cons** | May alter behaviour; cognitive load | Memory decay |
| **Best for** | Live problem identification | Expert users; detailed reflection |

**For Money Monsters:** Use **Concurrent** for navigation and usability tasks, **Retrospective** for proposition and emotional reaction probing.

### Practical Tips

1. **Warm participants up** with a simple practice task: "Describe putting your phone in your pocket while you do it"
2. **Prompt gently** when they fall silent: "What are you thinking?" or "Keep talking"
3. **Use the echo technique**: repeat their last phrase with slight interrogatory tone to encourage elaboration
4. **Accept imperfection**: most people self-edit; your job is to get as close to raw stream of thought as possible

## Task & Scenario Design

### Core Principles

**1. Goal-oriented, not step-oriented**
Transform research questions into realistic actions.

❌ "Click on Level 1, Lesson 3"
✅ "You want to learn something about budgeting. Find a lesson that interests you."

**2. Avoid UI terminology**
Never use words that appear in the interface.

❌ "Use the vocabulary collection feature"
✅ "You learned a new money term earlier. How would you find it again?"

**3. Provide context, not instructions**

❌ "Complete the baseline knowledge check"
✅ "You've just downloaded this app. What would you do first?"

### Task Writing Checklist

- [ ] Describes a **goal**, not steps
- [ ] **Avoids UI language** (button names, menu labels)
- [ ] Includes **enough context** to feel realistic
- [ ] **Doesn't reveal the solution** path
- [ ] Is **achievable** within session time
- [ ] Reflects **actual user motivations**
- [ ] Links to a **JTBD** we're testing

## Working with Young People

### Age Band Considerations

| Age | Key Characteristics | Session Implications |
|-----|---------------------|---------------------|
| **13-15** | Need strong scaffolding; forming identity; peer belonging critical | Shorter tasks; clearer instructions; more check-ins; avoid anything that feels like school |
| **16-18** | Higher scepticism; real-money relevance; acutely aware of being marketed to | Avoid condescension; acknowledge their expertise; treat opinions seriously |
| **18-25** | Financially independent; know they have gaps; time-poor, skeptical of institutions | Treat as adults; respect their constraints; don't assume wealth or easy paths |

### Critical Success Factors

**1. Never condescend**
> "Nothing turns off teenagers more than someone speaking to them condescendingly. You're the apprentice and they're the master."
> — Blink UX

**2. Attention span realities**
- Gen Z average attention span: ~8 seconds
- Under-25s switch focus every ~39 seconds
- TikTok videos under 15 seconds have 80% completion

**Implication**: Design exercises with quick wins and visible progress. Long, complex tasks will lose them. Break sessions into varied, punchy activities.

## Warm-Up & Rapport Building

### Why It Matters
> "Sessions without a warm-up are good but never great."

Use the **first 5-7 minutes** to build connection before any research questions. This is especially critical for teens.

### Effective Warm-Up Questions

**Universal openers:**
- "Tell me a bit about yourself"
- "What do you like to do in your spare time?"

**Context-relevant for Money Monsters:**
- "How do you usually learn new things — videos, apps, asking people, something else?"
- "Have you ever tried any apps like Duolingo or similar? What did you think?"
- "What's the last thing you watched or played that you'd actually recommend?"

**Money context (brief opener):**
- "Is money something that comes up in your life much, or not really?"

### Transition to Money Context
> "Great, that's really helpful. Before I show you what we're working on, I'd love to understand a bit more about how money fits into your life right now..."

## Money Context Discovery (10-12 min)

### Why This Matters
Understanding participants' existing relationship with money is essential context for interpreting everything that follows. Without this foundation, we risk misreading reactions to the app — someone might seem disengaged because the content is too basic for them, or confused because they lack prerequisite concepts.

**This section should happen BEFORE showing the app.** It establishes:
- Their current knowledge and confidence level
- Real situations they're navigating
- Emotional relationship with money
- Current learning behaviours and sources

### Current Money Reality (5-6 min)

**Start broad, then narrow:**

*Day-to-day money:*
- "Walk me through how money works in your life right now. Do you have your own money to manage?"
- "What do you actually spend money on week to week?"
- "Do you have a bank account? A card? Cash?"

*Sources and control:*
- "Where does your money come from — allowance, job, gifts, something else?"
- "How much control do you have over what you do with it?"
- "Do you have to ask before spending, or is it your call?"

*Age-specific probes:*

| Age Band | Additional Questions |
|----------|---------------------|
| **13-15** | "Do your parents give you money regularly, or is it more for specific things?" / "Have you ever saved up for something yourself?" |
| **16-18** | "Do you have a job or side hustle?" / "Are there money decisions you're starting to make on your own now?" |
| **18-25** | "What money stuff do you handle yourself vs. what do your parents still help with?" / "What's been the biggest money learning curve since becoming more independent?" |

### Money Feelings & Confidence (3-4 min)

**Emotional relationship:**
- "When you think about money, what's the first feeling that comes to mind?"
- "Is money something you feel sorted on, or is it a bit stressful/confusing?"
- "Has there been a time recently when you felt good about a money decision? Or frustrated by one?"

**Confidence probe:**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how confident do you feel about handling money stuff?"
- "What would it take to feel more confident?"

**Gaps and questions:**
- "Is there anything about money you wish you understood better?"
- "Have you ever been in a situation where you thought 'I have no idea how this works'?"

### Current Learning Behaviours (2-3 min)

**How they learn now:**
- "When you need to know something about money — like how something works or what to do — where do you go?"
- "Have you ever actually looked something up about money? What was it?"
- "Has anyone taught you about money? School? Parents? YouTube?"

**Quality of current sources:**
- "Do you feel like you're getting good information, or is it hard to know what to trust?"
- "Is there stuff about money you feel like you should know but no one's really explained?"

### Key Signals to Listen For

| Signal | What It Indicates |
|--------|------------------|
| **"I don't really think about it"** | Low engagement baseline — may need clear relevance hook |
| **"My parents handle it"** | Limited autonomy — app needs to feel like preparation, not current utility |
| **"I've had to figure it out myself"** | High motivation, potential power user — watch for "too basic" reactions |
| **"It stresses me out"** | Emotional barrier — tone and framing critical |
| **"I just Google/ask ChatGPT"** | Active learner with existing habits — competitive context matters |

## Expectations & Ideal Solution (5-7 min)

### Why This Matters
Before showing the app, we need to understand what participants would *want* from a money learning solution. This creates a benchmark against which they'll unconsciously judge Money Monsters — and surfaces expectations that might otherwise remain hidden.

**This section bridges the gap between:**
- Their current reality (what they just told us)
- Their reaction to the app (what we're about to show them)

### Ideal Solution Probes (3-4 min)

**Open the imagination:**
- "Imagine something existed that could actually help you get better with money. What would that look like?"
- "Would it be an app, a person, a video, something else entirely?"
- "What would it need to do for you to actually use it?"

**Format and style preferences:**
- "Would you want something quick you could dip into, or something more structured like a course?"
- "Would you want it to feel serious and professional, or more fun and casual?"
- "Would you rather learn by reading, watching, doing, or something else?"

**Content expectations:**
- "What topics would it need to cover for you?"
- "Would you want it to start from scratch, or jump to the stuff you actually care about?"

### Barriers & Scepticism (2-3 min)

**Surface doubts before they see the app:**
- "What would put you off something like this? What would make you think 'this isn't for me'?"
- "Have you ever tried anything like this before? What happened?"
- "What would make you trust it — or not trust it?"

**Social considerations:**
- "Would you use something like this openly, or would you want it to be private?"
- "Would it matter what your friends thought of it?"

### What You're Listening For

| Response Pattern | Implication for App Evaluation |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| **"Quick and easy"** | Will react negatively to lengthy content or complex navigation |
| **"Something that actually relates to my life"** | Watch for reactions to relevance of examples |
| **"Not preachy or boring"** | Tone sensitivity — monitor reactions to language |
| **"I'd need to see results"** | Progress/achievement mechanics may be important |
| **"I wouldn't want people to see it"** | Social acceptability of branding/design matters |
| **"I've tried stuff before and gave up"** | Probe what went wrong — retention barrier insight |

### Transition to App
> "That's really interesting. So I'm going to show you something we've been working on — it's an app about money for people your age. I want your completely honest reaction. There are no right or wrong answers, and you won't hurt anyone's feelings by being critical..."

## Moderation Techniques

### The Golden Rule
**You are the apprentice; they are the master.** Your job is to learn from them, not guide them.

### During Tasks

| Do | Don't |
|----|----|
| Use open-ended prompts: "What are you thinking?" | Ask leading questions: "Is that confusing?" |
| Echo their words: "You said 'weird'—what did you mean?" | Interpret their behaviour: "So you found that frustrating" |
| Note **facts and observations** | Record your **assumptions** |
| Stay neutral in body language | Nod/smile when they're going wrong |
| Let silence happen | Rush to fill pauses |

### Useful Prompts

**When they're stuck:**
- "What would you normally do here?"
- "What are you looking for?"
- "What did you expect to happen?"

**When they succeed too quickly:**
- "Walk me through what you just did"
- "What made you choose that option?"

**When they're silent:**
- "Keep talking, please"
- "What's going through your mind?"

## Core Session Exercises

These exercises map to the proposal's core research techniques. Use them in sequence for a structured session.

### 1. Blink Test — First Impressions (3-5 min)

**Purpose:** Test proposition clarity, relevance, and memorability. Mirrors real-world app discovery.

**How to run:**
1. Show app store listing/screenshot for 5-8 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   - "What was that?"
   - "Who do you think it's for?"
   - "What would make someone download that?"
   - "Would you? Why/why not?"

**Variations:**
- Show landing page hero section
- Show onboarding first screen
- Compare Money Monsters vs. competitor screenshot

**What you're testing:**
- Does the proposition land instantly?
- Do they see themselves as the target audience?
- Is the value clear without explanation?

---

### 2. Think-Aloud Navigation (15-20 min)

**Purpose:** Observe reality vs. intention. Identify usability issues and comprehension gaps.

**How to run:**
1. Give participant control of the Figma prototype
2. Set a goal-oriented task (see examples below)
3. Ask them to narrate their thoughts as they navigate
4. Prompt gently when silent: "What are you looking for?" / "Keep talking"

**Example tasks:**

*First encounter:*
> "You've just downloaded this app. Show me what you'd do."

*Goal-finding:*
> "You want to understand how interest works. Find something that would help."

*Return use:*
> "You started a lesson yesterday but didn't finish. Pick up where you left off."

**What you're testing:**
- Can they navigate confidently?
- Where do they hesitate or get stuck?
- Do labels and icons make sense?
- Is the information architecture intuitive?

---

### 3. Comprehension Probes (Integrated throughout)

**Purpose:** Test understanding vs. click-through. Make sure they're actually learning.

**How to run:**
After completing any lesson content, ask:
- "How would you explain that to a friend?"
- "In your own words, what's the main thing you just learned?"
- "Can you give me an example from your own life?"

**Harder probe (for older segments):**
- "Do you actually believe that? Does it match your experience?"
- "Is there anything there you'd want to check or look up elsewhere?"

**What you're testing:**
- Is content pitched at the right level?
- Are explanations clear and memorable?
- Does it connect to their real life?

---

### 4. Context & Usage Mapping (5-7 min)

**Purpose:** Understand functional, social, and emotional context of potential use.

**How to run:**
Show a few screens they've seen, then ask:
- "Where would you use something like this? On the bus? At home? In class?"
- "Would you use it alone, or with other people?"
- "When would you be most likely to open it? What would trigger that?"
- "Would you use it once to learn something, or come back regularly?"

**Follow-up probes:**
- "What would make you come back tomorrow?"
- "What would make you delete it?"

**What you're testing:**
- Usage context (solo vs. social, classroom vs. personal)
- Habit potential vs. one-time use
- Triggers and barriers to engagement

---

### 5. Forced Trade-Offs (5-7 min)

**Purpose:** Expose real priorities by forcing choices. Cut through polite approval.

**How to run:**
Present binary choices and ask them to pick:

*Content trade-offs:*
- "Would you rather have: shorter lessons you can do quickly, or deeper lessons that explain more?"
- "Would you rather: start with basic stuff and build up, or jump straight to the topics you care about?"

*Feature trade-offs:*
- "If you could only have one: streaks that track how often you use it, or badges for completing topics?"
- "Would you rather: see how you compare to others, or keep your progress private?"

*Value trade-offs:*
- "If this cost money, would you rather: pay once to unlock everything, or pay monthly for less?"
- "Would you use this more if: your school made you, or you found it yourself?"

**What you're testing:**
- What's non-negotiable vs. nice-to-have?
- Which features drive value vs. which are ignored?
- Monetisation receptivity

## Deeper Insight Exercises

These exercises probe motivation, behaviour, and competitive context. Use 2-3 per session based on time.

### 6. JTBD Switch Interview (7-10 min)

**Purpose:** Understand what triggers action vs. inertia. Explores pushes, pulls, and anxieties.

**How to run:**
After they've used the app, explore the "hiring" decision:

*Pushes (what's pushing them away from current state):*
- "Think of a time you wished you knew more about money. What was happening?"
- "Has there been a situation where you felt caught out or confused about money stuff?"

*Pulls (what attracts them to a solution):*
- "If something like this actually worked for you, what would that look like?"
- "What would it take for you to actually use this regularly?"

*Anxieties (what holds them back):*
- "What's the worst thing about an app like this?"
- "Is there anything that would make you not want to use it, even if it was good?"

*Habits (what they're doing now):*
- "Right now, when you need to know something about money, what do you do?"
- "Why would you switch from [their current approach] to this?"

**What you're testing:**
- Real triggers for seeking financial knowledge
- Barriers to adoption (including doing nothing)
- Competitive positioning vs. current behaviours

---

### 7. Progress Mapping (5-7 min)

**Purpose:** Explore what "getting better with money" actually means to them.

**How to run:**
Use a simple scale or journey framework:

> "Imagine a scale from 1-10 where 1 is 'I know nothing about money' and 10 is 'I'm completely confident handling all money stuff.'"

- "Where would you put yourself right now?"
- "Where would you want to be in a year?"
- "What would it take to move up one number?"
- "What would you actually notice in your life if you were at [their target]?"

**Follow-up after app exploration:**
- "Does this app feel like it would move you up that scale? By how much?"
- "What's missing that would help you move faster?"

**What you're testing:**
- Their self-perception of financial competence
- Gap between current and desired state
- Whether app connects to their progress definition

---

### 8. Competitive Contrast — "Eye Test" (7-10 min)

**Purpose:** Force comparison to reveal relative value. Users articulate preferences they can't express in isolation.

**How to run:**
Show Money Monsters alongside alternatives (prepare screenshots):

*App vs. App:*
Show a competitor financial literacy app
- "Which one feels more like it's for you?"
- "Which one would you actually use?"
- "What does [competitor] do better? What does [Money Monsters] do better?"

*App vs. Content:*
Show a TikTok finance creator screenshot
- "If you wanted to learn about budgeting, which would you go to first?"
- "What can you get from [TikTok] that you can't get from an app?"
- "What can you get from an app that you can't get from [TikTok]?"

*App vs. AI:*
- "If you had a money question, would you rather ask ChatGPT or use something like this?"
- "What's the difference? When would you use each?"

**What you're testing:**
- Competitive positioning
- Unique value proposition vs. real alternatives
- Where the app wins and loses

---

### 9. Projective Prompts (5-7 min)

**Purpose:** Uncover tone sensitivity and emotion through indirect techniques.

**How to run:**
Use sentence completion or association:

*Sentence completion:*
- "Using this app would make me feel..."
- "People who use apps like this are..."
- "If this app was a person, they would be..."
- "The best thing about this is... The worst thing is..."

*Association:*
- "What three words come to mind when you see this?"
- "If this was a brand you know, which brand would it be like?"
- "Is this more like homework or more like a game?"

*For older participants:*
- "Would you feel embarrassed if someone saw this on your phone? Why/why not?"
- "Would you tell a friend about this? What would you say?"

**What you're testing:**
- Emotional response to brand/tone
- Social acceptability
- Perceived positioning (educational vs. entertaining)

---

### 10. Co-Creation Exercises (5-10 min)

**Purpose:** Surface unmet needs and ideal positioning through creative tasks.

**How to run:**
Pick one activity based on participant energy:

*Pitch exercise:*
> "Imagine you had to convince your most sceptical friend to try this. What would you say to them?"

*TikTok caption:*
> "If you were posting about this on TikTok or Instagram, what would the caption be?"

*Fix-it task:*
> "If you could change one thing about this to make it actually good, what would it be?"

*Missing feature:*
> "What's not here that would make you actually want to use this?"

*Naming exercise:*
> "Forget 'Money Monsters' — if you were naming this, what would you call it?"

**What you're testing:**
- How they'd position/communicate the value
- Unmet needs and feature gaps
- Language that resonates with their peers

---

### 11. Novelty vs. Habit Probe (5 min)

**Purpose:** Determine if this is "one and done" or has repeat-use potential.

**How to run:**
After exploring the app:
- "Would you open this again tomorrow? What about next week?"
- "Is this something you'd use once to learn something, or keep coming back to?"
- "What would make you remember to open it?"
- "If you finished all the lessons, would you keep using it? Why?"

*Social angle:*
- "Would you share this with anyone? Who?"
- "Would you do this with a friend, or is it more a solo thing?"

**What you're testing:**
- Habit formation potential
- Shareability and viral loops
- Retention drivers

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Facilitation Errors

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| **Leading questions** | Hypothesis confirmation | Pre-write questions; use "what" not "do you think" |
| **Helping when stuck** | Discomfort watching struggle | Embrace productive struggle; redirect with questions |
| **Excessive encouragement** | Social nicety | Neutral responses: "Thank you" vs "Great job!" |
| **Filling silence** | Awkwardness | Wait 5-7 seconds before prompting |

### Task Design Errors

| Mistake | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **Using UI terminology** | "Tap the Settings icon" | "Change your preferences" |
| **Being too specific** | "Find the Level 2 Budget lesson" | "Find something about managing money" |
| **Being too vague** | "Explore the app" | "You want to learn about saving—find somewhere to start" |

### Bias Traps

1. **Confirmation bias**: Seeing what you expect to see
2. **Social desirability**: Participants saying what they think you want
3. **Observer effect**: Behaviour changes because they're watched

**Mitigation:**
- Debrief after each session
- Have multiple observers
- Look for disconfirming evidence
- Pay attention to behaviour, not just words

## Recommended Session Flow (60 min session)

**Design Principles:**
- 10-minute buffer built in (50 min of content)
- Generous warm-up time - essential for teens to feel comfortable and open up
- Focused app time - if it's just onboarding/quiz, 15 mins is realistic
- JTBD thinking woven throughout, not as separate section
- Reflection that's appropriate for young people

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  TOTAL SESSION: 60 min (50 min content + 10 min buffer)        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

1. WARM-UP & RAPPORT (10 min)
├── Welcome, consent, explain format (2 min)
│   └── "We're testing the app, not you - be as honest as you like"
├── Get to know them (5 min)
│   ├── "Tell me a bit about yourself"
│   ├── "What do you like doing in your free time?"
│   ├── "How do you usually learn new things - videos, apps, people?"
│   └── "What's something you've watched/played recently you'd recommend?"
└── Bridge to money (3 min)
    └── "Is money something that comes up in your life much, or not really?"

   ➤ Goal: Build genuine rapport. They need to feel safe before honest feedback.

2. MONEY CONTEXT & GUIDANCE SOURCES (8 min)
├── Current reality (4 min)
│   ├── "Walk me through how money works in your life right now"
│   ├── "Where does your money come from?"
│   └── "How much control do you have over what you do with it?"
├── Confidence & gaps (2 min)
│   ├── "How confident do you feel about money stuff, 1-10?"
│   └── "Is there anything you wish you understood better?"
└── Where they go (2 min)
    ├── "When you need to know something about money, where do you go?"
    └── "Has anyone taught you about money - school, parents, YouTube?"

   ➤ Goal: Understand their baseline and current behaviours.
   ➤ Objectives served: #2 (triggers/barriers), context for all insights

3. EXPECTATIONS OF A SOLUTION (5 min)
├── Ideal solution (3 min)
│   ├── "If something existed that could help you get better with money, what would it look like?"
│   ├── "Would you want quick bits or something more structured?"
│   └── "What would it need to do for you to actually use it?"
└── Barriers & scepticism (2 min)
    ├── "What would put you off something like this?"
    └── "Would you use it openly or want it private?"

   ➤ Goal: Set the benchmark before showing the app.
   ➤ Objectives served: #2 (barriers), #4 (motivation drivers)

4. PROPOSITION DISCOVERY (8 min)
├── Blink test (3 min)
│   ├── Show app store screenshot for 5-8 seconds, then hide
│   ├── "What was that? Who do you think it's for?"
│   └── "Would you download it? Why/why not?"
├── First impressions discussion (3 min)
│   ├── "What stands out to you?"
│   ├── "How would you explain this to a friend?"
│   └── "Does it feel like it's for someone like you?"
└── Quick competitive pulse (2 min)
    └── "If you wanted to learn about money, would you use this or [TikTok/ChatGPT/Google]? Why?"

   ➤ Goal: Test if the proposition lands instantly.
   ➤ Objectives served: #1 (proposition clarity), #2 (competitive choice)

5. APP WALKTHROUGH - THINK ALOUD (15 min)
├── Setup (1 min)
│   └── "I'm going to give you control. Talk me through what you're thinking as you go."
├── First encounter (5 min)
│   └── "You've just downloaded this. Show me what you'd do."
│   └── Let them navigate naturally, prompt: "What are you thinking?"
├── Onboarding / quiz experience (7 min)
│   └── Observe the baseline check / first lesson
│   └── Comprehension probes: "How would you explain that to a friend?"
│   └── "Did anything confuse you?"
└── Usage context (2 min)
    ├── "Where would you use this - bus, home, class?"
    └── "Would you use it alone or with others?"

   ➤ Goal: Observe real behaviour, identify friction points.
   ➤ Objectives served: #3 (usability), #4 (engagement)

6. REFLECTION & CLOSE (7-10 min)
├── Overall reaction (3 min)
│   ├── "Now you've seen it - what's your honest reaction?"
│   ├── "What's the best thing about it? What's the worst?"
│   └── Sentence completion: "Using this would make me feel..."
├── Return & retention (3 min)
│   ├── "Would you open this again tomorrow? Next week?"
│   ├── "What would make you remember to open it?"
│   └── "What's missing that would make you actually want to use it?"
├── Social proof (2 min)
│   ├── "Would you tell a friend about this? What would you say?"
│   └── "Would you feel OK if someone saw this on your phone?"
└── Wrap (2 min)
    ├── "Anything else you want to share that we haven't covered?"
    ├── "Thanks so much - your feedback genuinely helps"
    └── Incentive/next steps
```

## Closing Well with Young People

**Why it matters:** A weak close leaves them feeling like they were just "used" for research. A good close makes them feel valued and heard.

**Do:**
- Thank them genuinely: "This is really helpful - your feedback matters"
- Give them agency: "Anything else you want to share?"
- End on their terms: "How would you describe this to someone?"
- Be clear on next steps and incentive

**Don't:**
- Rush the goodbye
- End with a leading question ("So you liked it, right?")
- Leave them uncertain about what happens next

**Good closing prompt:**
> "Before we finish - if you could give one piece of advice to the people making this app, what would it be?"

This gives them the final word and often surfaces the most memorable insight of the session.

## Client Overview: Session Structure

A high-level view of how we're allocating time during each 60-minute session and what each section is designed to uncover.

| Section | Time | Sub-sections | What We're Finding Out |
|---------|------|--------------|------------------------|
| **1. Warm-up & Rapport** | 10 min | Welcome & consent, Getting to know them, Bridge to money | Building foundation for honest, unguarded feedback. Essential for teens to feel safe before giving critical opinions. |
| **2. Money Context** | 8 min | Current reality, Confidence & gaps, Where they go now | Understanding their baseline: how money fits into their life, where they're confident vs. uncertain, and current sources of guidance (parents, Google, TikTok, etc.) |
| **3. Expectations** | 5 min | Ideal solution, Barriers & scepticism | Setting the benchmark before showing the app: what would they *want* from a money learning tool, and what would put them off? |
| **4. Proposition Discovery** | 8 min | Blink test, First impressions, Competitive pulse | Does the proposition land instantly? Do they see themselves as the target? How does it compare to alternatives they'd actually use? |
| **5. App Walkthrough** | 15 min | First encounter, Onboarding/quiz, Usage context | Observing real behaviour: where do they get stuck, what do they understand, and where/when would they actually use this? |
| **6. Reflection & Close** | 7-10 min | Overall reaction, Return & retention, Social proof | Capturing honest verdict, likelihood of repeat use, social acceptability, and unmet needs. |
| **Buffer** | 10 min | — | For overruns, follow-up threads, or technical issues. |

**Total: 60 minutes**
