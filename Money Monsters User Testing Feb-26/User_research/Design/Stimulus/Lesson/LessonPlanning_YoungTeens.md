### Screen 4 {Question: Where Your Money Lives Matters — Feedback | Syllabus link: 2.3 Bank accounts — Different types, 2.4 Interest}

On C: "Safe from the bank, maybe — but it's not earning anything. And have you seen what happens to cash under mattresses?"

## Lesson 2: Your Money Goes Global

**Syllabus source:** Level 3 — Currency, Exchange Rates, Travel Money
**Monster:** Ciaran the Currency Monster
**Core concept:** The moment you leave the country, your money plays by different rules.

---

### Screen 1 {Opening Hook | Syllabus link: 3.1 What is Currency?, 3.4 Exchange Rates Explained}

> You're going on a school trip to France. You've saved £50 spending money.
>
> But when you get there, the prices are in **euros** — and your pounds don't work.
>
> You need to swap your money. But how much will you actually get?
>
> **Welcome to currencies. Let's find out.**

[Button: Find out]

### Screen 2 {Scene: The Trip | Syllabus link: 3.1 What is Currency?, 3.6 Currency Exchange in Practice}

>
> Naya's thinking:
> - Why can't I just use pounds?
> - How much will my £50 actually be worth?
> - What's the smartest way to get euros?

[Button: Continue]

---

### Screen 3 {Concept: Different Countries, Different Money | Syllabus link: 3.1 What is Currency?}

> Just like countries have different languages, they have different **currencies** — their own type of money.
>
> - UK → Pound (£)
> - France, Germany, Spain → Euro (€)
> - USA → Dollar ($)
> - Japan → Yen (¥)
>
> When you travel, you **swap your money** into the local currency. The price of that swap is called the **exchange rate.**

[Button: Continue]

---

### Screen 4 {Concept: What's an Exchange Rate? | Syllabus link: 3.4 Exchange Rates Explained}

> An exchange rate tells you how much of one currency you get for another.
>
> Example: if **£1 = €1.10** then for every £1 you swap, you get €1.10.
>
> How much spending money would Naya get?

[Button: Find out!]

---

### Screen 5 {Question 1 — How Much Does Naya Get? | Syllabus link: 3.4 Exchange Rates Explained}

> Naya swaps her £50 at a rate of **£1 = €1.10.** How many euros does she get?

- A) £50 — it's the same amount
- B) €110
- C) €55

[Button: Submit]

### Screen 6 {Question 1 — Feedback | Syllabus link: 3.4 Exchange Rates Explained}

**Correct: C**

"£50 × 1.10 = €55. She gets more euros than pounds — because the euro is worth less than the pound right now. That's what the exchange rate tells you."

On A: "She can't use pounds in France — she needs to swap them. And when she does, the exchange rate means she actually gets a different number of euros back."
On B: "Not quite — €110 would mean every pound is worth €2.20! The rate is €1.10 per pound, so it's 50 × 1.10 = €55."

[Button: Continue]

---

### Screen 7 {Concept: Rates Change | Syllabus link: 3.4 Exchange Rates Explained}

> Naya checks the exchange rate at different times:
>
> - **A month before the trip:** £1 = €1.08 → her £50 would get **€54.00**
> - **The day before the trip:** £1 = €1.10 → her £50 would get **€55.00**
> - **Whilst she's on holiday:** £1 = €1.12 → her £50 would get **€56.00**
>
> The rate moved three times — and she didn't do anything. That's because exchange rates **go up and down all the time** based on what's happening in the world.
>
> A small difference on £50. But imagine it on £500 — it could add up!

[Button: Continue]

---

>

- A) Airport — it's the most convenient
- B) Bank — it's trustworthy
- C) Online — she gets the most euros
- D) Doesn't matter — they're all about the same

**Correct: C**

"Same rate, different fees — and it costs her €6. That's a crêpe AND a drink in Paris gone on fees. Airport exchanges are almost always the worst deal because their fees are the highest."

On A: "Convenient, sure — but €6 less. That's a whole snack gone on fees."
On D: "Actually the difference is €6 — that adds up, especially on bigger amounts."
