### Concept: Cash Flow — Why Some Months Just Don't Add Up

**Screen 8**

> Ranveer's budget looks good on paper. But last month he was still skint by week three. This month he's got money left over. Same job, same pay. What's going on?
>
> **That's cash flow** — the timing of money coming in vs going out. Not all spending is the same:

| Spending type | What it means | Ranveer's example |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed** | Same amount, every month | Phone contract — £15 |
| **Variable** | Changes month to month | Food, going out — £20–£50 |
| **One-off** | Hits you out of nowhere | Mate's birthday, new trainers |

> A budget tells you the plan. Cash flow tells you what actually happened. **When the two don't match, it's usually the variable and one-off spending that's the problem.**

[Button: Got it]

### Question 4 — Cash Flow Crunch

**Screen 9**

> Ranveer's got his £200. Budget's set: £40 savings, £100 needs, £60 wants.
>
> But this month his mate's birthday AND new trainers drop in the same week. Total damage: £90 on wants — £30 over budget.
>
> He's in **negative cash flow** this month. What's the smartest move?

- A) Dip into savings — it's only £30
- B) Cover the £30 from next month's wants budget and accept a tighter few weeks
- C) Ignore it — one bad month doesn't matter
- D) Cancel his phone contract to free up cash

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: B**

"Borrowing from next month's wants — not savings — keeps the plan intact. It's a short-term squeeze, not a crisis. This is how real cash flow management works: you flex the timing, not the goals."

On A: "£30 feels small, but raiding savings for wants is a habit that kills long-term goals fast."
On C: "One bad month is fine. But if you don't adjust, one becomes three. Negative cash flow needs a response."
On D: "That's a need — cutting it creates a bigger problem than it solves."

### Lesson 1 Wrap-Up

**Screen 10**

> **What you just learned:**
> - The 50/30/20 rule gives your money a structure — adapt it to your situation
> - Needs come first, then savings, then wants
> - **Cash flow is about timing** — when money arrives vs when it leaves
> - Fixed costs are predictable; variable and one-off spending is where budgets blow up
> - When you overshoot, borrow from next month's wants — not from savings
> - Budgeting isn't about saying no — it's about knowing the trade-offs

### Concept: Pay Yourself First & Multi-Pot Saving

**Screen 4**

> Most people save whatever's left at the end of the month. Problem? **There's usually nothing left.**
>
> The trick is to **Pay Yourself First** — move money to savings the moment you get paid, before you spend anything.
>
> Then split it into separate pots for different goals:

| Goal | Pot | Why separate? |
|---|---|---|
| Laptop (3 months) | Easy-access savings | Need it soon — must be available |
| Inter-railing (12 months) | Notice or regular saver account | Don't need it yet — can earn better interest |
| University (2+ years) | Cash ISA or fixed-term bond | Long time horizon — maximise growth, tax-free |

> **Different timelines = different accounts. That's the whole game.**

[Button: Got it]
