# Lesson 1: Your Money's Just Sitting There (Savings & Banking)

## Lesson Metadata

- **Lesson Title:** Your Money's Just Sitting There
- **Level:** Level 4
- **Character:** Ranveer (17, works weekends at a sports shop, earns ~£200/month)
- **Monster Companion:** Vinnie the Vault Monster (unlocked at end of level)
- **Core Concepts:** Pay Yourself First, 50/30/20 budgeting & digital banking tools, compound interest, banking security & phishing
- **Tone:** Conversational, slightly provocative, anchored in "your money, your choices"

---

## Flow Structure

### 1. Homepage / Entry Point

- **Screen:** Homepage Navigation
- Player sees their avatar/monster ("Wealth Wanderer")
- Entry prompt: **"Your Money's Just Sitting There"**
- CTA: Tap to begin lesson

---

### 2. Lesson Intro Sequence

#### Screen 2a — Lesson Intro 1
- **Visual:** Split screen — driving test pass certificate on one side, empty savings balance on the other
- **Headline:** "YOU WANT DRIVING LESSONS. YOUR BANK SAYS NO."
- **Body:** £1,500 for lessons. You earn £300 a month. Should be done in five months, right? But after your phone bill, bus fare, food, and actually having a life — there's nothing left.

#### Screen 2b — Lesson Intro 2
- **Headline:** "Sound familiar?"
- **Body:** Phone bill. Bus fare. Food. A night out. There's always something real to pay for — so saving never happens. It's not a willpower problem. It's an order-of-operations problem.
- **CTA:** "Start Mini-lesson" -> Next

---

### 3. Concept Introduction — Meet Ranveer

#### Screen 3a — Concept 1
- **Visual:** Ranveer character (illustrated young person in a sports shop uniform)
- **Body:** "This is Ranveer. He's 17. Works weekends at a sports shop. Earns about £200 a month. Same problem — money in, money gone."

#### Screen 3b — Level Intro
- **Visual:** Ranveer looking at his phone, £0 savings balance
- **Body:** He wants driving lessons. He wants Barcelona with mates for his 18th. But he saves whatever's left over — which is always nothing. There's a simple fix. Let's help him find it.

---

### 4. Pop Quiz 1 — Pay Yourself First

#### Screen 4a — Pop Quiz 1
- **Question:** "Ranveer gets paid £200. He wants to save, but always ends up spending it all. What's the smartest move?"
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Wait until the end of the month and save whatever's left
  - B) Move money into savings the moment he gets paid, before spending anything ✅
  - C) Spend on essentials first, then treats, then try to save
  - D) Just put it all in a jar and hope for the best

#### Feedback — Option A: Wait until end of month
- **Message:** "This is what most people do. And most people save nothing. If you wait, there's never anything left."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: Savings first ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "Pay Yourself First. Move money into savings the second you get paid — before you even look at what's in the shops. Automate it and you won't even miss it."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C: Essentials then treats then save
- **Message:** "Sounds logical, but 'treats' have a way of expanding to fill whatever's available. Savings need to come first, not last."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D: Jar
- **Message:** "A jar earns zero interest and has no lock on it. A savings account does both."
- **CTA:** Try again

---

### 5. Key Decision — The 50/30/20 Split

#### Screen 5a — Level Intro (Question Setup)
- **Visual:** Ranveer's £200 shown as a bar, waiting to be divided
- **Body:** "Ranveer's paying himself first — £40 goes straight to savings. But what about the rest? There's a simple framework that stops the rest from just... disappearing."
- **Question:** "The 50/30/20 rule splits your money into three buckets. What does the 30 stand for?"
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) 30% on savings
  - B) 30% on needs — rent, food, transport
  - C) 30% on wants — the stuff you enjoy but don't strictly need ✅
  - D) 30% on debt repayments

#### Feedback — Option A: Savings
- **Message:** "Savings is the 20. Which — if you're paying yourself first — is already sorted. The 30 is for wants."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: Needs
- **Message:** "Needs gets the biggest share — 50%. That's rent, food, phone contract, transport. The 30 is for wants — the stuff you enjoy."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option C: Wants ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "50% needs (phone, transport, food). 30% wants (going out, clothes, subscriptions). 20% savings. It's a guide, not a prison — but knowing the split means you stop wondering where it all went. For Ranveer on £200: that's roughly £100 needs, £60 wants, £40 savings."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option D: Debt repayments
- **Message:** "Debt repayments would come out of needs or be a priority on top. The 30 is for wants — the fun stuff. Budgets aren't about cutting out everything you enjoy."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Screen 5b — Making It Real With Your Banking App
- **Visual:** Ranveer's banking app showing three labelled pots: "Needs £100", "Fun £60", "Savings £40" — with a toggle showing "Auto-sort on payday: ON"
- **Body:** "Most banking apps let you do this automatically. Set up savings pots — label them, set amounts, and your money sorts itself every time you get paid. Some apps even let you create rules like 'save £1 every time I buy coffee' or round up every purchase to the nearest pound. You don't have to do the maths in your head each month. Set it once, forget it, and your 50/30/20 runs itself."
- **CTA:** Continue

---

### 6. Concept Exploration — Compound Interest

#### Screen 6a — Level Intro
- **Visual:** Two lines on a graph — one flat, one curving upward
- **Body:** "Ranveer puts £1,000 into a savings account earning 5% interest. After year one, he's got £1,050. Nice. But here's where it gets interesting..."
- **Question:** "In year two, the bank calculates interest on..."
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Just the original £1,000
  - B) £1,050 — the original plus last year's interest ✅
  - C) Just the £50 interest he earned
  - D) It doesn't matter — interest is the same every year

#### Feedback — Option A: Original only
- **Message:** "That's simple interest — boring interest. Compound interest includes the interest you've already earned. Your money makes money on its money."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: £1,050 ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "Compound interest. Year 1: £50 earned. Year 2: £52.50. It accelerates. Over decades, this is how small amounts turn into serious money — without you lifting a finger."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C: Just the interest
- **Message:** "The interest gets added to your total. Then that bigger total earns interest. That's the compound effect — and it's the closest thing to free money."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D: Same every year
- **Message:** "With compound interest, it grows MORE each year. That's literally the whole point."
- **CTA:** Try again

### 7. Scenario Challenge — The Dodgy Text

#### Screen 7a — Level Intro (Scenario Setup)
- **Visual:** Ranveer's phone buzzing with a new text notification
- **Context:** "Ranveer's feeling good — savings on autopilot, ISA sorted, compound interest doing its thing. Then this lands on his phone."
- **Visual:** Text message interface — sender shows as "YourBank"
- **Text message:** "URGENT: Unusual activity detected on your account. Your card has been temporarily frozen. Tap here to verify your identity and restore access: yourbank-verify.com/secure"
- **CTA:** "What should Ranveer do?"

#### Screen 7b — What should Ranveer do?

- **Question:** What should Ranveer do?
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Tap the link immediately — his card's been frozen and he needs to fix it
  - B) Ignore the text completely and contact his bank directly using the number on his card or app ✅
  - C) Reply to the text asking if it's real
  - D) Forward it to his mates to see if they got the same one

#### Feedback — Option A: Tap the link
- **Message:** "That link isn't from his bank. It's a phishing scam — a fake page designed to steal his login details. One tap and they've got access to everything he's been building. Banks never send links by text asking you to log in."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: Ignore and contact bank directly ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "Never trust a link in a text — even if it looks like it's from your bank. Real banks will never ask you to verify your identity through a text link. If something looks off, go straight to the official app or call the number on the back of your card. Ranveer's savings are safe because he didn't panic."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C: Reply to the text
- **Message:** "Replying confirms your number is active — now they know someone's on the other end. Never engage with suspicious messages. Go directly to your bank through the app or official number."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D: Forward to mates
- **Message:** "Asking mates is natural — but while you're group-chatting, scammers are counting on you not acting fast enough the right way. Don't click, don't reply, don't forward. Just go straight to your bank."
- **CTA:** Try again

### 4. Pop Quiz 1 — Picking the Right Account

#### Screen 4a — Pop Quiz 1
- **Question:** "Sarah's got £2,000 she won't need for two years. She's comparing accounts: an easy-access account at 2% AER, a notice account at 3.5% AER (90 days' notice to withdraw), and a 2-year fixed-term bond at 5% AER. Which is the best fit?"
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Easy-access at 2% — she might need the money at any time
  - B) Notice account at 3.5% — a good middle ground
  - C) 2-year fixed-term bond at 5% — she knows exactly when she needs it, so she can lock it away for the best rate ✅
  - D) It doesn't matter — all savings accounts are basically the same

#### Feedback — Option A: Easy-access
- **Message:** "Easy-access is great for emergency money you might need tomorrow. But Sarah knows she won't touch this for two years. She's giving up 3% in interest for flexibility she doesn't need. Over two years on £2,000, that's roughly £100+ she's leaving on the table."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: Notice account
- **Message:** "Better than easy-access — but still not the best match. A 90-day notice account makes sense when you might need the money with some warning. Sarah has a fixed 2-year timeline, so she can do better."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option C: Fixed-term bond ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "She knows the money is for uni in exactly two years. A 2-year fixed-term bond locks it away — no temptation to dip in — and pays the highest rate. At 5% AER with compound interest, her £2,000 becomes roughly £2,205 without her lifting a finger. The key principle: match the account to the goal. The less access you need, the more interest you can earn."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option D: All the same
- **Message:** "Definitely not. The difference between 2% and 5% on £2,000 over two years is over £100. Same money, same effort — just a better decision. Account choice matters."
- **CTA:** Try again

---

### 5. Key Decision — The Rule of 72

#### Screen 5a — Level Intro (Question Setup)
- **Visual:** A timeline graphic showing money doubling at different speeds
- **Body:** "There's a shortcut that shows you how powerful interest rates really are. It's called the Rule of 72. Divide 72 by your interest rate and you get roughly how many years it takes your money to double. At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 3%: 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years. Same money — but one doubles 12 years earlier."
- **Question:** "Ranveer has £1,000 in an account earning 4% interest. Sarah has £1,000 at 6%. Using the Rule of 72, roughly how many MORE years will Ranveer wait for his money to double compared to Sarah?"
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) About 2 years longer
  - B) About 6 years longer ✅
  - C) About 12 years longer
  - D) They'll double at roughly the same time — 2% difference is nothing

#### Feedback — Option A: 2 years
- **Message:** "Bigger gap than that. At 4%, Ranveer's money doubles in 18 years (72 ÷ 4). At 6%, Sarah's doubles in 12 years (72 ÷ 6). That's a 6-year difference — all from a 2% gap in interest rate."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: 6 years ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "Ranveer: 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years. Sarah: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. A 2% difference in interest rate means Ranveer waits 6 extra years to double his money. This is why comparing AER matters — small percentages create huge differences over time. Always check the rate."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C: 12 years
- **Message:** "Not quite that dramatic. Ranveer doubles in 18 years, Sarah in 12. The difference is 6 years — still significant, but not 12. The Rule of 72 makes these comparisons easy."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D: Same time
- **Message:** "2% might sound small, but the Rule of 72 reveals the truth: 4% = 18 years to double, 6% = 12 years. That's 6 extra years of waiting. In savings, small differences in rate create big differences in outcome."
- **CTA:** Try again

---

### 6. Concept Exploration — ISAs: Tax-Free Growth

#### Screen 6a — Level Intro
- **Visual:** Two identical piggy banks — one labelled "Normal Account", the other "ISA" with a shield icon
- **Body:** "Sarah's dad tells her to put her uni savings in an ISA. She says: 'What's the point? I'm not earning enough to pay tax anyway.' He says: 'Not yet. But an ISA protects your interest forever — even when you do start earning.'"
- **Question:** "Sarah can open a Cash ISA at 17. If she puts her £2,000 in a Cash ISA at 4% AER, and later starts earning enough to pay tax, what happens to the interest she earns inside the ISA?"
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) She'll have to start paying tax on the interest once she's earning above the threshold
  - B) The interest stays completely tax-free — forever, no matter how much she earns ✅
  - C) She gets taxed on it when she withdraws the money
  - D) ISAs are only for people over 18, so she can't open one yet

#### Feedback — Option A: Taxed once earning
- **Message:** "That's how a normal savings account works. The whole point of an ISA is that the interest earned inside it is never taxed — regardless of your income, now or in the future. It's a permanent shield."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: Tax-free forever ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "That's the power of an ISA. Every penny of interest earned inside it is tax-free — now and in the future, no matter how much Sarah eventually earns. At 17, she can open a Cash ISA. When she turns 18, she could also look at a Lifetime ISA (LISA), where the government adds a 25% bonus on top — up to £1,000 free per year — towards a first home or retirement. Free money from the government, if you plan ahead."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C: Taxed on withdrawal
- **Message:** "ISAs aren't like pensions — there's no tax when you withdraw. The interest is tax-free when it's earned, and the money is tax-free when you take it out. That's what makes them so useful."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D: Over 18 only
- **Message:** "Cash ISAs are available from age 16. Junior ISAs (JISAs) are for under-18s and managed by parents. Sarah at 17 can absolutely open a Cash ISA — and she should."
- **CTA:** Try again

### 7. Scenario Challenge — What If the Bank Goes Bust?

#### Screen 7a — Level Intro (Scenario Setup)
- **Visual:** Ranveer looking at his phone, slightly worried expression
- **Context:** "Sarah's got her savings sorted — fixed-term bond for uni, ISA for tax-free growth. Then Ranveer sends her a message that makes her think about something she hadn't considered."
- **Visual:** Popup text message interface: New text from Ranveer
- **Text message:** "My uncle had savings in a bank that went bust a few years ago. He lost thousands. Should we be worried about where we keep our money?? 😬"
- **CTA:** "Reply to Ranveer"

#### Screen 7b — Reply to Ranveer

- **Question:** Reply to Ranveer
- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Yeah that's scary — maybe we should just keep cash at home where it's safe
  - B) Don't worry — the government guarantees everything, there's no limit
  - C) Banks in the UK are covered by the FSCS — it protects up to £85,000 per person, per banking group. Your uncle might not have known about it, or had more than that in one place ✅
  - D) That can't happen anymore — banks aren't allowed to fail

#### Feedback — Option A: Keep cash at home
- **Message:** "Cash at home earns no interest, isn't protected against theft or fire, and loses value to inflation every year. Bank savings with FSCS protection are far safer — up to £85,000 is guaranteed even if the bank fails."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B: No limit
- **Message:** "There IS a limit — £85,000 per person, per banking group. If you've got more than that (lucky you), you need to spread it across different banking groups to stay fully protected. The protection is real, but it has a ceiling."
- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option C: FSCS covers £85,000 ✅ (Correct)
- **Message:** "The FSCS (Financial Services Compensation Scheme) automatically protects up to £85,000 per person, per banking group. No need to apply — it just kicks in. Ranveer's uncle may have had savings above the limit in one bank, or the bank may not have been UK-regulated. The lesson: check your bank is FSCS-protected, and if your savings ever grow past £85,000, spread them across different banking groups."
- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option D: Can't happen
- **Message:** "Banks can still fail — it's rare, but it happens. That's exactly why the FSCS exists. It's not about trusting that nothing will go wrong — it's about knowing you're protected if it does."
- **CTA:** Try again

## Narrative & Pedagogical Notes (Both Lessons)

### Tone
- Conversational and slightly provocative ("Your money's just sitting there", "Is it growing fast enough?")
- Non-judgmental but direct about consequences
- Uses relatable older-teen scenarios (sports shop job, driving lessons, saving for uni, banking apps)
- Framed around the user's personal relationship with money — not abstract finance

### Feedback Pattern
- **Correct:** Brief positive reinforcement + concept explanation with real-world framing
- **Incorrect:** Explains WHY the answer is wrong in practical, relatable terms, then loops back
- **Partially correct:** Acknowledged as valid but gently redirected
- No shaming — incorrect feedback always teaches

### Progression Logic
- Linear flow: intro -> concept -> question -> feedback -> next concept
- Questions build on each other within each lesson
- Lesson 1: save first -> budget split -> compound interest -> spot scams
- Lesson 2: right account type -> Rule of 72 -> ISAs -> FSCS protection
- Both lessons stay within Level 5 (Savings & Banking) syllabus
- Monster companion (Vinnie) continues across both lessons

### Text Message Mechanic
- Each lesson includes one scenario framed as a text exchange
- Lesson 1: Ranveer gets a phishing text (player decides what to do)
- Lesson 2: Ranveer texts about his uncle losing savings in a bank collapse (player replies)
- Multiple choice answers framed as text message replies

## Narrative & Pedagogical Notes (Both Lessons)

### Tone
- Conversational and slightly cheeky ("Silence won't kill him", "Future you might not love that")
- Non-judgmental but firm on correct financial behaviour
- Uses relatable older-teen scenarios (sports shop job, driving lessons, Barcelona trip, Nando's, birthdays)

### Feedback Pattern
- **Correct:** Brief positive reinforcement + concept explanation
- **Incorrect:** Explains WHY the answer is wrong in practical terms, then loops back
- **Partially correct (e.g., spend birthday money):** Acknowledged as valid but gently redirected
- No shaming — incorrect feedback always teaches

### Progression Logic
- Linear flow: intro -> concept -> question -> feedback -> next concept
- Questions build on each other within each lesson
- Lesson 1: framework -> classify -> track -> apply under pressure
- Lesson 2: save first -> where to save -> how it grows -> prioritise goals
- Monster companion unlocked at each level end

### Ranveer Text Message Mechanic
- Each lesson includes one scenario framed as a text exchange
- Lesson 1: Tom texts Ranveer about a night out (player replies as Ranveer)
- Lesson 2: Ranveer texts Sarah about birthday money (player replies as Sarah)
- Multiple choice answers framed as text message replies
