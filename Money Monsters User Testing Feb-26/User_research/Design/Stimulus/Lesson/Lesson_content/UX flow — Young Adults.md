# Money Monsters — Young Adults UX Flow

> **Purpose of this document:** Structured UX flow reference for the young adults segment (18-25). Two lessons covering credit (Level 6) and investing/financial planning (Levels 7 & 8). Follows the screen-by-screen format of the app prototype. Use this to build Figma screens and understand sequence, tone, question logic, and feedback patterns.

---

## Segment Notes — Young Adults (18-25)

- **Assumed knowledge:** Basic financial literacy (earning, saving, budgeting, accounts). They've lived through these — this isn't abstract.
- **Life context:** First jobs, first flats, first credit products. Managing real money with real consequences. Some at university, some working full-time, some doing both. Not all are graduates — many went straight into work and have real-world financial experience but gaps in formal financial knowledge (pensions, investing, credit mechanics).
- **Income reality:** Ranges widely. Some have spare cash, many don't. Content must feel relevant whether you've got £150/month spare or £20. Don't assume disposable income — acknowledge that even small steps matter.
- **Tone:** Direct, no-nonsense, slightly wry. Not patronising. They don't need to be told what a bank account is — they need to understand the mechanics that catch people out. Never preachy about saving — many know they should but can't yet.
- **Characters:** Sarah (24, first proper job, managing credit and starting to invest) and Antonio (slightly older, saving for mortgage, thinking longer-term)

### 3. Pop Quiz 1 — The Credit Card Trap

#### Screen 3a — Question

- **Visual:** Credit card with balance shown
- **Body:**
  > Sarah's 24. First proper job, first flat. She puts £800 on her credit card — meals out, a new jacket, some online shopping. Her minimum payment is £20.
  >
  > If she only pays the minimum each month, what happens?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) The £800 slowly disappears on its own
  - B) She pays interest on the remaining balance — potentially paying hundreds more over time
  - C) The bank writes off the rest after 12 months
  - D) Her credit score improves because she's making regular payments

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (B)

- **Body:**
  > That's it. Pay the minimum on £800 at a typical 22% APR and you could end up paying over £200 in interest — and it takes years to clear. The minimum keeps you in good standing, but it's designed to keep you in debt longer.
  >
  > But what does "22% APR" actually mean? And why does it matter so much?

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Incorrect (A, C, D)

- **Body:**
  > The minimum payment covers barely more than the interest. The remaining balance keeps accruing charges. That £800 could cost you over £1,000 if you only pay the minimum.

- **CTA:** Try again

### 4. Pop Quiz 2 — APR: The Number That Matters

#### Screen 4a — Question

- **Visual:** Two credit cards side by side — Card A: 19.9% APR, Card B: 34.9% APR
- **Body:**
  > APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate — it's the yearly cost of borrowing. The higher the APR, the more you pay.
  >
  > Sarah's comparing two cards. She'll carry a £1,000 balance for a year. Roughly how much more would Card B cost her in interest?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) About £15 more
  - B) About £50 more
  - C) About £150 more
  - D) They're basically the same

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (C)

- **Body:**
  > Card A ≈ £199 interest. Card B ≈ £349 interest. That's £150 difference on the same amount. APR is the single most important number when comparing credit — always check it.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Incorrect (A, B, D)

- **Body:**
  > The gap is bigger than you'd think. 19.9% on £1,000 = ~£199. 34.9% on £1,000 = ~£349. That £150 difference is real money — a weekend away, a month's groceries. Always compare APR.

- **CTA:** Try again

### 5. Pop Quiz 3 — Forms of Credit & Their Risks

#### Screen 5a — Question

- **Visual:** Four icons: credit card, personal loan, BNPL checkout, payday loan advert
- **Body:**
  > Credit cards aren't the only way to borrow. There are personal loans, buy now pay later (BNPL), overdrafts, payday loans — each with very different costs and risks.
  >
  > Your car breaks down. Repair cost: £400. You don't have it in savings. Which option makes the most financial sense?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Put it on a credit card at 22% APR and pay the minimum each month
  - B) Use a buy now, pay later service and hope you can cover the instalments
  - C) Put it on a 0% interest credit card (if you have one) and pay it off within the interest-free period
  - D) Take out a payday loan — it's quick and easy

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (C)

- **Body:**
  > A 0% card means you pay exactly what you owe — no extra cost — as long as you clear it before the interest-free period ends. That's credit working FOR you.
  >
  > **Quick guide to borrowing costs:**
  > - **0% credit card:** Free if paid within the interest-free period
  > - **Personal loan:** Fixed rate, predictable — good for bigger planned purchases
  > - **Standard credit card:** Expensive if you carry a balance (typically 20-35% APR)
  > - **Buy now, pay later:** Feels free, but missed payments = fees + damage to your creditworthiness. It IS borrowing, even if it doesn't feel like it.
  > - **Payday loans:** APRs can exceed 1,000%. Absolute last resort.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option A

- **Body:**
  > Better than a payday loan, but you'll pay significant interest over time on the minimum. A 0% card — if you have one — costs nothing as long as you clear it in time.

- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option B

- **Body:**
  > BNPL feels harmless, but it's still borrowing. Miss an instalment and you'll face fees — and BNPL providers increasingly report to credit agencies. A 0% card is a smarter move.

- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D

- **Body:**
  > Payday loans can carry APRs over 1,000%. This should be an absolute last resort — explore every other option first.

- **CTA:** Try again

### 6. Pop Quiz 4 — Creditworthiness: Why It Matters

#### Screen 6a — Question

- **Visual:** Sarah looking at a flat rental listing, Antonio looking at a mortgage offer
- **Body:**
  > Every time you borrow, repay (or miss a repayment), it builds a picture of how reliable you are with money. Lenders, landlords, even some employers use this to decide whether to trust you.
  >
  > This is your **creditworthiness** — your track record of handling borrowed money. Get it right early, and doors open: better mortgage rates, easier flat rentals, cheaper credit. Get it wrong, and things get expensive fast.
  >
  > Which of these would HELP build your creditworthiness? **Select all that apply.**

- **Format:** Multiple select
- **Options:**
  - A) Registering to vote (confirms your identity and address)
  - B) Taking out a phone contract in your name and paying on time
  - C) Applying for 5 credit cards at once to build history faster
  - D) Using a credit builder card for small purchases and paying in full each month
  - E) Avoiding borrowing entirely until you're 30

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (A, B, D)

- **Body:**
  > These three build your profile gradually and responsibly. Registering to vote confirms your identity. A phone contract shows regular, reliable payments. A credit builder card — used carefully — shows lenders you can handle credit.
  >
  > Creditworthiness isn't built overnight. It's a track record — and the earlier you start building it the right way, the more options you'll have when it matters (mortgages, rentals, better rates on borrowing).

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — On C

- **Body:**
  > Multiple applications in a short period actually damages your creditworthiness. Each one leaves a record that lenders can see — and it looks desperate.

#### Feedback — On E

- **Body:**
  > No borrowing history is almost as bad as a bad one. Lenders want evidence you can manage credit responsibly — avoiding it entirely gives them nothing to go on.

- **CTA:** Try again

## LESSON 2: Making Your Money Actually Grow

### Lesson Metadata

- **Lesson Title:** Making Your Money Actually Grow

- **Monster Companions:** Frank the Foresight Monster & Gina the Growth Monster
- **Core Concepts:** Power of starting early (compound growth), where to invest (ISAs), risk-return relationship
- **Tone:** Encouraging but honest — investing isn't magic, but inaction has a cost

---

### 1. Homepage / Entry Point

- **Screen:** Homepage Navigation

- CTA: Tap to begin lesson

### 2. Lesson Intro Sequence

#### Screen 2a — Opening Hook

- **Visual:** Growth chart, money compounding
- **Body:**

  >

  >
  > Same money. Same effort. Wildly different outcomes.
  >
  > **This lesson is about understanding why — and how to get started.**

- **CTA:** "Show me"

#### Screen 2b — Lesson Intro

- **Visual:** Frank and Gina monsters
- **Body:**

- **CTA:** "Let's go"

### 3. Pop Quiz 1 — The Power of Starting Early

#### Screen 3a — Question

- **Visual:** Two timelines compared — Raveneer vs. Antonio
- **Body:**
  > Raveneer and Antonio both invest £100/month into the same fund, earning an average 7% return.
  >
  > - Raveneer starts at 22 and stops at 32 (10 years, £12,000 invested)
  > - Antonio starts at 32 and invests until 62 (30 years, £36,000 invested)
  >
  > Who has more at age 62?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Antonio — he invested three times as much
  - B) Raveneer — compound growth on his early start outweighs Antonio's larger contributions
  - C) They end up with roughly the same
  - D) Impossible to tell without more information

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (B)

- **Body:**
  > Raveneer: around £141,000. Antonio: around £123,000. Raveneer invested just £12,000 over 10 years and let it grow untouched. Antonio put in £36,000 over 30 years — and still ends up with less. That's compound interest at work. Time is the most powerful factor in investing.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Incorrect (A, C, D)

- **Body:**
  > Raveneer: around £141,000. Antonio: around £123,000. Counterintuitive, right? Raveneer's £12,000 had more time to compound than Antonio's £36,000. Starting early — even with less money — typically beats starting later with more. That's the single biggest advantage young investors have.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Clickable Overlay — "What's compound interest?"

- **Trigger:** Tappable link in both correct and incorrect feedback screens
- **Body:**
  > Compound interest is when your money earns interest — and then that interest earns interest too. So instead of growing in a straight line, your pot snowballs.
  >
  > Example: you invest £1,000 and earn 5% a year. After year one you've got £1,050. In year two, you earn 5% on £1,050 — not just the original £1,000. That difference feels tiny at first, but over 30 years that £1,000 grows to over £4,300 without you adding another penny. The longer you leave it, the harder it works.

### 4. Pop Quiz 2 — Where to Put It

#### Screen 4a — Question

- **Visual:** Account type icons
- **Body:**
  > Sarah's researching where to invest. She keeps hearing about ISAs, pensions, and investment platforms. Which account would give her the most tax-efficient way to invest up to £20,000 per year?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) A general investment account
  - B) A Stocks and Shares ISA
  - C) A Lifetime ISA (she's saving for her first home)
  - D) Her current account

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (B)

- **Body:**
  > A Stocks and Shares ISA lets you invest up to £20,000 per year with zero tax on gains or dividends. For most young adults, it's the simplest and most flexible way to start investing. And you don't need thousands — many platforms let you start from £25/month.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option C

- **Body:**
  > Good shout — a Lifetime ISA gives a 25% government bonus, which is brilliant if you're saving for a first home or retirement. But there's a £4,000 annual limit and penalties for early withdrawal for other purposes. It works best alongside a S&S ISA, not instead of one.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option A

- **Body:**
  > No tax advantages — you'll pay tax on gains and dividends. Use a S&S ISA first.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option D

- **Body:**
  > Current accounts aren't investment accounts. Your money earns little to nothing.

- **CTA:** Continue

### 5. Pop Quiz 3 — Understanding Risk

#### Screen 5a — Question

- **Visual:** Raveneer and Antonio side by side, each with £3,000
- **Body:**
  > Raveneer puts £3,000 into a single tech company's shares. Antonio splits £3,000 across a broad index fund covering hundreds of companies.
  >
  > The tech company drops 40%. What's most likely true?

- **Format:** Multiple choice
- **Options:**
  - A) Both lose roughly the same amount
  - B) Raveneer loses more — Antonio's spread across hundreds of companies absorbs the hit better
  - C) Antonio loses more — index funds are riskier than individual shares
  - D) Neither loses anything — shares always recover quickly

- **CTA:** Submit

#### Feedback — Correct (B)

- **Body:**
  > Raveneer's single company dropped 40%, so his £3,000 is now worth £1,800. Antonio's money is spread across hundreds of companies — one bad performer barely dents the whole fund. That's diversification: don't put all your eggs in one basket.

- **CTA:** Continue

#### Feedback — Option A

- **Body:**
  > Not quite. Raveneer had everything in that one company, so he takes the full 40% hit — his £3,000 drops to £1,800. Antonio's money is spread across hundreds of companies, so one bad performer has much less impact. That's the power of diversification.

- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option C

- **Body:**
  > Other way round. Index funds spread your money across hundreds of companies, which actually reduces risk. Raveneer concentrated everything in one company — so when it dropped, he lost big. Antonio's diversified fund cushions that blow.

- **CTA:** Try again

#### Feedback — Option D

- **Body:**
  > Shares can and do lose value — sometimes permanently. Raveneer's company dropped 40%, and there's no guarantee it recovers. That's why spreading your money across lots of investments (diversification) matters — it protects you when one goes wrong.

- **CTA:** Try again

## Narrative & Pedagogical Notes

### Tone
- Direct and practical ("It's future-you's money, with a fee attached")
- Slightly wry, not sarcastic ("Feels like free money. It's not.")
- Respects the audience's intelligence — no over-explanation of basics
- Uses real numbers and concrete examples (£800, 22% APR, £150/month)

### Feedback Pattern
- **Correct:** Brief positive reinforcement + concept explanation with real-world grounding
- **Incorrect:** Explains WHY the answer is wrong using practical consequences, not just theory
- **Specific feedback per wrong answer:** Where a wrong answer reveals a common misconception, it gets its own targeted response (e.g., BNPL, payday loans, crypto)
- No shaming — incorrect feedback always teaches

### Question Format Mix
- Multiple choice (majority — quick, mobile-friendly)
- Multiple select (one per lesson — tests deeper understanding)

### Progression Logic
- **Lesson 1:** Opening hook (first credit card) → Credit card trap (minimum payments) → APR explained → Forms of credit & their risks → Creditworthiness
- **Lesson 2:** Opening hook (compound growth) → Power of starting early → Where to invest (ISAs) → Understanding risk
- Each question builds on the last — scaffolded learning, not random quiz

### Key Differences from Younger Segments
- No character "meets" the concept of money for the first time — they're already living it
- Scenarios involve adult decisions: credit cards, mortgages, investments, car repairs
- Financial amounts are realistic for young adults (£800 credit card spend, £400 emergency) — but investing examples acknowledge a range (£25-£150/month) to avoid alienating lower earners
- Questions test application and judgement, not just recall
- Feedback references real-world products (Klarna, ISAs, index funds) by name
- The "right answer" is often about trade-offs, not absolutes
- Content doesn't assume a graduate path — no references to student debt as a universal experience, no assumption of graduate salary levels
- Never lectures about saving — acknowledges that many young adults know they should save but have limited capacity to do so right now

### Flow Structure (per lesson)
1. **Homepage entry** (1 screen)
2. **Intro sequence** (2 screens — hook + lesson framing)
3. **Questions with inline character scenarios** (Lesson 1: 4 questions; Lesson 2: 3 questions — each with per-answer feedback)
4. **Wrap-up** (summary + monster unlock)
- **Total: ~8-10 screens per lesson** — significantly tighter than before, characters introduced through scenarios not standalone intros
