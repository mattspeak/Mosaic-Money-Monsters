Putting money into assets expected to increase in value

Playing an active role in growing your wealth

Thinking longer-term than saving

**Lesson 8.3: Understanding Bonds**

Setting the scene

*Visual: Antonio with notebook. Professor Money with calculator.*

**Text - Professor Money**: Bonds are like IOUs from governments and
companies that pay you interest for lending them your money!

**Text - Antonio**: I\'ve heard bonds are less risky than shares. How do
they actually work?

**Text - Gina**: Bonds play an important role in many portfolios. They
offer different characteristics than shares. Let me explain.

What are bonds

Text: What Are Bonds?

Loans to governments or companies

You lend money and receive interest payments

Your original amount returned at the end (maturity)

Can be traded like shares

**Text - Antonio**: So with shares I\'m an owner, but with bonds I\'m a
lender?

**Text - Gina**: Exactly! That\'s the key difference.

Text: Key Bond Features:

Face value (the amount you lend)

Coupon (the interest rate paid)

Maturity date (when you get your money back)

Credit rating (how reliable the borrower is)

Text: Types of Bonds:

Government bonds (generally lowest risk)

Corporate bonds (company borrowing)

High-yield bonds (higher risk, higher interest)

**Text - Information box**: Bond prices and interest rates have an
inverse relationship - when interest rates rise, existing bond prices
typically fall, because newer bonds offer better rates.

reduce risk

equities

**Bond**: A loan to a government or company that pays interest

**Coupon**: The interest rate paid on a bond

**Face Value**: The original amount lent when buying a bond

**Maturity**: When a bond\'s term ends and the original amount is
returned

**Investment Fund**: Pooled investments from many people, professionally
managed

**Index Fund**: A fund that tracks a market index like the FTSE 100

**ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)**: A fund traded on stock exchanges like
individual shares

**Active Fund**: A fund where a manager selects investments trying to
beat the market

**Passive Fund**: A fund that simply tracks a market index, with lower
fees

**Asset Allocation**: How investments are divided among different types
(shares, bonds, cash)

**Rebalancing**: Adjusting your portfolio back to your target allocation

**Pound-Cost Averaging**: Investing fixed amounts regularly regardless
of market conditions

**Stocks and Shares ISA**: A tax-efficient account for investments

**Lifetime ISA**: An account with 25% government bonus for first home or
retirement
