### Question 1 — The Credit Card Trap

**Screen 5**

> Sarah puts £800 on her credit card over a month — meals out, a new jacket, some online shopping. Her minimum payment is £20.
>
> If she only pays the minimum each month, what happens?

- A) The £800 slowly disappears on its own
- B) She pays interest on the remaining balance — potentially paying hundreds more over time
- C) The bank writes off the rest after 12 months
- D) Her credit score improves because she's making regular payments

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: B**

"That's it. Pay the minimum on £800 at a typical 22% APR and you could end up paying over £200 in interest — and it takes years to clear. The minimum keeps you in good standing, but it's designed to keep you in debt longer."

On incorrect: "The minimum payment covers barely more than the interest. The remaining balance keeps accruing charges. That £800 could cost you over £1,000 if you only pay the minimum."

### Question 2 — APR Decoded

**Screen 6**

> You're comparing two credit cards. Card A: 19.9% APR. Card B: 34.9% APR.
>
> You plan to carry a £1,000 balance for a year. Roughly how much more would Card B cost you in interest?

- A) About £15 more
- B) About £50 more
- C) About £150 more
- D) They're basically the same

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: C**

"Card A ≈ £199 interest. Card B ≈ £349 interest. That's £150 difference on the same amount. APR is the single most important number when comparing credit — always check it."

On incorrect: "The gap is bigger than you'd think. 19.9% on £1,000 = ~£199. 34.9% on £1,000 = ~£349. That £150 difference is real money — a weekend away, a month's groceries."

### Question 4 — What Would You Do?

**Screen 8**

> Your car breaks down. Repair cost: £400. You don't have it in savings. Which option makes the most financial sense?

- A) Put it on a credit card at 22% APR and pay the minimum each month
- B) Use a buy now, pay later service and hope you can cover the instalments
- C) Put it on a 0% interest credit card (if you have one) and pay it off within the interest-free period
- D) Take out a payday loan — it's quick and easy

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: C**

"A 0% card means you pay exactly what you owe — no extra cost — as long as you clear it before the interest-free period ends. No interest, no fees. That's credit working FOR you."

On A: "Better than a payday loan, but you'll pay significant interest over time if you're only hitting the minimum."
On B: "Could work if you're sure you can cover every instalment. But miss one and you'll face fees and a credit score hit."
On D: "Payday loans can carry APRs over 1,000%. This should be an absolute last resort — explore every other option first."

### Question 5 — Building Your Credit Score

**Screen 9**

> You've just turned 18 and have zero credit history. Which of these would HELP you start building a good score? **Select all that apply.**

- A) Getting on the electoral register
- B) Taking out a phone contract in your name and paying on time
- C) Applying for 5 credit cards at once to build history faster
- D) Using a credit builder card for small purchases and paying in full each month
- E) Avoiding credit entirely until you're 30

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: A, B, D**

"These three build your profile gradually and responsibly. The electoral register confirms your identity. A phone contract shows regular payments. A credit builder card — used carefully — shows lenders you can handle credit."

On C: "Multiple applications in a short period tanks your score. Each one leaves a 'hard search' on your file."
On E: "No credit history is almost as bad as bad credit history. Lenders want to see you can manage borrowing — avoiding it entirely leaves them with nothing to go on."

### Question 2 — The Power of Starting Early

**Screen 6**

> Two people both invest £100/month into the same fund, earning an average 7% annual return.
>
> - Person A starts at 22 and stops at 32 (10 years, £12,000 invested)
> - Person B starts at 32 and invests until 62 (30 years, £36,000 invested)
>
> Who has more at age 62?

- A) Person B — they invested three times as much
- B) Person A — compound growth on their early start outweighs Person B's larger contributions
- C) They end up with roughly the same
- D) Impossible to tell without more information

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: B**

"This surprises everyone. Person A invested £12,000 over 10 years, then let it grow untouched for 30 more years. Person B invested £36,000 over 30 years. Person A still comes out ahead — that's compound growth. Your money earns returns, then those returns earn returns. Time is the most powerful factor in investing."

On incorrect: "Counterintuitive, right? Person A's smaller amount had more time to compound. Starting early — even with less money — typically beats starting later with more. That's the single biggest advantage young investors have."

On A: "No tax advantages — you'll pay tax on gains and dividends. Use a S&S ISA first."

### Question 4 — Understanding Risk

**Screen 8**

> Antonio says: *"I want high returns but zero risk."*
>
> What's the reality?

- A) That's totally achievable if you pick the right investments
- B) Higher potential returns always come with higher risk — there's no way around it
- C) Risk only matters if you're investing over £10,000
- D) Government bonds guarantee high returns with no risk

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: B**

"Risk and return are permanently linked. The trick isn't avoiding risk — it's managing it. Diversification (spreading your money across different investments), a long time horizon, and regular investing all help smooth out the bumps."

On incorrect: "If someone promises high returns with zero risk, that's a red flag. Every investment carries some risk. The key is matching the level of risk to your timeline and comfort level — and diversifying so no single bad investment can wipe you out."

### Question 5 — Building a Portfolio

**Screen 9**

> Sarah decides to invest £150/month. She's 24, won't need this money for 10+ years, and is comfortable with moderate risk. Which approach makes the most sense?

- A) Put everything into one company's shares — she's heard they're doing well
- B) Invest in a broad index fund that tracks hundreds of companies, and keep contributing monthly
- C) Keep it all in cash and wait for the "perfect" time to invest
- D) Put everything into cryptocurrency — it's the future

[Button: Submit]

#### Response

**Correct: B**

"A broad index fund gives you instant diversification across hundreds of companies. Monthly contributions mean she's pound-cost averaging — buying more when prices dip, less when they're high. Over 10+ years, this simple, consistent approach has historically outperformed most alternatives."

On A: "One company = one point of failure. If it tanks, so does everything. Diversification is the most basic risk management principle."
On C: "Time in the market beats timing the market. Waiting for the 'perfect moment' usually means missing growth. Regular investing removes the guesswork."
On D: "Crypto is highly volatile and speculative. It might have a place as a very small part of a portfolio, but betting everything on it isn't a strategy — it's a gamble."
