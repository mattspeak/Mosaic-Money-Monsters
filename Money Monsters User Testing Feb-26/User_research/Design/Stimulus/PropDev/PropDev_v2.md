# Prioritised Directions

---

# Slide 1 – Direction 1

# Train Your Money Brain
You're already making money decisions. Make better ones, starting now.

## What you'll get better at
- Spot good vs bad money choices
- Think ahead before you spend
- Learn from mistakes safely

---

# Slide 2 – Direction 1

**Train Your Money Brain**

## Territory to explore
Confidence and control — replacing uncertainty with knowing what you're doing.
- Tests whether "you're already making money decisions" lands as empowering (you're not starting from zero) or obvious
- Tests whether "train your brain" feels active and appealing — or like homework
- Probes whether "learn from mistakes safely" is a genuine draw — do they want a safe space to practise, or does that feel artificial?

## Probes to explore if chosen by participant
- What kind of money decisions are you already making — even small ones?
- "Train your money brain" — does that make you want to try it, or does it feel a bit cringe?
- If you could practise money stuff without any real consequences, would that actually be useful — or would it feel pointless because it's not real?

---

# Slide 1 – Direction 2

# Money Stuff Nobody Explains
The stuff about money you're supposed to just know, but nobody actually tells you.

## What you'll learn
- How to make your money go further
- What's actually worth saving for (and how)
- The stuff that catches people out, before it catches you

---

# Slide 2 – Direction 2

**Money Stuff Nobody Explains**

## Territory to explore
The hidden knowledge gap — things about money you're expected to know but were never taught.
- Tests whether "you're supposed to just know" creates recognition and relief — or whether it feels dramatic / exaggerated
- Tests whether the bullets feel like genuine insider knowledge ("the stuff that catches people out") or just generic money advice repackaged
- Probes whether this framing makes them lean in with curiosity or pull back because admitting you don't know feels uncomfortable

## Probes to explore if chosen by participant
- Is there something about money you've had to figure out on your own that you wish someone had just told you?
- "Money stuff nobody explains" — does that feel true to you, or do you think there's loads of info out there already?
- If you saw this, would you want to check it out by yourself — or would you only do it if a friend was doing it too?

# De-prioritised Directions

---

# Slide 1 – Direction 3

# Real Money Skills for Life
Simple money skills you can use straight away, for stuff you actually do.

## What you'll get better at
- Making your money last longer
- Saving for what you actually want
- Spotting the scams before they catch you

---

# Slide 2 – Direction 3

**Real Money Skills for Life**

## Territory to explore
Practical, outcome-led — money skills positioned as immediately useful, not aspirational.
- Tests whether "skills you can use straight away" feels real or like a promise that doesn't land
- Tests whether the specific bullets (lasting longer, saving, scams) match what they actually worry about — or miss the mark
- Probes whether "for stuff you actually do" creates relevance or feels too vague to mean anything

## Probes to explore if chosen by participant
- Which of those three things matters most to you right now — and is there something about money it's missing?
- When you hear "stuff you actually do" — what comes to mind? What money stuff are you actually dealing with?
- Does this sound like something you'd try, or does it sound a bit like a school lesson dressed up?

# Slide 1 – Direction 4

# Ready for Real Life
Learn the money stuff school doesn't really explain.

## What you'll get better at
- Understand rent, bills, bank accounts and credit
- Know what actually happens when you get paid
- Build habits that protect you from costly mistakes

---

# Slide 2 – Direction 4

**Ready for Real Life**

## Territory to explore
Life-stage readiness — money skills framed around adult milestones and the gap between school and real life.
- Tests whether "school doesn't explain" creates a sense of unfairness / recognition or feels like blaming
- Tests whether the specific milestones (rent, bills, payslips, credit) feel imminent and relevant — or distant and abstract depending on age band
- Probes whether "ready for real life" motivates or feels slightly patronising ("I'm already living real life")

## Probes to explore if chosen by participant
- When you read "rent, bills, bank accounts and credit" — does that feel like your life now, or like stuff that's coming later?
- What's the thing on that list you'd most want someone to actually explain properly?
- Does "ready for real life" make you feel like it's helping you get ahead — or like it's saying you're behind?

# Slide 1 – Direction 5

# Tame Your Money Monsters
Power up your money skills in minutes.
Tame the monsters that mess with your spending, saving and choices.

## What you'll get better at
- Spending, saving and choosing smarter
- Spotting the traps that catch you out
- Knowing what to do when money stuff gets real

---

# Slide 2 – Direction 5

**Tame Your Money Monsters**

## Territory to explore
The branded game proposition — learning framed as play, not education.
- Tests whether the "monster" concept creates instant intrigue or feels try-hard / too young
- Tests whether "power up your money skills in minutes" sets the right expectation — quick, low-commitment, game-like — or sounds like an ad
- Probes whether "tame the monsters that mess with your spending" gives a clear enough picture of what you'd actually do

## Probes to explore if chosen by participant
- What do you picture when you hear "Money Monsters" — what kind of thing do you imagine it is?
- "Power up in minutes" — does that sound like something you'd actually open, or more like something you'd scroll past?
- If a mate sent this to you, what would you think? Would you try it or take the piss?
