# Proposition Directions for User Testing

## How to Use This Document

Each direction is written as **landing page copy** — the minimum a young person would see on a single screen before deciding whether to engage. Designed to be built as simple mobile-first test pages.

Each direction is deliberately different in **tone, framing, and emotional territory**. The goal is to identify which *territory* resonates — not to find finished copy.

All directions connect to the **Money Monsters** brand and mechanic (monsters = money topics you tame through learning) but each frames it differently.

---

## Direction 1: "Tame your Money Monsters"

### Overview

**Headline:**
Level Up Your Money; one Monster at a Time.

**Territory:** Play and progress: Money Monsters gives you... fun whilst getting better with money.

**How Each Segment Reads It**

- **13–15 y/o:** "Collect" and "level up" are native gaming language — Pokémon, Roblox. The value sneaks in through play.
- **16–18 y/o:** "Level up" feels like progression without being patronising. Duolingo comparison sets clear format expectations.
- **18–25 y/o:** "Something you'll actually use" is his filter for everything. Duolingo signals quick, structured, low-commitment — fits into breaks.

### What You Get

**Play your way to being good with money**
It's a game. You collect Monsters, level up, and get smarter with money while you're at it.

**Why it works**

- It's actually fun — not homework dressed up as an app
- Every Monster you beat teaches you something useful
- You keep coming back because you want to, not because you have to

### Landing Page Copy

**Headline:**
Level Up Your Money; one Monster at a Time.

**Value statement:**
Every Monster teaches you something about money you'll actually use — from stretching your money to spotting scams. 
**How it works:**

1. **Choose Your Challenge**
Pick a Monster (like Spending, Saving, or Earning), start taming and win skills for life.

2. **Beat the Clock**
Duolingo-style lessons. Real life situations, zero essays, win in minutes.

3. **Level up your Money skills**
The more Monsters you collect, the more you know about money!

**Trust line:**
Built with financial educators. Free to use. No ads, no data selling.

## Design Rationale

### Why These 3 Directions

Each occupies a **distinct emotional territory** while connecting to the Money Monsters brand:

| Direction | Emotional Territory | Monster Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Collect Monsters. Level Up.** | Play and progress — fun first, learning follows | Monsters = collectible characters that teach you things |
| **Money Master in Minutes. No Lessons.** | Anti-lesson learning — real scenarios, not classrooms | Monsters = proof you've mastered it |
| **Your Money Cheat Code** | Insider edge + social confidence — shortcut to becoming the one who gets it | Monsters = skills you unlock that most people learn the hard way |

### What's Different From the Original Directions

The original directions (Life Skills, Money Confidence, Immediate Relevance) were useful starting hypotheses but had limitations:

1. **Too age-staged** — "From pocket money to paychecks" explicitly segments by age, risking alienation for anyone who doesn't see themselves in the named stage
2. **Too parent-facing** — "Real skills for real money" reads as parent-approved, which is a signal for teens to disengage (Lily: "My parents would love it — usually means it's not for me")
3. **Too abstract** — "Know your money. Own your decisions" is aspirational but doesn't tell you what you'll *get*
4. **No connection to the product** — none referenced Monsters, the gamified mechanic, or the brand identity

These 3 directions are designed to:

- **Lead with the user's feeling, not the product category** — none say "financial literacy" or "money skills"
- **Make Monsters do real work** — in every direction, the Monster mechanic adds meaning (not just decoration)
- **Work across ages without being age-staged** — each lands differently by segment, documented in the bullet points
- **Be testable as real landing pages** — headline, value line, mechanism, trust = a full screen

### Competitive Positioning

Drawing from the landscape analysis, these directions learn from what works elsewhere:

- **Gimi's "Financial Superskills"** reframe → our "level up" / "cheat code" reframe — same strategy of making finance feel like a power-up
- **Money Wise Game's "Life skills not financial skills"** → our avoidance of category language across all directions
- **Bloom's "300+ bite-sized lessons"** → our "quick levels" format promise across all directions
- **KidVestors' "Earn while you learn"** → our collectibility/progress mechanic (Directions 1 and 3)
- **Finfluencer authenticity** → our conversational, peer-level tone (especially Direction 2)
- **Duolingo's low-barrier onboarding** → Direction 1's explicit "like Duolingo" comparison; Direction 3's "shortcuts" framing

## Recommendation for Testing

Present all 3 as simple single-screen pages (mobile-first). For each, ask participants:

1. **Which one would make you tap to find out more?** (forced rank)
2. **What do you think this app does?** (comprehension check — do they get the Monster mechanic?)
3. **Who is this for?** (audience signal check — do they see themselves?)
4. **What puts you off, if anything?** (barrier identification)
5. **What would you expect to see next?** (expectation setting for onboarding)
