# Onboarding Flow: Research Stimulus

# Money Monsters: Onboarding Questions 

**Purpose:** Max 7 user-centred questions to personalise the learning pathway. Same underlying topics as the current intro test (money basics, budgeting, saving vs spending, interest, digital money, credit/borrowing, financial planning) — but reframed around the user's real life, not textbook knowledge.

**Design principles:**
- Questions are about *their* situation, choices, and confidence — not definitions
- Each question maps to a learning topic so placement still works
- Language adapts by age segment (13-15 / 16-18 / 18-25)
- Fast, low-stakes, no "wrong answers" — everything is a useful signal
- **The question itself should feel interesting, not like a test**

**Format:** After the age-gate (Q1 from Option 1), present the segment-appropriate version below.

---

## How it works

Each question covers a core topic from the current intro test. The user's answers signal their starting level — but the questions feel like a conversation, not an exam.

| # | Underlying topic | What we're really assessing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Money basics / purpose of money | Do they think about money beyond spending? |
| 2 | Budgeting / needs vs wants | Can they prioritise when resources are limited? |
| 3 | Saving | Do they have a saving habit or mindset? |
| 4 | Interest / compound growth | Do they understand money can grow (or cost more) over time? |
| 5 | Digital money / online banking | How comfortable are they with digital financial tools? |
| 6 | Credit & borrowing | Do they understand the difference between spending their money and borrowing? |
| 7 | Financial planning / goals | Do they think ahead about money? |

## Segment A: Young Teens (13-15)

*Tone: Friendly, concrete, uses scenarios they recognise. No jargon.*

**Q1 — Money basics**
You get £20 for your birthday. What's the first thing that goes through your head?
- a) What am I going to buy?
- b) I'll save some and spend some
- c) I wonder how I could make it grow to a bigger amount?
- d) I don't really think about it

**Q2 — Budgeting / needs vs wants**
You've got £15 left for the week. Your phone needs credit (£5) and your friends are going to the cinema (£10). What do you do?
- a) Cinema — I'll sort my phone later
- b) Phone credit first, then cinema if there's enough
- c) Skip both and save it
- d) I'm not sure — it's hard to choose

**Q3 — Saving**
Have you ever saved up for something you really wanted?
- a) Yes — and I got it!
- b) I've tried but I end up spending the money
- c) Not really, I spend what I get
- d) I'm saving for something right now

**Q4 — Interest / growth**
If you put £100 in a savings account and left it there for a year, what do you think would happen?
- a) You'd still have exactly £100
- b) You'd have a bit more because the bank adds a small amount
- c) You'd have less because the bank charges you
- d) I'm not sure

**Q5 — Digital money**
How do you usually pay for things?
- a) Cash
- b) A card (mine or a parent's)
- c) Phone payment (Apple Pay, Google Pay etc.)
- d) I don't really buy things on my own yet

**Q6 — Credit & borrowing**
Which of these have you come across? (Pick all that apply)
- a) Borrowing money from a friend or family member
- b) A phone contract or subscription where you pay monthly
- c) Buy Now, Pay Later (like Klarna or Clearpay)
- d) I haven't really come across any of these yet

**Q7 — Financial planning**
When you think about money and your future, which is closest to how you feel?
- a) I don't really think about it yet
- b) I think about it sometimes but it feels far away
- c) I've got some things I want to save for
- d) I already have a plan for what I want to do with money

## Segment B: Older Teens (16-18)

*Tone: More direct, treats them as near-adults. Acknowledges real-world money pressures — part-time jobs, subscriptions, thinking about what's next. Scenarios they're actually navigating.*

**Q1 — Money basics**
You've just been paid £120 from your part-time job. Your phone contract is £20, you owe a friend £15, and there's a festival ticket for £80. What do you do?
- a) Get the festival ticket — I'll sort the rest later 
- b) Pay what I owe first, then see if I can still afford the ticket
- c) Cover the essentials, skip the festival, save the rest
- d) I'd try to find a cheaper ticket or sell something to make it all work

**Q2 — Saving**
Have you ever saved for something that cost more than £100?
- a) Yes — I set a target and hit it
- b) I've tried but kept dipping into it
- c) Not yet, but I want to
- d) I don't really earn enough to save that much

**Q3 — Interest / growth**
A mate tells you they've put £200 into crypto because "it's the best way to grow your money." What's your reaction?
- a) They're probably right — crypto is where the money is
- b) It could go up, but it could also drop — it's risky
- c) I'd rather put it somewhere safer like a savings account
- d) I don't really know enough about crypto to say

**Q4 — Which describes you?**
Pick the one that sounds most like how you handle money:
- a) **The spender:** Money comes in, money goes out — I don't track it much
- b) **The planner:** I know what I've got and roughly where it goes
- c) **The saver:** I always put something aside, even if it's small
- d) **The hustler:** I'm always looking for ways to earn or grow money

**Q5 — Borrowing & credit**
Which of these types of credit or borrowing have you used or dealt with? (Pick all that apply)
- a) Borrowed money from friends or family
- b) Buy Now, Pay Later (Klarna, Clearpay, etc.)
- c) A phone contract, car finance, or other monthly payment plan
- d) An overdraft or credit card
- e) None of these — I haven't borrowed or used credit yet

**Q6 — Digital confidence**
Which of these have you done on your own? (Pick the most advanced one)
- a) Paid for something with my card or phone
- b) Checked my balance and recent transactions on a banking app
- c) Transferred money to someone or split a bill
- d) Set up a subscription, direct debit, or standing order

**Q7 — Looking ahead**
When you think about money over the next couple of years, which sounds most like you?
- a) I try not to think about it — it stresses me out
- b) I've got things I want (car, uni, travel) but no plan yet
- c) I'm already saving or working toward a specific goal
- d) I've started looking into stuff like credit scores, investing, or ISAs

## Segment C: Young Adults (18-25)

*Tone: Peer-to-peer, real stakes, acknowledges that they're already dealing with money whether they feel ready or not. No hand-holding — speaks to them as adults navigating real financial decisions.*

**Q1 — Money basics**
When your pay comes in each month, what happens?
- a) It goes out as fast as it comes in — I don't really track it
- b) I cover the essentials (rent, bills, food) and see what's left
- c) I've got a rough system — I know what goes where most months
- d) I budget properly — fixed costs, spending money, savings all accounted for

**Q2 — Saving & investing**
Which of these best describes where you're at with saving? (Pick all that apply)
- a) I don't really have any savings right now
- b) I've got a bit put aside for short-term things (holidays, purchases, emergencies)
- c) I'm saving for something longer-term (house deposit, big goal, building a safety net)
- d) I've got some money in investments (stocks, ISA, crypto, pension contributions)

**Q3 — Which describes you?**
Pick the one that sounds most like how you handle money:
- a) **The reactor:** Money comes in, money goes out — I deal with things as they come up
- b) **The planner:** I know what I've got and roughly where it goes each month
- c) **The saver:** I always put something aside, even when things are tight
- d) **The builder:** I'm actively trying to grow my money — investing, side income, the lot

**Q4 — Borrowing & credit**
Which of these types of credit or borrowing have you used or dealt with? (Pick all that apply)
- a) Borrowed money from friends or family
- b) Buy Now, Pay Later (Klarna, Clearpay, etc.)
- c) An overdraft
- d) A credit card
- e) A loan, car finance, or other structured borrowing
- f) None of these — I haven't used credit yet

**Q5 — Digital confidence**
Which of these have you done on your own? (Pick the most advanced one)
- a) Used my banking app to check balances and transfer money
- b) Set up and managed direct debits or standing orders
- c) Compared savings accounts, switched providers, or opened an ISA
- d) Looked into investing, pensions, or credit scores and taken action on it

**Q6 — Looking ahead**
When you think about money over the next few years, which sounds most like you?
- a) I'm focused on getting through right now — future planning feels like a luxury
- b) I've got goals (house deposit, travel, paying off debt) but no real plan yet
- c) I'm already saving or working toward something specific
- d) I've started looking into investing, pensions, or building long-term financial security

## Scoring logic (suggested)

Each answer maps to a sophistication signal:

| Answer pattern | Likely placement |
|---|---|
| Mostly a) answers | Level 1 — Money Trailblazer (start from foundations) |
| Mostly b) answers | Level 1-2 — Some awareness, needs structure |
| Mostly c) answers | Level 2 — Money Scout (solid basics, ready for deeper content) |
| Mostly d) answers | Level 3+ — Already capable, stretch with advanced topics |

**Key difference from the current intro test:** This approach assesses *behaviour and confidence* rather than *factual recall*. A user who can't define "compound interest" but already saves regularly is in a very different place to one who can define it but has never opened a savings account. Option 2 captures this.

## Rationale: How Option 2 maps to the client's current intro test

The current intro test has 15 knowledge-based questions. Option 2 consolidates these into 7 questions that cover the same learning domains — but shift from *"can you recall this fact?"* to *"how do you actually handle this in your life?"*

### Question-by-question mapping

| Option 2 | Client's current questions covered | What changed and why |
|---|---|---|
| **Q1 — Money basics** | Q1 (purpose of money), Q3 (match saving/spending/earning/borrowing) | The current test asks users to define money and match textbook terms. Option 2 asks what they *do* when they get money — which reveals the same understanding (do they think beyond spending?) without feeling like a classroom quiz. A 14-year-old who says "I'll save some and spend some" is demonstrating the same knowledge as one who correctly matches "saving = keeping money for future use" — but in their own language. |
| **Q2 — Budgeting / needs vs wants** | Q2 (budgeting is only for adults — T/F), Q4 (Tom saving for a console — first step?) | The current questions test whether users know budgeting matters and can identify the "right" first step in saving. Option 2 puts them *in* a budget crunch — limited money, competing priorities — and watches how they think. This tests the same skill (prioritisation, needs vs wants) but through a decision they'd actually face. The scenario scales by age: cinema vs phone credit (13-15), gig vs bills (16-18), emergency repair vs credit card (18-25). |
| **Q3 — Saving** | Q4 (saving process), Q11 (best way to save for a house deposit) | The current test asks which saving approach is "most effective" — a knowledge question with a textbook answer. Option 2 asks about their *actual* saving behaviour: do they save, have they succeeded, are they saving now? This is a stronger placement signal. Someone who says "I save regularly and know how much I have" doesn't need Level 1's "what is saving?" lessons — regardless of whether they can name the best account type. |
| **Q4 — Interest / growth** | Q7 (highest return investment), Q10 (match interest/inflation/debt/investment), Q13 (mortgage down payment and interest), Q15 (credit card interest calculation) | Four current questions test interest and growth as factual recall (definitions, calculations, which investment has highest return). Option 2 distils this to one intuition check: does money grow, shrink, or stay the same in a savings account? This single question efficiently separates users who grasp the core concept from those who don't. The 18-25 version adds a light calculation (4% of £1,000) to differentiate further up the scale. |
| **Q5 — Digital money** | Q5 (credit vs debit cards — T/F), Q8 (ATMs only for withdrawals — T/F), Q6 (match currencies to countries) | The current test asks factual trivia: do ATMs only withdraw cash, do credit and debit cards work the same way, which symbol is the Yen. Option 2 asks what financial tools they *actually use and are confident with* — cash, card, phone payment, banking apps, direct debits. This tells us far more about their readiness for digital finance content than knowing that ATMs can also check balances. Currency matching (Q6) is dropped entirely — it tests geography, not financial capability. |
| **Q6 — Credit & borrowing** | Q5 (credit vs debit cards), Q12 (improving credit score), Q15 (credit card interest calculation) | Three current questions test credit knowledge through definitions and calculations. Option 2 reframes by age: for 13-15s, lending £10 to a friend (do they intuitively understand lending risk?); for 16-18s, Buy Now Pay Later (do they recognise it as borrowing?); for 18-25s, credit scores (do they know what builds one?). Each version tests the same underlying concept — understanding that borrowing has consequences — through the mechanism most relevant to that age group. |
| **Q7 — Financial planning** | Q4 (saving process — first step), Q14 (Emma investing for retirement — strategy) | The current test asks about investment strategy for a 40-year horizon — a question most users under 25 will guess at. Option 2 asks where they are with forward planning *in their own terms*. The time horizon scales naturally — "money and your future" (13-15), "next 1-2 years" (16-18), "next 5 years" (18-25). This reveals planning maturity without requiring knowledge of diversified portfolios. |

### What's consolidated or dropped

| Current test content | Option 2 treatment |
|---|---|
| Q6 — Currency matching (£, ¥, CHF, €) | **Dropped.** Tests geography, not financial literacy. Doesn't predict learning needs. |
| Q8 — ATMs only for withdrawals (T/F) | **Absorbed into Q5** (digital money comfort). Whether someone uses ATMs matters less than whether they're comfortable with digital tools generally. |
| Q9 — Exchange rate calculation (£200 at 1:170) | **Dropped.** A maths test, not a financial behaviour signal. Can be taught in-content if relevant. |
| Q10 — Match interest/inflation/debt/investment | **Absorbed into Q4** (interest intuition). Vocabulary matching is tested implicitly — if they understand money grows in a savings account, they grasp the concept of interest. Inflation, debt, and investment terms are better introduced in lessons than tested blind in onboarding. |
| Q13 — Mortgage down payment (T/F) | **Absorbed into Q7** (financial planning). The mortgage-specific knowledge is niche for this audience; forward-planning behaviour is the real signal. |

### The core argument

The current intro test treats onboarding as a **knowledge exam** — 15 questions that sort users by what they can recall or calculate. This creates three problems for the target audience:

1. **It's intimidating.** Despite the friendly framing ("don't worry if some questions seem challenging"), exchange rate calculations and investment portfolio questions will make many young users feel out of their depth *before they've started*. This is a dropout risk at exactly the moment you need momentum.

2. **It measures the wrong thing.** Factual recall doesn't predict learning needs. A 16-year-old who can't define "compound interest" but already saves 20% of their part-time wages doesn't need Level 1. A 22-year-old who can calculate credit card interest but has never opened a savings account does.

3. **It's too long.** 15 questions is a significant commitment before the user has received any value from the app. Duolingo's placement test works because users already understand the value proposition (learn a language). Money Monsters hasn't earned that patience yet — especially with sceptical teens.

Option 2 solves all three: **7 questions, no wrong answers, measures behaviour and confidence, feels like a conversation.** It covers the same 7 learning domains and produces the same 4-level placement — but the user experience is "tell us about you" rather than "let's see what you know."

## After completion — personalised response (same warmth as Option 1)

The placement message should feel like recognition, not judgement:

- **Level 1:** "Looks like you're just getting started with money — and that's exactly why this exists. We'll build your confidence step by step."
- **Level 2:** "You've already got some good instincts. Let's sharpen them and fill in the gaps."
- **Level 3+:** "You're ahead of most people your age. Let's take your money skills to the next level."
