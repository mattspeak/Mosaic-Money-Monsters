# **Young Teens Session Guide (Ages 13-15)**

A specialised session design for the youngest segment, prioritising engagement, clarity, and age-appropriate techniques.

---
## **Moderator Intro Script**

*Say this at the start of the session, before anything else.*

"Hi — thanks for joining. My name is [name]. What we're going to do today is pretty simple: we're going to have a chat about what you think about money, and then I'll show you something that some people have been working on and get your thoughts on it.

Just so you know — I don't work for the company that made the thing i'll share with you. My job is just to speak to people like you and note down what you think. There are no right or wrong answers. Honestly, the more you tell me exactly what you think — even if it's 'this is rubbish' — the more useful it is.

Sound good? Do you want start with how old you are and your favourite hobbies?"

---

### **Moderator Reminders**

1. Never like homework — keep it playful, not academic  
2. Frequent variety — switch activities every 5-7 minutes maximum  
3. Visual and tactile — drawing, pointing, ranking \> long verbal answers  
4. Peer validation — what would friends think matters enormously  
5. Respect their intelligence — provide structure without coming across as condescending

---

## **Session Flow (60 min)**

| Section                        | Time   | What We're Finding Out                                                                                                                                               |
| ------------------------------ | ------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Intro & framing                | 2 min  | Who the moderator is, what we're doing, setting expectations.                                                                                                        |
| Warm-up                        | 5 min  | Building rapport through a money-related creative exercise. Gets them comfortable and thinking about money before we go deeper.                                      |
| Relationship with money        | 8 min  | Understanding their baseline: how money fits into their life, where they're confident vs. uncertain, and current sources of guidance (parents, Google, TikTok, etc.) |
| What matters to you            | 5 min  | Jobs to be done card sort — what money needs matter most to them, ranked in their own words.                                                                         |
| Proposition reactions          | 8 min  | Do the propositions land instantly? Do they see themselves as the target? Which framing resonates and why?                                                           |
| Money Monsters app walkthrough | 20 min | Observing real behaviour — where they get stuck, what they understand, learning format, navigation, gamification.                                                    |
| Reflection & close             | 9 min  | Honest verdict, standalone vs. embedded app question, monetisation, and wrap-up.                                                                                     |
| Buffer                         | 3 min  | For overruns or technical issues                                                                                                                                     |

## **1. WARM-UP: DESIGN YOUR DREAM MONEY APP (5 min)**
–

### **Aim**

* Break "interview" formality / reduce nerves  
* Get them thinking about money in a creative, low-pressure way  
* Build rapport and trust
### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"Before we get into anything, I want you to design something for me. Grab a pen and paper."

Dream Money App (2 min)

"Imagine you could design a money app that's made only for you and your life right now. Not for adults, not for your parents — just for you. Sketch what it would look like. Stick figures totally fine. You've got 2 minutes."

While they draw, prompt gently:

* "What's the main thing it does?"  
* "What would you tap on first?"  
* "Is it fun, serious, or somewhere in between?"  

Show and Tell (2 min)

"Hold it up and talk me through it."

Probes:

* "What makes this different from stuff that already exists?"
* "What would make you open it every day?"

## **2. RELATIONSHIP WITH MONEY (8 min)**

### **Aim**

Understand their baseline:

* How money fits into their life  
* Current engagement level with money  
* Confidence vs. knowledge gap (do they know what they don't know?)  
* Emotional baseline  
* Sources of guidance (parents, Google, TikTok, nowhere)  
* Appetite for learning more

### **Part A: Money in Their Life (4 min)**

"I want to understand a bit about you and money. Just tell me what comes to mind."

* **"Do you have your own money?"**  
  * If yes: "Where does it come from?" / "How often?"  
  * If no: "How do you get money when you need it?"  
* **"What was the last major thing you spent money on?"**  
  * Probe: "Was that a planned thing or just... it happened?"  
* **"Have you ever saved up for something?"**  
  * If yes: "What was it? How long did it take?"  
  * If no: "Is there anything you'd want to save for?"  
* Where do you keep your money? 
  * "Cash, card, or app — what do you use most?"**  
* **"How do your parents talk to you about Money?"**

### **Part B: Confidence and Learning (3 min)**

"A couple more questions..."

* Compare yourself to your class mates, and then score yourself "1-10, how good would you say you are with money? 10 being the best, 1 being rubbish.
  * Probe: "What makes you say \[number\]?" / "What would make it higher?"  
* "Who taught you about money — or where do you pick stuff up from?"   
  * Probe: school, parents  
    * What did you learn? Can you remember?  
  * Probe: "Do you ever look stuff up yourself? Where do you look?"  
* "Is there anything about money you wish you understood better?"

```
Modorator shares Figjam board. Emoji check in is optional if participant is struggling to artiulate their perspective.
```

### **Part C: Emoji Check (1 min)**

"Quick one — if you had to pick one emoji that describes how you feel about money, which one?"

Show options: 😎 💪 🤔 😬 🫤 😴 😰 🤷

"Why that one?"

If they say something positive (😎 💪): "Is that more like — genuinely good, or more just... fine, nothing to worry about?" / "What makes it feel good?"

If they say neutral or negative: "Is that more like you just don't think about it much, or does it stress you out?"

## **3. WHAT MATTERS TO YOU: CARD SORT (5 min)**

### **Aim**

* Surface which money-related needs matter most to them — in their own priorities
* Ground the session in jobs to be done before showing the app
* Identify gaps between what the app addresses and what they actually care about

### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"I'm going to show you some quotes. These are all things young people have said about money. I want you to sort them into three boxes."

```
Moderator shares figboard with JTBD exercise, giving 'Remote Control' access to participant. As participant to wiggle mouse to check it's working.
```

Show the three boxes:
- **DON'T CARE** — "This isn't really me"
- **KINDA IMPORTANT** — "Yeah, I think about this sometimes"
- **VERY IMPORTANT** — "This really matters to me"

**Step 1 — Sort (2 min)**

Let participant read and sort statements one at a time. 

**I-statements:**

1. "I want to feel like I actually know what I'm doing with money"
2. "I want to make my money last longer between getting it"
3. "I don't want to get caught out by scams or tricks"
4. "I want to save up for something without giving up halfway"
5. "I want to understand the stuff about money that nobody explains"
6. "I want to feel confident, not embarrassed, talking about money"
7. "I want to spot when something's a rip-off"
8. "I want to know what to do when money stuff gets real"

**Step 2 — Rank (1 min)**

"Now look at the ones in VERY IMPORTANT column. Can you put those in order — most important at the top? I can move them to save us a bit of time.

Discussion (1 min)

* "Talk me through your top one or two — why those?"
* "How do you currently try and achieve these things?"
	*   If participant says they don't, probe as to why.

## **4. PROPOSITION REACTIONS (8 min)**

### **Aim**

* Test whether the proposition framing lands instantly  
* Understand which direction resonates and why  
* Surface language and emotional reactions before showing the app itself

### **Blink Test (3 min)**

Setup

"Now I'm going to show you something we've been working on. But I'm only showing it for about 5 seconds. I'll ask you what stood out, and then we'll look at it again."

```
Ensure remote control is turned off. Change Figjam boards to share propositions. Ensure the order is randomized and not in the order you did the last IDI.
```

1. Show proposition card 1 for \~5 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   * "What did you spot?"
   * "What stood out to you?"
   * "Who do you think it's for?"
   * "Would you download it? Quick answer, yes or no."
   * "Why?"
4. Repeat for cards 2 and 3

```
If participant struggles, go through each with an emoji check in
```
### **First Reactions — Emoji Board (1 min)**

Show all three proposition cards together and ask:

"Pick emojis that match your reaction to each one"
Offer options:

* 🔥 \= fire, this is good
* 🤔 \= not sure yet
* 😬 \= bit cringe
* 👶 \= feels too young for me
* 🧠 \= feels smart
* 😴 \= looks boring
* 🎮 \= feels like a game
* 📚 \= feels like school

"Why those ones?"

### **Deeper Read — chosen proposition (2 min)**

Ask participant to pick the one that appeals most:

"If you could only click one of these to find out more about what it does, which one would it be?"

Then show the full card for their chosen proposition and probe accordingly:

**If Proposition 1: Real Money Skills for Life**

> **Real Money Skills for Life**
> Simple money skills you can use straight away, for stuff you actually do.
> *What you'll get better at:* Making your money last longer · Saving for what you actually want · Spotting the scams before they catch you

Probes:
- "Which of those three things matters most to you right now — and is there something about money it's missing?"
- "When you hear 'stuff you actually do' — what comes to mind? What money stuff are you actually dealing with?"
- "Does this sound like something you'd try, or does it sound a bit like a school lesson dressed up?"

---

**If Proposition 2: Train Your Money Brain**

> **Train Your Money Brain**
> You're already making money decisions. Make better ones, starting now.
> *What you'll get better at:* Spot good vs bad money choices · Think ahead before you spend · Learn from mistakes safely

Probes:
- "What kind of money decisions are you already making — even small ones?"
- "'Train your money brain' — does that make you want to try it, or does it feel a bit cringe?"
- "If you could practise money stuff without any real consequences, would that actually be useful — or would it feel pointless because it's not real?"

---

**If Proposition 3: Money Stuff Nobody Explains**

> **Money Stuff Nobody Explains**
> The stuff about money you're supposed to just know, but nobody actually tells you.
> *What you'll learn:* How to make your money go further · What's actually worth saving for (and how) · The stuff that catches people out, before it catches you

Probes:
- "Is there something about money you've had to figure out on your own that you wish someone had just told you?"
- "'Money stuff nobody explains' — does that feel true to you, or do you think there's loads of info out there already?"
- "If you saw this, would you want to check it out by yourself — or would you only do it if a friend was doing it too?"

## **5. APP WALKTHROUGH (20 min)**

### **Before You Start — Personal View Framing**

*Say this before sharing the Figma link.*

"One thing I want to flag before we look at this. What's really useful for us is your personal reaction — what *you* think, what *you* feel. Not what you think other people might like, or who else might use it. Even if your gut reaction is 'this isn't for me' — that's exactly what we want to know.

So try to resist saying things like 'I think people would like this' — tell me if *you* like it."

### **Aim**

* Observe real behavior — where they get stuck, what they understand  
* Response to the learning format  
  * Navigation clarity and intuitive understanding  
  * Content comprehension and age-appropriateness  
  * Engagement level throughout  
  * Pain points and confusion  
  * Gamification effectiveness  
  * Topic relevance and gaps

| Step | Time | Purpose |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Figma setup & priming | 4 min | Get them ready to think aloud |
| First impressions | 2 min | Capture instinctive reaction |
| Onboarding quiz | 2 min | Observe how questions land |
| Onboarding debrief | 3 min | Emotional capture while fresh |
| Lesson completion | 4 min | Does the learning format work? |
| Lesson debrief | 2 min | Gamification and motivation |
| Learning topics | 3 min | Which topics resonate? |

"I won't be telling you what buttons to press; you'll have to work that out for yourself."

## **6. REFLECTION & CLOSE (9 min)**

### **Sentence Completion (2 min)**

```
ONLY to Use if participant is struggling to articulate their final view
```

"Complete these sentences for me, I'll start it and you have to finish with your own words"

* "Using this app would make me feel..."  
* "I would use this if..."  
* "The best thing about it is..."  
* "The worst thing about it is..."  
* "I would tell my friends it's..."

### **Final Verdict (3 min)**

"Give me your final verdict. Would you play with this if it wasn't me sharing it?"
"If you could change ONE thing to make this actually good, what would it be?"
"One piece of advice for the people making this — what should they know?"
### **Standalone vs. Embedded (2 min)**

"Imagine this app exists and works well. Do you think you'd download it as its own separate app — like something you'd go to just to learn about money? Or would it make more sense to you if it was built into something you already use, like a banking app or a school app?"

Probes:
- "Why do you think that?"
- "Would having it inside [banking app / school portal] make you more likely to use it — or would you ignore it there?"
- "Is there a particular app or place it would feel more natural in?"

### **Monetisation — Light Touch (2 min)**

"The app would always be free to use — the main stuff would stay free forever. But imagine they added some extra stuff you could pay for — like bonus levels on things like investing, or you could buy coins to unlock things in the app. How would you feel about that?"

**Probes:**
- "Is there anything in an app like this you'd actually consider paying for?"
- "What kind of extra stuff would have to be really good for you to spend money on it?"
  
**If participant struggles:**
- Extension on certain lessons - further explanations etc.
- Extra levels
- Access to online support regarding specific questions?
- We might implement lives they would only regenerate 1x a day so if they would pay to have those replenished

### **Wrap-Up (2 min)**

"Anything else you want to say that we haven't talked about?"  
Thank them genuinely. Confirm incentive.

## **Moderation Tips for Young Teens**

### **Do**

* Use their language — mirror back the words they use  
* Keep energy up — vary your tone, keep things moving  
* Praise the honesty — "That's really useful" when they're critical  
* Give choices — "Want to keep going or try something else?"  
* Use visuals — show, point, rank rather than just talk  
* Normalise not knowing — "A lot of people find this confusing"

### **Don't**

* Don't use "so..." leading phrases — they'll agree with whatever follows  
* Don't explain or defend the app — if they're confused, note it, don't fix it  
* Don't fill silences too fast — but don't let them last more than 5-7 seconds  
* Don't be too formal — this isn't an exam  
* Don't say "great job" — stay neutral, not encouraging/discouraging

### **Recovery Moves**

| Situation        | What to Do                                                                          |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| They shut down   | Switch to something visual: "Let's try something different — look at these screens" |
| One-word answers | Offer options: "Is that like, boring-boring, or just not-for-me?"                   |
| They're confused | Normalise: "That's the point — we need to know what's unclear"                      |
| They're bored    | Speed up: "Quick fire round — give me one word for each of these"                   |
