# **Older Teens Session Guide (Ages 15-18)**

A specialised session design for the older teen segment, prioritising genuine engagement, credibility, and age-appropriate techniques.

---
## **Moderator Intro Script**

*Say this at the start of the session, before anything else.*

"Hi — thanks for joining. My name is [name]. What we're going to do today is pretty simple: we're going to have a conversation about how you think about money, and then I'll show you something that some people have been working on and get your honest thoughts on it.

Just so you know — I don't work for the company that made what I'll share with you. My job is to speak to people like you and understand what you actually think. There are no right or wrong answers. Genuinely, the more honest you are — even if it's 'this isn't for me' or 'this is pointless' — the more useful it is.

Sound good? Want to kick off with how old you are and what you're into at the moment?"

---

### **Moderator Reminders**

1. Treat them as capable adults — avoid anything that feels patronising
2. Frequent variety — switch activities every 5-7 minutes maximum
3. Credibility matters — they'll disengage if the session feels shallow or juvenile
4. Peer context — what their friends think and do is still highly relevant
5. Acknowledge complexity — they're aware money is complicated, don't oversimplify

---

## **Session Flow (60 min)**

| Section                        | Time   | What We're Finding Out                                                                                                                                               |
| ------------------------------ | ------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Intro & framing                | 2 min  | Who the moderator is, what we're doing, setting expectations.                                                                                                        |
| Warm-up                        | 5 min  | Building rapport through a money-related creative exercise. Gets them engaged and thinking about money before we go deeper.                                          |
| Relationship with money        | 8 min  | Understanding their baseline: how money fits into their life, where they're confident vs. uncertain, and current sources of guidance (parents, Google, TikTok, etc.) |
| What matters to you            | 5 min  | Jobs to be done card sort — what money needs matter most to them, ranked in their own words.                                                                         |
| Proposition reactions          | 8 min  | Do the propositions land instantly? Do they see themselves as the target? Which framing resonates and why?                                                           |
| Money Monsters app walkthrough | 20 min | Observing real behaviour — where they get stuck, what they understand, learning format, navigation, gamification.                                                    |
| Reflection & close             | 9 min  | Honest verdict, standalone vs. embedded app question, monetisation, and wrap-up.                                                                                     |
| Buffer                         | 3 min  | For overruns or technical issues                                                                                                                                     |

## **1. WARM-UP: DESIGN YOUR DREAM MONEY APP (5 min)**
–

### **Aim**

* Break "interview" formality / reduce nerves
* Get them thinking about money in a creative, low-pressure way
* Build rapport and trust
### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"Before we get into anything, I want you to design something for me. Grab a pen and paper."

Dream Money App (2 min)

"Imagine you could design a money app built specifically for you and where you're at right now. Not for kids, not for your parents — something that actually fits your life. Sketch out what it would look like. Stick figures are totally fine. You've got 2 minutes."

While they draw, prompt gently:

* "What's the main thing it does?"
* "What would you open first?"
* "Is it more about learning, doing, or tracking?"

Show and Tell (2 min)

"Walk me through it."

Probes:

* "What makes this different from what already exists?"
* "What would make you actually open it regularly?"

## **2. RELATIONSHIP WITH MONEY (8 min)**

### **Aim**

Understand their baseline:

* How money fits into their life
* Current engagement level with money
* Confidence vs. knowledge gap (do they know what they don't know?)
* Emotional baseline
* Sources of guidance (parents, Google, TikTok, nowhere)
* Appetite for learning more

### **Part A: Money in Their Life (4 min)**

"I want to get a sense of where you're at with money. Just tell me what comes to mind."

* **"Do you have your own money coming in?"**
  * If yes: "Where from?" / "How do you manage it?"
  * If no: "How do you get money when you need it?"
* **"What was the last significant thing you spent money on?"**
  * Probe: "Was that planned, or more of a spontaneous thing?"
* **"Have you ever saved up for something specific?"**
  * If yes: "What was it? How did you approach it?"
  * If no: "Is there anything you'd want to save for?"
* Where do you keep your money?
  * "Cash, card, or app — what do you use most?"**
* **"How do your parents talk to you about money?"**

### **Part B: Confidence and Learning (3 min)**

"A couple more questions..."

* Compared to your peers, score yourself "1-10, how good would you say you are with money? 10 being excellent, 1 being really struggling."
  * Probe: "What makes you say \[number\]?" / "What would move it higher?"
* "Where have you picked up what you know about money — who or what has shaped how you think about it?"
  * Probe: school, parents
    * What stuck? What didn't?
  * Probe: "Do you look stuff up yourself? Where do you go?"
* "Is there anything about money you feel like you should probably know better by now?"

```
Moderator shares Figjam board. Emoji check in is optional if participant is struggling to articulate their perspective.
```

### **Part C: Emoji Check (1 min)**

"Quick one — if you had to pick one emoji that describes how you feel about money right now, which one?"

Show options: 😎 💪 🤔 😬 🫤 😴 😰 🤷

"Why that one?"

If they say something positive (😎 💪): "Is that genuinely confident, or more like — things are fine, nothing to worry about yet?" / "What makes it feel good?"

If they say neutral or negative: "Is that more like you don't think about it much, or does it actually stress you out?"

## **3. WHAT MATTERS TO YOU: CARD SORT (5 min)**

### **Aim**

* Surface which money-related needs matter most to them — in their own priorities
* Ground the session in jobs to be done before showing the app
* Identify gaps between what the app addresses and what they actually care about

### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"I'm going to show you some statements. These are all things young people have said about money. I want you to sort them into three boxes."

```
Moderator shares figboard with JTBD exercise, giving 'Remote Control' access to participant. Ask participant to wiggle mouse to check it's working.
```

Show the three boxes:
- **DON'T CARE** — "This isn't really relevant to me"
- **KINDA IMPORTANT** — "Yeah, I think about this sometimes"
- **VERY IMPORTANT** — "This genuinely matters to me"

**Step 1 — Sort (2 min)**

Let participant read and sort statements one at a time.

**I-statements:**

1. "I want to feel like I actually know what I'm doing with money"
2. "I want to make my money last longer between getting it"
3. "I don't want to get caught out by scams or bad deals"
4. "I want to save up for something without losing momentum"
5. "I want to understand the stuff about money that nobody explains"
6. "I want to feel confident, not awkward, talking about money"
7. "I want to spot when something's a rip-off"
8. "I want to know what to do when money stuff gets serious"

**Step 2 — Rank (1 min)**

"Now look at the ones in your VERY IMPORTANT column. Can you order those — most important at the top? I can move them if that's easier."

Discussion (1 min)

* "Talk me through your top one or two — why those?"
* "How do you currently try and address these?"
	*   If participant says they don't, probe as to why.

## **4. PROPOSITION REACTIONS (8 min)**

### **Aim**

* Test whether the proposition framing lands instantly
* Understand which direction resonates and why
* Surface language and emotional reactions before showing the app itself

### **Blink Test (3 min)**

Setup

"Now I'm going to show you something we've been working on. I'll only show it for about 5 seconds. I'll ask what stood out, and then we'll look again."

```
Ensure remote control is turned off. Change Figjam boards to share propositions. Ensure the order is randomized and not in the order you did the last IDI.
```

1. Show proposition card 1 for \~5 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   * "What did you notice?"
   * "What stood out?"
   * "Who do you think it's aimed at?"
   * "Would you download it? Yes or no."
   * "Why?"
4. Repeat for cards 2 and 3

```
If participant struggles, go through each with an emoji check in
```
### **First Reactions — Emoji Board (1 min)**

Show all three proposition cards together and ask:

"Pick emojis that match your reaction to each one"
Offer options:

* 🔥 \= fire, this is good
* 🤔 \= not sure yet
* 😬 \= bit cringe
* 👶 \= feels too young for me
* 🧠 \= feels smart
* 😴 \= looks boring
* 🎮 \= feels like a game
* 📚 \= feels like school

"Why those ones?"

### **Deeper Read — chosen proposition (2 min)**

Ask participant to pick the one that appeals most:

"If you could only click one of these to find out more, which would it be?"

Then show the full card for their chosen proposition and probe accordingly:

**If Proposition 1: Real Money Skills for Life**

> **Real Money Skills for Life**
> Simple money skills you can use straight away, for stuff you actually do.
> *What you'll get better at:* Making your money last longer · Saving for what you actually want · Spotting the scams before they catch you

Probes:
- "Which of those three things is most relevant to you right now — and is there something it's missing?"
- "When you hear 'stuff you actually do' — what money situations are you actually dealing with at the moment?"
- "Does this feel genuinely useful, or does it still sound a bit like a school lesson?"

---

**If Proposition 2: Train Your Money Brain**

> **Train Your Money Brain**
> You're already making money decisions. Make better ones, starting now.
> *What you'll get better at:* Spot good vs bad money choices · Think ahead before you spend · Learn from mistakes safely

Probes:
- "What kind of money decisions are you already making — day to day or bigger ones?"
- "'Train your money brain' — does that feel motivating, or does it come across as a bit try-hard?"
- "If you could test out money decisions without real consequences, would that actually be useful to you?"

---

**If Proposition 3: Money Stuff Nobody Explains**

> **Money Stuff Nobody Explains**
> The stuff about money you're supposed to just know, but nobody actually tells you.
> *What you'll learn:* How to make your money go further · What's actually worth saving for (and how) · The stuff that catches people out, before it catches you

Probes:
- "Is there something about money you've had to figure out yourself — that you wish someone had just explained?"
- "'Money stuff nobody explains' — does that resonate, or do you think there's actually loads of information out there if you look?"
- "Would you use something like this on your own, or only if someone recommended it to you?"

## **5. APP WALKTHROUGH (20 min)**

### **Before You Start — Personal View Framing**

*Say this before sharing the Figma link.*

"One thing I want to flag before we look at this. What's really useful for us is your personal reaction — what *you* think, what *you* feel. Not what you think other people might like, or who else might use it. Even if your gut reaction is 'this isn't for me' — that's exactly what we want to know.

So try to resist saying things like 'I think people would like this' — tell me if *you* like it."

### **Aim**

* Observe real behavior — where they get stuck, what they understand
* Response to the learning format
  * Navigation clarity and intuitive understanding
  * Content comprehension and age-appropriateness
  * Engagement level throughout
  * Pain points and confusion
  * Gamification effectiveness
  * Topic relevance and gaps

| Onboarding debrief | 3 min | Emotional capture while fresh |
| Lesson completion | 4 min | Does the learning format work? |

"I won't be telling you what buttons to press; you'll have to work that out for yourself."

## **6. REFLECTION & CLOSE (9 min)**

### **Sentence Completion (2 min)**

```
ONLY to Use if participant is struggling to articulate their final view
```

"Complete these sentences for me, I'll start and you finish with your own words"

* "Using this app would make me feel..."
* "I would use this if..."
* "The best thing about it is..."
* "The thing that needs work is..."
* "I would describe this to a friend as..."

### **Final Verdict (3 min)**

"Give me your honest verdict. Would you actually use this if it wasn't me showing it to you?"
"If you could change one thing to make this genuinely good, what would it be?"
"One piece of advice for the team building this — what do they need to hear?"
### **Standalone vs. Embedded (2 min)**

"Imagine this app exists and works well. Would you download it as a standalone app — something you'd go to specifically to learn about money? Or would it make more sense built into something you already use, like a banking app or a school platform?"

Probes:
- "Why do you think that?"
- "Would having it inside [banking app / school portal] make you more likely to use it — or would you just ignore it there?"
- "Is there a particular app or platform it would sit more naturally in?"

### **Monetisation — Light Touch (2 min)**

"The app would always be free — the core stuff stays free. But imagine they added premium content you could pay for — like deeper dives on things like investing, or you could buy coins to unlock things in the app. How would you feel about that?"

**Probes:**
- "Is there anything in an app like this you'd genuinely consider paying for?"
- "What kind of extra content would have to be really good for you to spend money on it?"

**If participant struggles:**
- Extension on certain lessons - further explanations etc.
- Extra levels
- Access to support for specific questions?
- Lives that regenerate once a day — would they pay to replenish them?

### **Wrap-Up (2 min)**

"Anything else you want to add that we haven't covered?"
Thank them genuinely. Confirm incentive.

## **Moderation Tips for Older Teens**

### **Do**

* Treat them as intelligent — they'll disengage fast if they feel talked down to
* Keep energy up — vary your tone, keep things moving
* Acknowledge good insight — "That's a really useful point" when they're specific
* Give them agency — "Want to explore that more or move on?"
* Use visuals — show, point, rank rather than just talk
* Normalise uncertainty — "Loads of people aren't sure about this stuff"

### **Don't**

* Don't use "so..." leading phrases — they'll agree with whatever follows
* Don't explain or defend the app — if they're confused, note it, don't fix it
* Don't fill silences too fast — but don't let them last more than 5-7 seconds
* Don't be too formal or too casual — find the peer register
* Don't say "great job" — stay neutral, not encouraging/discouraging

### **Recovery Moves**

| Situation        | What to Do                                                                          |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| They shut down   | Switch to something visual: "Let's try something different — look at these screens" |
| One-word answers | Offer options: "Is that more like — genuinely not for you, or just not yet?"        |
| They're confused | Normalise: "That's exactly the point — we need to know what's unclear"              |
| They're bored    | Speed up: "Quick fire — one word for each of these"                                 |
