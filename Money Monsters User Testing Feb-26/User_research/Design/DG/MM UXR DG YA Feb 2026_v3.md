# **Young Adults Session Guide (Ages 18-25)**

A specialised session design for the young adult segment, prioritising peer credibility, real-world relevance, and honest engagement.

---
## **Moderator Intro Script**

*Say this at the start of the session, before anything else.*

"Hi — thanks for joining. My name is [name]. What we're going to do today is pretty straightforward: we're going to have a conversation about how you relate to money, and then I'll show you something that's been in development and get your candid thoughts on it.

Just to be clear — I have no stake in what you think of it. My role is to speak to people like you and capture your honest perspective. There are no right or wrong answers. The more direct you are — even if your view is 'this isn't for me' or 'this misses the point' — the more valuable it is.

Shall we start with a bit of background on you? How old are you and what's your situation at the moment — studying, working, both?"

---

### **Moderator Reminders**

1. Peer register — speak to them as you would a thoughtful colleague, not a student
2. Real-world grounding — their financial reality (rent, income, debt, independence) is far more complex than younger segments
3. Scepticism is useful — don't shy away from it, probe it
4. Avoid anything that feels like financial advice — stay in research mode
5. They may be self-conscious about gaps in knowledge — normalise this actively

---

## **Session Flow (60 min)**

| Section                        | Time   | What We're Finding Out                                                                                                                                               |
| ------------------------------ | ------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Intro & framing                | 2 min  | Who the moderator is, what we're doing, setting expectations.                                                                                                        |
| Warm-up                        | 5 min  | Building rapport through a money-related reflective exercise. Gets them thinking about money before we go deeper.                                                    |
| Relationship with money        | 8 min  | Understanding their baseline: how money fits into their life, where they're confident vs. uncertain, and current sources of guidance.                                |
| What matters to you            | 5 min  | Jobs to be done card sort — what money needs matter most to them, ranked in their own words.                                                                         |
| Proposition reactions          | 8 min  | Do the propositions land instantly? Do they see themselves as the target? Which framing resonates and why?                                                           |
| Money Monsters app walkthrough | 20 min | Observing real behaviour — where they get stuck, what they understand, learning format, navigation, gamification.                                                    |
| Reflection & close             | 9 min  | Honest verdict, standalone vs. embedded app question, monetisation, and wrap-up.                                                                                     |
| Buffer                         | 3 min  | For overruns or technical issues                                                                                                                                     |

## **1. WARM-UP: DESIGN YOUR DREAM MONEY APP (5 min)**
–

### **Aim**

* Break "interview" formality / put them at ease
* Get them thinking about money in a creative, low-stakes way
* Build rapport and trust
### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"Before we get into things properly, I want you to do a quick creative exercise. Grab a pen and paper."

Dream Money App (2 min)

"Imagine you could design a money app built entirely around your life right now — not for teenagers, not for someone with a pension — for where you actually are. Sketch out what it would look like. Rough is fine. You've got 2 minutes."

While they draw, prompt gently:

* "What's the primary thing it does?"
* "What would you open first?"


Show and Tell (2 min)

"Walk me through it."

Probes:

* "Is it more about helping you understand money, manage it, or grow it?"
* "What's missing from what already exists that made you design it this way?"
* "What would make this something you'd actually use consistently?"

## **2. RELATIONSHIP WITH MONEY (8 min)**

### **Aim**

Understand their baseline:

* How money fits into their life right now
* Current engagement level with money
* Confidence vs. knowledge gap (do they know what they don't know?)
* Emotional baseline
* Sources of guidance (family, social media, trial and error)
* Appetite for learning more

### **Part A: Money in Their Life (4 min)**

"I want to understand where you're at with money at the moment. Just tell me what comes to mind."

* **"What does your financial situation look like right now?"**
  * "Are you working, studying, both? Do you have your own income?"
  * "What are your main outgoings — rent, bills, that kind of thing?"
* **"What was the last significant financial decision you made?"**
  * Probe: "How did you approach it? Did you research it, or go with your gut?"
* **"Are you actively saving at the moment?"**
  * If yes: "What for? How do you manage that?"
  * If no: "Is that something you're thinking about, or not a priority right now?"
* How do you manage your money day-to-day?
  * "Bank app, spreadsheet, just winging it — what does that look like?"**
* **"Who or what do you turn to when you have a money question?"**

### **Part B: Confidence and Learning (3 min)**

"A couple more questions..."

* "Honestly — how financially literate would you say you are? If you had to rate yourself 1-10, where do you land?"
  * Probe: "What's behind that number?" / "What would move it higher?"
* "Where did you actually learn what you know about money — school, family, experience, figuring it out the hard way?"
  * Probe: "What stuck? What do you wish you'd been taught earlier?"
  * Probe: "Do you actively seek out financial information? Where do you go?"
* "Is there an area of personal finance you feel like you should have a better handle on by now?"

```
Moderator shares Figjam board. Emoji check in is optional if participant is struggling to articulate their perspective.
```

### **Part C: Emoji Check (1 min)**

"Quick one — if you had to pick one emoji that captures how you feel about your relationship with money right now, which one?"

Show options: 😎 💪 🤔 😬 🫤 😴 😰 🤷

"Why that one?"

If they say something positive (😎 💪): "Is that genuinely comfortable, or more like — managing okay, nothing's gone wrong yet?" / "What's making it feel positive?"

If they say neutral or negative: "Is that more like background stress, or something more specific?"

## **3. WHAT MATTERS TO YOU: CARD SORT (5 min)**

### **Aim**

* Surface which money-related needs matter most to them — in their own priorities
* Ground the session in jobs to be done before showing the app
* Identify gaps between what the app addresses and what they actually care about

### **The Exercise**

Setup (1 min)

"I'm going to show you some statements. These are things that have come up when talking to people in your age group about money. I want you to sort them into three boxes based on how relevant they feel to you."

```
Moderator shares figboard with JTBD exercise, giving 'Remote Control' access to participant. Ask participant to wiggle mouse to check it's working.
```

Show the three boxes:
- **DON'T CARE** — "Not really relevant to me"
- **KINDA IMPORTANT** — "Yeah, I think about this sometimes"
- **VERY IMPORTANT** — "This genuinely matters to me right now"

**Step 1 — Sort (2 min)**

Let participant read and sort statements one at a time.

**I-statements:**

1. "I want to feel genuinely on top of my finances, not just getting by"
2. "I want to make my money go further each month"
3. "I don't want to get caught out by scams, hidden fees, or bad deals"
4. "I want to build savings consistently without constantly falling off track"
5. "I want to understand the financial concepts no one properly explained to me"
6. "I want to feel confident making financial decisions, not second-guessing myself"
7. "I want to spot when something's a rip-off or not worth it"
8. "I want to know what to do when things get financially complicated"

**Step 2 — Rank (1 min)**

"Now look at what's in your VERY IMPORTANT column. Can you order those — most pressing at the top? I can move them if that's easier."

Discussion (1 min)

* "Talk me through your top one or two — why those?"
* "What do you currently do to address these?"
	*   If participant says nothing, probe as to why.

## **4. PROPOSITION REACTIONS (8 min)**

### **Aim**

* Test whether the proposition framing lands instantly
* Understand which direction resonates and why
* Surface language and emotional reactions before showing the app itself

### **Blink Test (3 min)**

Setup

"Now I'm going to show you something we've been working on. I'll show it for about 5 seconds, ask what stood out, then we'll look again properly."

```
Ensure remote control is turned off. Change Figjam boards to share propositions. Ensure the order is randomized and not in the order you did the last IDI.
```

1. Show proposition card 1 for \~5 seconds
2. Hide it
3. Ask immediately:
   * "What did you pick up from that?"
   * "What stood out?"
   * "Who do you think it's aimed at?"
   * "Would you download it? Quick instinct — yes or no."
   * "Why?"
4. Repeat for cards 2 and 3

```
If participant struggles, go through each with an emoji check in
```
### **First Reactions — Emoji Board (1 min)**

Show all three proposition cards together and ask:

"Pick emojis that match your reaction to each one"
Offer options:

* 🔥 \= fire, this is good
* 🤔 \= not sure yet
* 😬 \= bit cringe
* 👶 \= feels too young for me
* 🧠 \= feels smart
* 😴 \= looks boring
* 🎮 \= feels like a game
* 📚 \= feels like school

"Why those ones?"

### **Deeper Read — chosen proposition (2 min)**

Ask participant to pick the one that appeals most:

"If you could only click one of these to find out more, which would it be — and why?"

Then show the full card for their chosen proposition and probe accordingly:

**If Proposition 1: Real Money Skills for Life**

> **Real Money Skills for Life**
> Simple money skills you can use straight away, for stuff you actually do.
> *What you'll get better at:* Making your money last longer · Saving for what you actually want · Spotting the scams before they catch you

Probes:
- "Which of those three things is most relevant to where you're at right now — and is there anything important it's missing?"
- "When you hear 'stuff you actually do' — what financial situations does that bring to mind for you?"
- "Does this feel practical and useful, or does it still come across as a bit simplistic?"

---

**If Proposition 2: Train Your Money Brain**

> **Train Your Money Brain**
> You're already making money decisions. Make better ones, starting now.
> *What you'll get better at:* Spot good vs bad money choices · Think ahead before you spend · Learn from mistakes safely

Probes:
- "What kinds of money decisions do you find yourself making regularly — and which ones do you find hardest?"
- "'Train your money brain' — does that feel empowering to you, or does the framing feel a bit off?"
- "The idea of learning from mistakes in a safe environment — is that something you'd actually value, or does it feel too abstract?"

---

**If Proposition 3: Money Stuff Nobody Explains**

> **Money Stuff Nobody Explains**
> The stuff about money you're supposed to just know, but nobody actually tells you.
> *What you'll learn:* How to make your money go further · What's actually worth saving for (and how) · The stuff that catches people out, before it catches you

Probes:
- "Is there specific financial stuff you've had to figure out on your own — things you wished someone had just laid out clearly?"
- "'Money stuff nobody explains' — does that feel true to your experience, or do you think the information is out there if you look for it?"
- "Would you use something like this off your own back, or would you need a recommendation to take it seriously?"

## **5. APP WALKTHROUGH (20 min)**

### **Before You Start — Personal View Framing**

*Say this before sharing the Figma link.*

"One thing I want to flag before we look at this. What's really useful for us is your personal reaction — what *you* think, what *you* feel. Not what you think other people might like, or who else might use it. Even if your gut reaction is 'this isn't for me' — that's exactly what we want to know.

So try to resist saying things like 'I think people would like this' — tell me if *you* like it."

### **Aim**

* Observe real behavior — where they get stuck, what they understand
* Response to the learning format
  * Navigation clarity and intuitive understanding
  * Content comprehension and appropriateness for this age group
  * Engagement level throughout
  * Pain points and confusion
  * Gamification effectiveness — does it feel patronising or genuinely engaging?
  * Topic relevance and gaps

## **6. REFLECTION & CLOSE (9 min)**

### **Sentence Completion (2 min)**

```
ONLY to Use if participant is struggling to articulate their final view
```

"Complete these sentences for me — I'll start and you finish in your own words"

* "Using this app would make me feel..."
* "I would use this if..."
* "The strongest thing about it is..."
* "The thing that would put me off is..."
* "I would describe this to a friend as..."

### **Final Verdict (3 min)**

"Honest verdict — would you actually download and use this if it wasn't me showing it to you?"
"If you could change one thing to make this something you'd genuinely use, what would it be?"
"One piece of honest advice for the team building this — what do they need to know?"
### **Standalone vs. Embedded (2 min)**

"Imagine this is a fully working product. Would you download it as a standalone app — something you'd go to specifically to build financial knowledge? Or would it make more sense as something embedded in a platform you already use, like a banking app or financial tool?"

Probes:
- "What's behind that?"
- "Would having it inside [banking app / another platform] make you more likely to engage with it — or would it get ignored?"
- "Is there a specific app or context where this would feel most natural?"

### **Monetisation — Light Touch (2 min)**

"The core app would always be free. But imagine they introduced a premium tier — things like more advanced content on investing or tax, or the ability to unlock additional features. How would you respond to that?"

**Probes:**
- "Is there content in an app like this you'd genuinely consider paying for?"
- "What would have to be in the paid tier to make it worth it?"

**If participant struggles:**
- Deeper content on specific topics — investing, tax, pensions
- Extra levels or advanced modules
- Access to Q&A or personalised support
- Lives that regenerate once a day — would they pay to replenish them?

### **Wrap-Up (2 min)**

"Anything else you want to add that we haven't covered?"
Thank them genuinely. Confirm incentive.

## **Moderation Tips for Young Adults**

### **Do**

* Treat them as equals — they respond to being taken seriously
* Acknowledge the complexity of their financial reality — don't oversimplify
* Welcome critical perspectives — "That's a useful challenge" when they push back
* Give them room to lead — if they go off-script in a useful direction, follow it
* Use concrete examples — their financial decisions are real, not hypothetical
* Normalise knowledge gaps — "This is stuff most people don't know"

### **Don't**

* Don't use "so..." leading phrases — they'll agree with whatever follows
* Don't explain or defend the app — if they're confused or critical, note it, don't justify it
* Don't fill silences too fast — but don't let them last more than 5-7 seconds
* Don't be overly formal — but don't try too hard to be casual either
* Don't say "great answer" — stay neutral throughout

### **Recovery Moves**

| Situation          | What to Do                                                                              |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| They shut down     | Switch to something concrete: "Let me show you a specific screen — what do you think?" |
| Surface-level answers | Probe directly: "Say more about that — what's behind it?"                           |
| They're confused   | Normalise: "That's exactly what we're trying to understand — where it's not clear"     |
| They're dismissive | Probe the dismissal: "That's a fair reaction — what would have to be different?"        |
